# An Academic Critique of Internal Contradictions in the Quran with Reference to Eschatological Descriptions of Hell’s Sustenance

**Source:** [Max Shimba Ministries](https://www.maxshimbaministries.org/2025/07/an-academic-critique-of-internal.html)

**Category:** 21 Max Shimba Ministries

---

<div class="post-body entry-content" id="post-body-8374859261994473942" itemprop="description articleBody">
<p> <strong>Title: An Academic Critique of Internal Contradictions in the Quran with Reference to Eschatological Descriptions of Hell’s Sustenance</strong></p><p>
<strong>Author: Dr. Maxwell Shimba, Shimba Theological Institute</strong></p>
<hr/>
<h3>Abstract</h3>
<p>Muslim apologists frequently assert that the Quran is of divine origin based on its internal consistency. This claim is derived from verses such as Surah 4:82, which challenges readers to identify discrepancies as a test of divine authorship. However, a critical literary and theological analysis of the Quran’s eschatological descriptions—particularly regarding the food of Hell—raises important questions concerning internal coherence. This article investigates the multiplicity of descriptions concerning Hell's sustenance in the Quran and assesses whether such variances constitute theological contradictions or can be explained as thematic diversity. The findings contribute to the broader discourse on the Quran’s divine claim of inerrancy and consistency.</p>
<hr/>
<h3>1. Introduction</h3>
<p>The Quran explicitly states in Surah 4:82:</p>
<blockquote>
<p><em>“Do they not reflect upon the Qur'an? If it had been from other than Allah, they would have found within it much contradiction.”</em> (Quran 4:82, Sahih International)</p>
</blockquote>
<p>This verse has been pivotal in Islamic apologetics, serving as a self-verifying test of divine origin. Muslim scholars have historically contended that the absence of contradiction in the Quran is evidence of its perfection and divine authorship (Al-Tabari, <em>Tafsir al-Tabari</em>; Al-Qurtubi, <em>Al-Jami’ li Ahkam al-Qur’an</em>).</p>
<p>Nonetheless, when examining the Quran's descriptions of Hell and its inhabitants' conditions, apparent inconsistencies arise—particularly regarding the sustenance of the damned. This article focuses on these inconsistencies to determine whether they breach the Quran’s self-imposed test of inerrancy.</p>
<hr/>
<h3>2. Descriptions of Food in Hell: A Textual Analysis</h3>
<p>Three primary passages describe what the inhabitants of Hell will consume:</p>
<h4>2.1 Bitter Thorny Plant – <em>Dhaari’</em></h4>
<ul>
<li>
<p><strong>Surah 88:6</strong>: <em>“No food will there be for them but a bitter dari’.”</em><br/>
The term “dari’” refers to a thorny desert plant, commonly identified with <em>Al-Shubrum</em>, which is both inedible and harmful (Ibn Kathir, <em>Tafsir al-Qur'an al-Azim</em>).</p>
</li>
</ul>
<h4>2.2 Pus from Wounds – <em>Ghislin</em></h4>
<ul>
<li>
<p><strong>Surah 69:36</strong>: <em>“Nor hath he any food except the corruption from the washing of wounds.”</em><br/>
The word <em>ghislin</em> is interpreted by many exegetes (e.g., Al-Jalalayn) as a fluid composed of bodily excretions or pus, indicating extreme degradation.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<h4>2.3 Fruit of the Tree of Zaqqum</h4>
<ul>
<li>
<p><strong>Surah 37:64–66</strong>: <em>“For it is a tree that springs out of the bottom of Hellfire. The shoots of its fruit-stalks are like the heads of devils. Truly they will eat thereof and fill their bellies therewith.”</em><br/>
The tree of Zaqqum, often interpreted as a symbol of spiritual and physical corruption, offers a grotesque image of nourishment (Al-Qurtubi, <em>Tafsir</em>, vol. 15).</p>
</li>
</ul>
<hr/>
<h3>3. Theological and Logical Implications</h3>
<p>From a purely theological standpoint, each of these descriptions could be interpreted metaphorically to signify various stages or types of punishment. However, the language employed is not explicitly metaphorical. Instead, it describes the physical act of eating, suggesting literal experiences in the afterlife.</p>
<h4>3.1 Contradiction or Multiplicity?</h4>
<p>Islamic apologists often argue that these are not contradictions but complementary descriptions (Al-Razi, <em>Mafatih al-Ghayb</em>). Yet, the Quranic text offers these descriptions in the context of exclusivity:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>“<strong>No food</strong> will there be for them <strong>but</strong> a bitter thorny plant” (88:6)</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>“<strong>Nor</strong> hath he any food <strong>except</strong> corruption from wounds” (69:36)</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>The Arabic terms <em>“illa”</em> (except) and <em>“laisa lahu ta’am illa”</em> (he has no food except) emphasize exclusivity and mutual exclusion. This raises a hermeneutical dilemma: how can multiple exclusive foods coexist in a logically coherent description of eschatological reality?</p>
<h4>3.2 The Failure of the Quran’s Internal Test?</h4>
<p>Given that Surah 4:82 offers a self-imposed falsifiability test—i.e., the presence of any contradiction would negate its divine origin—the co-existence of mutually exclusive descriptions challenges the claim of perfect consistency. Scholars such as W.M. Watt (1961) and John Wansbrough (1977) have long noted that literary tension and chronological layering within the Quran may reflect evolving redaction rather than singular authorship.</p>
<hr/>
<h3>4. Comparative Perspective and Scriptural Consistency</h3>
<p>In contrast, biblical eschatology—though also diverse in metaphor—maintains internal coherence without using absolute exclusive terminology. For instance, descriptions of "weeping and gnashing of teeth" or "lake of fire" (Matthew 13:42, Revelation 20:14) differ in imagery but are unified in tone and purpose without suggesting mutually exclusive realities.</p>
<hr/>
<h3>5. Conclusion</h3>
<p>The Quran’s descriptions of Hell's sustenance, when examined through the lens of exclusivity and internal coherence, raise significant theological questions. The presence of multiple, mutually exclusive food types—each presented as the only food available—presents a serious challenge to the claim found in Surah 4:82. While theological flexibility and metaphorical readings may offer partial explanations, the linguistic and logical exclusivity embedded in the verses limits interpretive maneuverability.</p>
<p>Thus, this case study illustrates how a close, scholarly critique of internal Quranic content may contest the assertion of absolute consistency and divine authorship. It invites further interfaith, philosophical, and textual dialogue regarding the Quran’s nature and origin.</p>

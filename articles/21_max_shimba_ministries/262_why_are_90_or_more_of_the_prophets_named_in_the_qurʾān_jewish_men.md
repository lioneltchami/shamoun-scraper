<h2 data-end="225" data-start="212">Abstract</h2>
<p data-end="846" data-start="226">The Qurʾān names roughly 25 prophets explicitly; a large majority correspond to figures from the Hebrew Bible (the Torah / Tanakh) and later canonical Jewish and Christian tradition (e.g., Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, Moses, David, Solomon, Jonah, Job, Joseph). This article examines that empirical fact, surveys Islamic and Christian/Jewish source material, considers historical and literary explanations, and raises critical questions directed to Muslim theological and historical accounts. The paper concludes by suggesting avenues for further research and offers footnote-style references and a journal-style bibliography.</p>
<hr data-end="851" data-start="848"/>
<h2 data-end="900" data-start="853">1. Introduction — statement of the problem</h2>
<p data-end="1660" data-start="901">Readers of the Qurʾān will note that most of the prophets named in its text are the same persons who appear in the Hebrew Bible. If one compiles the names that appear by explicit mention, a canonical count frequently cited in Islamic literature is 25 named prophets (though Islamic tradition also speaks of many thousands of prophets sent to humankind). The striking overlap between Qurʾānic prophet-names and the Hebrew Bible raises academic questions about source-relations, reception, and the Qurʾān’s self-presentation as a continuation or correction of earlier revelation. <h2 data-end="2877" data-start="2823">3. Historical and literary context (brief survey)</h2>
<p data-end="3513" data-start="2878">Scholars of early Islam note several relevant points: the Qurʾān addresses communities in late antique Arabia who were already familiar with Jewish and Christian narratives; the Qurʾān self-presents as correcting or restoring what it calls distorted earlier scriptures; and the use of familiar biblical names and episodes functions rhetorically to situate Islam within an Abrahamic line and to make moral/theological points using well-known exemplars. <h2 data-end="3593" data-start="3520">4. Onomastics (names and forms) — Arabic translations of older names</h2>
<p data-end="4268" data-start="3594">A straightforward observation is that the Qurʾān uses Arabicized forms of Semitic names (e.g., Ibrāhīm ← *Avraham/Abraham; Mūsā ← <em data-end="3731" data-start="3724">Moses</em>; Isḥāq ← <em data-end="3748" data-start="3741">Isaac</em>). Linguistically and historically this is unsurprising: Arabic and Hebrew/Aramaic are Semitic languages that share many roots and name forms; when a text moves between Semitic languages names are typically adapted phonologically. But the deeper question is not phonology alone — it is why the Qurʾān’s corpus uses a particular roster of named figures rather than naming more local Arabian, African, or Mesopotamian prophetic figures. This demands a historical explanation (see §5). <span class="" data-state="closed"><span class="ms-1 inline-flex max-w-full items-center relative top-[-0.094rem] animate-[show_150ms_ease-in]" data-testid="webpage-citation-pill"><a alt="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_characters_and_names_mentioned_in_the_Quran?utm_source=chatgpt.com" class="flex h-4.5 overflow-hidden rounded-xl px-2 text-[9px] font-medium text-token-text-secondary! bg-[#F4F4F4]! dark:bg-[#303030]! transition-colors duration-150 ease-in-out" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_characters_and_names_mentioned_in_the_Quran?utm_source=chatgpt.com" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="relative start-0 bottom-0 flex h-full w-full items-center">Wikipedia</span></a></span></span></p>

<h2 data-end="4328" data-start="4275">5. Explanatory frameworks (competing hypotheses)</h2>
<p data-end="4518" data-start="4329">Below are plausible scholarly frameworks that can help explain the predominance of Israelite/Jewish-named prophets in the Qurʾān. Each has different theological and historical consequences.</p>
<p data-end="5049" data-start="4520"><strong data-end="4577" data-start="4520">A. Continuity hypothesis (Islamic theological claim).</strong><br data-end="4580" data-start="4577"/>
Islamic doctrine often asserts prophetic continuity: God sent prophets to every people (Qurʾān 35:24; 16:36) and the Qurʾān presumes many unnamed prophets, but it names those who are paradigmatic for its message. Thus, from an Islamic theological vantage, the Qurʾān’s list emphasizes the Abrahamic line because that lineage is central to the Qurʾān’s teaching. (Qurʾān 21:25; 33:40 discusses Muhammad as the seal of the prophets.) <span class="" data-state="closed"><span class="ms-1 inline-flex max-w-full items-center relative top-[-0.094rem] animate-[show_150ms_ease-in]" data-testid="webpage-citation-pill"><a alt="https://quran.com/en/al-anbya/25?utm_source=chatgpt.com" class="flex h-4.5 overflow-hidden rounded-xl px-2 text-[9px] font-medium text-token-text-secondary! bg-[#F4F4F4]! dark:bg-[#303030]! transition-colors duration-150 ease-in-out" href="https://quran.com/en/al-anbya/25?utm_source=chatgpt.com" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="relative start-0 bottom-0 flex h-full w-full items-center"><span class="flex h-4 w-full items-center justify-between"><span class="max-w-[15ch] grow truncate overflow-hidden text-center">Quran.com</span><span class="-me-1 flex h-full items-center rounded-full px-1 text-[#8F8F8F]">+1</span></span></span></a></span></span></p>
<p data-end="5494" data-start="5051"><strong data-end="5092" data-start="5051">B. Reception/availability hypothesis.</strong><br data-end="5095" data-start="5092"/>
Late antique Arabian audiences were exposed to Jewish and Christian narratives circulating in oral and written form. The Qurʾān selects names and episodes that would have immediate recognition and rhetorical force with such audiences; hence the high overlap. This is a literary-historical account that treats the Qurʾān as reworking existing narrative material. <span class="" data-state="closed"><span class="ms-1 inline-flex max-w-full items-center relative top-[-0.094rem] animate-[show_150ms_ease-in]" data-testid="webpage-citation-pill"><a alt="https://ehrmanblog.org/creating-the-quran-where-did-the-scripture-of-islam-really-come-from-guest-post-by-stephen-shoemaker/?utm_source=chatgpt.com" class="flex h-4.5 overflow-hidden rounded-xl px-2 text-[9px] font-medium text-token-text-secondary! bg-[#F4F4F4]! dark:bg-[#303030]! transition-colors duration-150 ease-in-out" href="https://ehrmanblog.org/creating-the-quran-where-did-the-scripture-of-islam-really-come-from-guest-post-by-stephen-shoemaker/?utm_source=chatgpt.com" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="relative start-0 bottom-0 flex h-full w-full items-center"><span class="flex h-4 w-full items-center justify-between"><span class="max-w-[15ch] grow truncate overflow-hidden text-center">The Bart Ehrman Blog</span><span class="-me-1 flex h-full items-center rounded-full px-1 text-[#8F8F8F]">+1</span></span></span></a></span></span></p>
<p data-end="5965" data-start="5496"><strong data-end="5538" data-start="5496">C. Canon-formation and selection bias.</strong><br data-end="5541" data-start="5538"/>
The Qurʾān is not an encyclopedic list of every prophet in world history; rather, it quotes and names a selection of exemplars suitable to its theological aims (e.g., monotheism, covenant, patience under persecution). Those exemplars happen to be the most prominent figures in the Judeo-Christian scriptural tradition. This is a functional explanation rather than a claim about origins. <span class="" data-state="closed"><span class="ms-1 inline-flex max-w-full items-center relative top-[-0.094rem] animate-[show_150ms_ease-in]" data-testid="webpage-citation-pill"><a alt="https://quran.com/en/an-nisa/163-165?utm_source=chatgpt.com" class="flex h-4.5 overflow-hidden rounded-xl px-2 text-[9px] font-medium text-token-text-secondary! bg-[#F4F4F4]! dark:bg-[#303030]! transition-colors duration-150 ease-in-out" href="https://quran.com/en/an-nisa/163-165?utm_source=chatgpt.com" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="relative start-0 bottom-0 flex h-full w-full items-center">Quran.com</span></a></span></span></p>
<p data-end="6318" data-start="5967"><strong data-end="6009" data-start="5967">D. Polemical/communicative hypothesis.</strong><br data-end="6012" data-start="6009"/>
Some scholars argue the Qurʾān intentionally engages Jewish and Christian scriptures polemically — to assert superiority/correction of what it calls corruptions — and therefore foregrounds the Israelite prophets as subjects of correction, exhortation, or confirmation. <span class="" data-state="closed"><span class="ms-1 inline-flex max-w-full items-center relative top-[-0.094rem] animate-[show_150ms_ease-in]" data-testid="webpage-citation-pill"><a alt="https://yaqeeninstitute.org/read/paper/the-qurans-engagement-with-christian-and-jewish-literature?utm_source=chatgpt.com" class="flex h-4.5 overflow-hidden rounded-xl px-2 text-[9px] font-medium text-token-text-secondary! bg-[#F4F4F4]! dark:bg-[#303030]! transition-colors duration-150 ease-in-out" href="https://yaqeeninstitute.org/read/paper/the-qurans-engagement-with-christian-and-jewish-literature?utm_source=chatgpt.com" rel="noopener" target="_blank"><span class="relative start-0 bottom-0 flex h-full w-full items-center"><span class="flex h-4 w-full items-center justify-between"><span class="max-w-[15ch] grow truncate overflow-hidden text-center">Yaqeen Institute for Islamic Research</span><span class="-me-1 flex h-full items-center rounded-full px-1 text-[#8F8F8F]">+1</span></span></span></a></span></span></p>

<h2 data-end="9394" data-start="9337">8. Discussion — implications and a modest conclusion</h2>
<p data-end="10280" data-start="9395">That most named prophets in the Qurʾān are figures known from Jewish (and Christian) scriptures is an empirically verifiable fact. Explanations range from theological claims of prophetic continuity to historical explanations about the textual environment of late antique Arabia. For critics, this overlap raises questions about source dependence, authorship, and the Qurʾān’s self-presentation as either a restoration of older revelation or as a text that draws upon familiar religious biographies to make new claims. For Muslim apologists, the overlap is typically explained by continuity (the same God sending the same core revelation to Abrahamic figures) and by functional selection (the Qurʾān names those exemplars most relevant to its message).

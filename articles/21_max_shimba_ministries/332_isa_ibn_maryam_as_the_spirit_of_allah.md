<h2><strong>Isa Ibn Maryam as the Spirit of Allah: A Theological Examination from the Qur'an</strong></h2>
<p><strong>By Dr. Maxwell Shimba, Servant of Jesus Christ, Our Great God (Titus 2:13)</strong></p>
<hr/>
<h3><strong>Introduction</strong></h3>
<p>This paper seeks to examine a profound theological assertion based on Qur’anic testimony: that <em>Isa Ibn Maryam (Jesus, son of Mary)</em> is not merely a prophet or messenger, but the <em>Spirit of Allah</em> — and since the <em>Spirit of Allah is Allah Himself</em>, Isa must therefore be divine. Drawing directly from the Qur’an, this study explores the unique relationship between Allah and Isa Ibn Maryam as expressed in the sacred Islamic text and presents an argument for Isa’s divinity within this context.</p>
<hr/>
<h3><strong>The Nature of God and Spirit</strong></h3>
<p>Scripture affirms that <em>God is Spirit</em> (John 4:24), just as human beings possess a spirit, a soul, and dwell in a body. The intrinsic nature of spirit cannot be separated from its identity. A person’s spirit is fundamentally themselves — inseparable and essential. This theological principle applies universally: one cannot detach the essence of a being from its spirit.</p>
<hr/>
<h3><strong>Qur'anic Testimony of Isa Ibn Maryam</strong></h3>
<p>Upon examining the Qur’an, I discovered a consistent declaration that Isa Ibn Maryam uniquely shares in the Spirit of Allah in a way no other prophet or human being does. Consider the following verses:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p><strong>Al-Tahrim 66:12:</strong><br/>
<em>“And Mary, daughter of Imran, who guarded her chastity, and We breathed into her of Our Spirit. She believed in the words of her Lord and His scriptures and was of the devoutly obedient.”</em></p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>Here, the Qur’an states that what entered Mary’s womb was <em>the Spirit of Allah</em>, not a created spirit but <em>Our Spirit</em>, referring to Allah’s own essence.</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p><strong>Al-Nisa 4:171:</strong><br/>
<em>“The Messiah, Isa son of Mary, was only a Messenger of Allah and His Word which He conveyed to Mary, and a Spirit from Him…”</em></p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>Notably, Isa is called both <em>the Word of Allah</em> and <em>a Spirit from Him</em>, placing him in a unique ontological category distinct from any other prophet.</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p><strong>Al-Anbiya 21:91:</strong><br/>
<em>“And she who guarded her chastity, so We breathed into her of Our Spirit and made her and her son a sign for all peoples.”</em></p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>Again, Isa’s conception is directly linked to <em>the Spirit of Allah</em>, not a created spirit or angel.</p>
<hr/>
<h3><strong>Theological Reasoning: The Spirit of Allah Is Allah</strong></h3>
<p>No other human being — neither prophets, messengers, nor ordinary people — is ever described in the Qur’an as <em>the Spirit of Allah</em>. This distinction reserved for Isa signifies a unique divine relationship. Simple reflective reasoning leads to a powerful conclusion:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p><strong>Is my spirit and I two different beings?</strong><br/>
No. My spirit is essentially myself.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Is the Spirit of God not God Himself?</strong><br/>
Indeed, the Spirit of God is inseparable from His essence.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>Thus, if <em>the Spirit of Allah</em> entered Mary and became Isa, how can Isa not be Allah, since the Spirit of Allah is Allah Himself?<br/>
If Allah affirms Isa to be <em>“Our Spirit”</em>, then Isa bears the divine essence and identity.</p>
<hr/>
<h3><strong>Conclusion</strong></h3>
<p>The Qur'an, a book declared to be without doubt by Muslims (Al-Baqarah 2:2), explicitly teaches that Isa Ibn Maryam is <em>the Spirit of Allah</em>. By following both the Qur’anic declarations and theological logic — that <em>the Spirit of Allah is Allah Himself</em> — one must conclude that Isa partakes in the divine nature.</p>
<p>Such a conclusion aligns perfectly with Christian theology as revealed in Titus 2:13, where Jesus is called <em>“our Great God and Savior.”</em></p>
<hr/>
<h3><strong>Final Reflection</strong></h3>
<p>This theological reflection invites both Muslim and Christian scholars to re-examine the identity of Isa Ibn Maryam within their respective scriptures. If the Qur’an indeed testifies that Isa is <em>the Spirit of Allah</em>, and the Spirit of Allah is Allah Himself, then Isa’s divinity is affirmed, not merely within Christian doctrine but within the textual witness of the Qur’an itself.</p>

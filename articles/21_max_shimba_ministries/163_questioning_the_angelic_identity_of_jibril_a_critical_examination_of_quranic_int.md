# Questioning the Angelic Identity of Jibril: A Critical Examination of Qur’anic Interpretation

<p> <strong>Title:</strong></p><p>
<strong>Questioning the Angelic Identity of Jibril: A Critical Examination of Qur’anic Interpretation</strong><br/>
<em>By Dr. Maxwell Shimba – Shimba Theological Institute</em></p>
<hr/>
<h3><strong>Abstract</strong></h3>
<p>Islamic theology traditionally identifies <em>Jibril</em> (Gabriel) as one of the four archangels of Allah. However, a critical reading of the Qur’an reveals an absence of explicit declaration where Allah directly calls Jibril an <em>angel</em> (<em>malak</em>). The question arises: If the Qur’an is the ultimate source of truth, why does it never clearly say, “Jibril is one of the angels”? This article engages in a theological and exegetical debate challenging the conventional Islamic interpretation, particularly through the lens of <strong>Surah Al-Baqarah 2:98</strong>, where Jibril and Michael (<em>Mikail</em>) are mentioned separately from “the angels.”</p>
<hr/>
<h3><strong>1. Introduction: The Foundational Question</strong></h3>
<p>The Qur’an, in Surah Al-Baqarah (2:98), states:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>“Whoever is an enemy to Allah, and His angels, and His messengers, and Jibril and Mikail, then indeed, Allah is an enemy to the disbelievers.”</p>
</blockquote>
<p>This verse explicitly <em>distinguishes</em> between “angels” (<em>mala’ikah</em>) and “Jibril and Mikail.” The immediate linguistic and theological question is:<br/>
<strong>If Jibril and Mikail are indeed angels, why are they mentioned separately from ‘the angels’?</strong><br/>
In classical Arabic rhetoric, separation of nouns within a list usually indicates <em>categorical distinction</em> rather than redundancy. Hence, this verse invites critical scrutiny:<br/>
Was Jibril truly considered an angel by the Qur’an’s author, or was his nature conceptually different?</p>
<hr/>
<h3><strong>2. The Missing Declaration: “Jibril is an Angel”</strong></h3>
<p>Unlike the Bible, where Gabriel is explicitly called <em>“the angel Gabriel”</em> (Luke 1:26, Daniel 8:16), the Qur’an <strong>never once</strong> describes Jibril using the title <em>malak</em> (angel).<br/>
There is <strong>no verse</strong> in the Qur’an where Allah says:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>“Jibril is one of My angels.”</p>
</blockquote>
<p>This silence raises profound theological and linguistic questions:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p><strong>Why is Jibril’s angelic status assumed rather than stated?</strong></p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>If Jibril were truly an angel, why does the Qur’an not use the same grammatical structure it uses for other angels, such as the angels of death, punishment, or recording?</strong></p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Could Jibril have been a different class of celestial being—a spirit, or a divine messenger distinct from the angelic order?</strong></p>
</li>
</ul>
<hr/>
<h3><strong>3. Qur’anic Terminology: Jibril as “Ruh” (Spirit)</strong></h3>
<p>In multiple verses, Jibril is called <em>Ruh al-Qudus</em> (the Holy Spirit) or <em>Ruh al-Amin</em> (the Trustworthy Spirit):</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p><em>“Say, the Holy Spirit has brought it down from your Lord in truth”</em> (Surah An-Nahl 16:102)</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><em>“The Trustworthy Spirit has brought it down upon your heart”</em> (Surah Ash-Shu‘ara 26:193–194)</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>If Jibril is consistently identified as a <em>Ruh</em> (Spirit) and never explicitly as a <em>Malak</em> (Angel), one must ask:<br/>
<strong>Are “Ruh” and “Malak” identical terms in the Qur’an?</strong><br/>
If not, why has Islamic theology merged the two into one category?</p>
<p>Moreover, the Qur’an in <em>Surah Al-Qadr (97:4)</em> says:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>“The angels and the Spirit descend therein by permission of their Lord.”<br/>
Here again, <strong>the Spirit (Ruh)</strong> is listed <strong>alongside</strong> the angels, implying a <em>distinct being</em>, not one of them.</p>
</blockquote>
<hr/>
<h3><strong>4. The Linguistic Distinction and Theological Implications</strong></h3>
<p>Arabic linguists and Qur’anic commentators have long recognized that <strong>when two entities are mentioned separately</strong>, it indicates <strong>difference of essence or category</strong>.<br/>
Thus, when the Qur’an says:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>“The angels and the Spirit,”<br/>
it cannot mean “the angels including the Spirit.”<br/>
Rather, it suggests:<br/>
“The angels (a class of beings) and another distinct being known as the Spirit.”</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Therefore, from a purely linguistic standpoint, the Qur’an consistently differentiates Jibril (the Spirit) from the angelic hosts.</p>
<p>This raises further theological questions:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>If Jibril is <em>the Spirit</em>, how can he simultaneously be <em>an angel</em>?</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Did early Muslims or the Qur’an’s redactors misunderstand the original Judeo-Christian references to the Holy Spirit and the angel Gabriel as separate entities?</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Could the conflation of “Ruh” and “Malak” reflect a later interpretive tradition rather than the Qur’an’s original intent?</p>
</li>
</ul>
<hr/>
<h3><strong>5. Comparative Scriptural Context</strong></h3>
<p>In the <strong>Bible</strong>, Gabriel is clearly defined as an angel:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>“I am Gabriel, who stands in the presence of God” (Luke 1:19).<br/>
“The man Gabriel, whom I had seen in the vision, came to me in swift flight” (Daniel 9:21).</p>
</blockquote>
<p>In contrast, the Qur’an never uses such clarity.<br/>
Instead, it attributes the Qur’an’s revelation to <em>Ruh al-Qudus</em> and <em>Ruh al-Amin</em>, titles that align more with the Christian understanding of the <strong>Holy Spirit</strong> than with an angelic messenger.</p>
<p>Hence, one might debate:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Did the Qur’an adopt the name “Jibril” from Judeo-Christian sources but reinterpret his role to fit Islamic theology?</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Was Jibril originally conceived as the Spirit of Revelation, not an angelic being, and only later identified as one through Islamic exegesis (<em>tafsir</em>)?</p>
</li>
</ul>
<hr/>
<h3><strong>6. Debate Questions for Muslim Scholars</strong></h3>
<p>To stimulate further scholarly debate, the following critical questions should be addressed:</p>
<ol>
<li>
<p><strong>Where in the Qur’an</strong> does Allah directly call Jibril an <em>angel</em> (<em>malak</em>)?</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Why does <em>Surah Al-Baqarah 2:98</em> <strong>separate</strong> Jibril and Mikail from “the angels”?</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Why does the Qur’an refer to Jibril as <em>Ruh al-Qudus</em> and <em>Ruh al-Amin</em>, but never <em>Malak Jibril</em>?</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>If <em>Ruh</em> and <em>Malak</em> are the same, why are they consistently distinguished in Qur’anic grammar and syntax (e.g., Surah Al-Qadr 97:4, Surah An-Nahl 16:2)?</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Could “Jibril” in the Qur’an represent a <strong>metaphor for divine inspiration or spirit</strong>, rather than a literal angelic being?</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>How does Islamic theology reconcile the Qur’anic “Ruh” with the Christian concept of the <strong>Holy Spirit</strong>?</p>
</li>
</ol>
<hr/>
<h3><strong>7. Conclusion</strong></h3>
<p>The Qur’an’s portrayal of Jibril raises significant linguistic, theological, and hermeneutical questions. Nowhere is Jibril explicitly called an <em>angel</em>; rather, he is identified as <em>the Spirit</em>. Moreover, Qur’anic verses consistently separate him from “the angels,” suggesting a distinct identity or nature.<br/>
Thus, the Islamic assumption that Jibril is an angel appears to stem not from Qur’anic revelation itself but from <strong>post-Qur’anic interpretation and theological construction</strong>.</p>
<p>This invites a re-examination of Islamic angelology and the origin of revelation within the Qur’an’s framework—questions that remain unresolved within orthodox Islamic scholarship.</p>

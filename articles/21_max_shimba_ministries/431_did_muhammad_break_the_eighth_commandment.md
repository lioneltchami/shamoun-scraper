<h2><strong>A Theological and Scholarly Critique</strong></h2><p>
<em>By Dr. Maxwell Shimba, Shimba Theological Institute</em></p>
<h3><strong>Introduction</strong></h3>
<p>The Eighth Commandment, found in Exodus 20:15, is a pillar of Judeo-Christian morality: <strong>“You shall not steal.”</strong> This commandment upholds the sanctity of private property, condemning theft in all its forms. The question arises: Did Muhammad, the founder of Islam, uphold this commandment, or do his actions and teachings—documented in Islamic sources—constitute a violation? This article explores this question in depth, inviting scholarly debate and comparative analysis.</p>
<hr/>
<h3><strong>I. The Biblical Standard: The Eighth Commandment</strong></h3>
<p>The commandment “You shall not steal” (Exodus 20:15; Deuteronomy 5:19) is unambiguous. In the Torah and throughout the Bible, theft is condemned, whether it is robbery, fraud, kidnapping (Exodus 21:16), or even withholding fair wages (Leviticus 19:13).<br/>
<strong>Jesus affirmed this law</strong> (Matthew 19:18), and Paul warned: “Let the thief no longer steal, but rather let him labor, doing honest work with his own hands” (Ephesians 4:28). Property rights and respect for others' belongings are integral to biblical faith.</p>
<hr/>
<h3><strong>II. Muhammad’s Actions in Light of the Eighth Commandment</strong></h3>
<h4><strong>A. Raids and Distribution of Spoils (Ghanima)</strong></h4>
<p>Islamic historical sources describe Muhammad’s participation in caravan raids and battles, particularly after the Hijra to Medina.</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p><strong>The Raid at Nakhla:</strong> Early Muslims, under Muhammad’s command, attacked a Meccan caravan during a sacred month, seizing goods and captives (<a href="https://archive.org/details/TheLifeOfMuhammad_201606">Ibn Ishaq, <em>Sirat Rasul Allah</em></a>).</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>The Battle of Badr and Distribution of Booty:</strong> After victory at Badr, Muhammad divided spoils among his followers and kept a portion for himself (Quran 8:1, 8:41; <a href="https://sunnah.com/bukhari:4273">Sahih al-Bukhari 4273</a>).</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Scholarly Debate:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>
<p><strong>Defenders of Islam</strong> argue that these actions were not “theft” but acts of war, sanctioned by revelation, in the context of Arab tribal warfare.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Critics and non-Muslim scholars</strong> argue that, regardless of justification, seizing the property of others without consent constitutes theft by biblical and universal moral standards.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<h4><strong>B. Quranic Sanction for Plunder</strong></h4>
<p>The Quran addresses the question of spoils:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>“They ask you (O Muhammad) about the spoils of war. Say: The spoils are for Allah and the Messenger...” (<em>Quran 8:1</em>)<br/>
“And know that whatever of war-booty that you may gain, verily one-fifth (1/5th) thereof is assigned to Allah, and to the Messenger...” (<em>Quran 8:41</em>)</p>
</blockquote>
<p>This institutionalizes the distribution of seized property. The Hadith further details how Muhammad allotted portions to his family and followers (<a href="https://sunnah.com/muslim:1758">Sahih Muslim 1758</a>).</p>
<h4><strong>C. The Case of Fadak</strong></h4>
<p>After the Jewish tribe of Banu Nadir was expelled from Medina, Muhammad took possession of their lands without battle (<a href="https://sunnah.com/muslim:1757">Sahih Muslim 1757</a>). He also took the oasis of Fadak, which became his personal property.</p>
<hr/>
<h3><strong>III. Theological Debate: Was This Stealing?</strong></h3>
<h4><strong>A. The Islamic View</strong></h4>
<p>Muslim theologians contend that:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>The context of war justifies seizure of enemy property.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Actions sanctioned by “divine command” are not theft but legitimate spoils.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The pre-Islamic Arabian context normalized raids.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<h4><strong>B. The Judeo-Christian and Universal Ethical Perspective</strong></h4>
<ul>
<li>
<p>The commandment “You shall not steal” is absolute, not subject to context.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Seizing property, land, or goods by force or without due process is theft, regardless of religious justification.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Jesus and the apostles taught radical love for enemies, not plundering them (Matthew 5:44; Romans 12:17-21).</p>
</li>
</ul>
<h4><strong>C. Modern Scholarly Analysis</strong></h4>
<ul>
<li>
<p><strong>W. Montgomery Watt</strong> notes, “The early Muslim raids, though justified in Islamic tradition, would be considered acts of brigandage by contemporary standards” (<em>Muhammad at Medina</em>, 1956).</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Alfred Guillaume</strong> highlights the pragmatic and economic motivations behind the raids.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<hr/>
<h3><strong>IV. Case Study: The Execution of Banu Qurayza and Property Seizure</strong></h3>
<p>After the execution of the Banu Qurayza, Muhammad and his followers divided the women, children, and property ([Ibn Ishaq, <em>Sirat Rasul Allah</em>]; <a href="https://sunnah.com/bukhari:3043">Sahih Bukhari 3043</a>).</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p><strong>Biblical Law:</strong> Condemns the unjust taking of property, even in war (Deuteronomy 20:10-15 sets standards for war but still expects mercy and restraint).</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Question:</strong> Can divine sanction be invoked for acts universally recognized as theft?</p>
</li>
</ul>
<hr/>
<h3><strong>V. Conclusion: A Call to Honest Debate</strong></h3>
<p>Did Muhammad break the Eighth Commandment?</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p><strong>Islamic tradition claims divine sanction</strong> overrides human law, but from the perspective of the Bible and universal ethics, the taking of property by force is theft.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Christianity’s ethic</strong> forbids stealing, justifying violence or theft by divine right is rejected.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<h3><strong>Final Questions for Debate:</strong></h3>
<ol>
<li>
<p><strong>Does divine command ever justify theft, or is the commandment absolute?</strong></p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Should morality be context-dependent or universal?</strong></p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>If Muhammad is claimed to be a prophet in the biblical line, should his actions be held to the standard of the Ten Commandments?</strong></p>
</li>
</ol>

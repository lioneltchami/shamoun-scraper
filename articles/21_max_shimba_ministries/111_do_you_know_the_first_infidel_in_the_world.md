<h3><strong>Introduction</strong></h3>
<p>Who are the infidels?<br/>
Do you know who the first infidels in the world were?</p>
<p>The term <em>“kafiri”</em> (infidel or unbeliever) is not originally Islamic—it appears in the <strong>Holy Bible</strong> long before Muhammad, the prophet of Islam, was born, and long before Islam or the Qur’an existed. The word <em>kafiri</em> existed in Scripture centuries before the Arabs of the 7th century (such as Abu Bakr, Umar, Uthman, and Ali) emerged as followers of Islam.</p>
<p>Thus, Muslims merely <strong>found the word already in use</strong> among the people who read and taught from the Holy Bible.</p>
<p>In fact, nowhere in the Qur’an are Christians called <em>kafirs</em> (infidels). Rather, the Qur’an describes Christians as <strong>those who fear God and are learned</strong> (Surat al-Ma’idah 5:82).</p>
<p>To confirm that the word <em>kafiri</em> is biblical, let us examine several scriptural references.</p>
<hr/>
<h3><strong>Biblical References to the Word “Infidel”</strong></h3>
<ul>
<li>

</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Nehemiah 5:8–9; 5:17; 6:16</strong></p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Lamentations 1:3; 1:10; 5:2</strong></p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>1 Timothy 1:8–11</strong></p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Jude 1:4</strong></p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>1 John 4:1–6</strong></p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>These texts demonstrate that the term “infidel” or “unbeliever” was in use <strong>long before Muhammad’s birth</strong>, before the Qur’an was written, and before the rise of Islam.</p>
<hr/>
<h3><strong>The Meaning and Use of “Kafiri”</strong></h3>
<p>The word <em>kafiri</em> is Arabic and literally means <strong>“a rejecter” or “one who opposes.”</strong><br/>
In other words, it refers to a person who <strong>rejects or opposes the word of God.</strong></p>
<p>Whenever there is an initial divine statement (a <em>preceding statement</em>), and a later person or doctrine contradicts it, the contradictor becomes a <em>kafiri</em>—a rejecter of divine truth.</p>
<p>Today, Muslims often refer to Christians as <em>kafirs</em>, saying, “These infidels trouble us” or “Let us fight the infidels.” But biblically and historically, that accusation is misplaced.</p>

<ol>

</li>

</li>

</li>
</ol>
<hr/>
<h3><strong>A Brief Historical Context: Jesus and Muhammad</strong></h3>
<p>Jesus Christ is the <strong>founder of the Christian faith.</strong><br/>
According to <strong>Hebrews 12:2</strong>, He is “the author and finisher of our faith.”</p>
<p>It has been over <strong>2,000 years (since A.D. 33)</strong> since Jesus ascended into heaven, while the Islamic calendar (Hijri year 1436 at the time of writing) begins with Muhammad’s movement centuries later. Muhammad was born around <strong>A.D. 570</strong>—well after the life, death, and resurrection of Jesus.</p>
<p>Therefore, <strong>Jesus and the Christian message preceded Muhammad and Islam.</strong><br/>
The teachings of Jesus are the <strong>preceding (original) statements</strong>, while Muhammad’s later assertions are <strong>contradictory statements</strong>—making them, by definition, <em>kafiri</em> (opposing truths).</p>
<hr/>
<h3><strong>The Foundational Christian Statement: Jesus is the Son of God</strong></h3>
<p>In <strong>John 9:35–38</strong>, Jesus Himself declares that He is <strong>the Son of God</strong>.<br/>
This is an original divine claim—a <em>preceding statement</em>.</p>
<p>If any later teaching declares, “Jesus is not the Son of God,” that is a <strong>contradictory statement</strong>—an act of rejection of divine revelation—and thus the speaker is, by definition, a <em>kafiri</em>.</p>
<hr/>
<h3><strong>Who First Declared That Jesus Is the Son of God?</strong></h3>
<p>According to <strong>Luke 1:26–35</strong>, it was not a human being who first called Jesus the Son of God, but <strong>God Himself</strong>, speaking through the angel <strong>Gabriel</strong>.</p>
<p>This declaration is confirmed again in:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p><strong>Matthew 3:13–17</strong> – At Jesus’ baptism, the Father declares, “This is my beloved Son.”</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Matthew 17:1–5</strong> – At the Transfiguration, the same divine voice affirms the Sonship of Jesus.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>These are divine <em>preceding statements</em>, proclaimed by God long before Muhammad’s birth (A.D. 570) or the beginning of Islam (A.D. 610).</p>
<p>Therefore, when the Qur’an or any Islamic teaching denies that Jesus is the Son of God, such denial <strong>opposes God’s original declaration</strong> and falls under the biblical definition of <em>kafiri</em>—a rejecter of divine truth.</p>
<hr/>
<h3><strong>The First Infidel in the World</strong></h3>
<p>When God created humanity, He gave a clear command:</p>
<blockquote>
<p><strong>Genesis 2:16–17</strong> – “You must not eat from the tree of the knowledge of good and evil, for when you eat from it you will certainly die.”</p>
</blockquote>
<p>This is the <strong>original divine statement</strong>.<br/>
But then the serpent contradicted God, saying:</p>
<blockquote>
<p><strong>Genesis 3:1–4</strong> – “You will not certainly die.”</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Here, the serpent (Satan) directly opposed God’s word—making <strong>Satan the first infidel (kafiri)</strong> in the world.</p>
<p>Thus, anyone who rejects God’s revelation—such as denying that Jesus is the Son of God—aligns with the same spirit of rebellion as Satan and becomes a <em>kafiri</em>.</p>
<hr/>
<h3><strong>The Qur’an’s Contradiction</strong></h3>
<p>The Qur’an states in <strong>Surat al-An‘am 6:101</strong>:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>“He is the Creator of the heavens and the earth. How can He have a son when He has no wife?”</p>
</blockquote>
<p>This verse misunderstands the divine nature, reducing it to human reasoning. It assumes that God needs a physical wife to have a Son—an anthropomorphic limitation foreign to biblical theology.</p>
<p>The <strong>Bible</strong>, however, teaches that believers are children of God <strong>not by flesh or blood</strong>, but by faith and divine adoption (<strong>John 1:12–13</strong>).</p>
<p>Hence, the Qur’anic view <strong>opposes</strong> the earlier divine revelation and becomes, by biblical definition, a <em>kafiri</em> statement.</p>
<hr/>
<h3><strong>Children of God vs. Children of the Devil</strong></h3>
<p><strong>1 John 3:10</strong> declares:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>“In this the children of God and the children of the devil are manifest.”</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Those who reject their identity as children of God demonstrate spiritual parentage under the devil.</p>
<p>Similarly, Islamic tradition (Hadith Sahih Muslim, Vol. 4) refers to Muhammad’s wives as <em>“Ummul Mu’minin”</em>—the <em>Mothers of the Faithful.</em><br/>
But does calling them “mothers” literally mean they gave birth to all Muslims? Of course not.</p>
<p>This exposes the inconsistency of literalizing divine sonship while metaphorically interpreting human motherhood in Islam.</p>
<hr/>
<h3><strong>Conclusion</strong></h3>
<p>Christianity <strong>predates</strong> Islam.<br/>
Therefore, Islamic doctrines that <strong>oppose</strong> the preexisting truths of the Gospel are, by theological and historical definition, <em>kafiri</em>—rejections of divine revelation.</p>
<p>Consequently, <strong>Islam, Allah, Muhammad, and Jibril (Gabriel)</strong>—by their denial of Jesus’ divine Sonship—stand in contradiction to the Word of God and thus fall under the biblical category of <em>kafiri</em> (infidels).</p>

# Was Muhammad Prophesied in the New Testament as the Helper or the Holy Spirit? A Theological and Historical Analysis

<div class="post-body entry-content" id="post-body-6696867591076044273" itemprop="description articleBody">
<p></p><p><strong>Title:</strong><br/>
<strong>Was Muhammad Prophesied in the New Testament as the Helper or the Holy Spirit? A Theological and Historical Analysis</strong></p><p><strong>Author:</strong><br/>
<em>Dr. Maxwell Shimba, Servant of Jesus Christ, the Great God (Titus 2:13)</em><br/>
<em>Shimba Theological Institute, New York, NY</em></p><hr/><h3>Abstract</h3><p>Some Islamic apologists claim that Muhammad was prophesied in the New Testament, specifically in the Gospel of John (chapters 14–16), as the "Helper" or "Comforter" (Greek: <em>Paraklētos</em>), also known as the Holy Spirit. This article critically examines these claims by analyzing the biblical text and historical Christian interpretation. It demonstrates that such assertions are inconsistent both with the internal biblical evidence and with traditional Islamic theology.</p><hr/><h3>Introduction</h3><p>A recurring claim among some Islamic scholars and da’wah proponents is that the <em>Paraklētos</em>—translated as "Helper" or "Comforter" in the Gospel of John—refers to the prophet Muhammad. This interpretation, however, diverges from both Christian theological understanding and the internal coherence of the Gospel narrative. The relevant passages in question are John 14:16–26, 15:26, and 16:5–15.</p><p>This paper provides a theological rebuttal to such claims and outlines why the <em>Paraklētos</em> must be identified as the Holy Spirit, not Muhammad, as understood consistently throughout Christian history.</p><hr/><h3>Scriptural Analysis: Gospel of John 14–16</h3><p>The relevant passages read:</p><ul>
<li>
<p><strong>John 14:26</strong> – “But the Helper, the Holy Spirit, whom the Father will send in my name, He will teach you all things...”</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>John 15:26</strong> – “When the Helper comes, whom I will send to you from the Father—the Spirit of truth who proceeds from the Father—He will bear witness about me.”</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>John 16:13–14</strong> – “When the Spirit of truth comes... He will glorify me, for He will take what is mine and declare it to you.”</p>
</li>
</ul><hr/><h3>Inconsistencies with the Islamic Interpretation</h3><p>If these passages were truly about Muhammad, then logically Muslims would need to affirm the following five propositions:</p><ol>
<li>
<p><strong>Muhammad glorified Jesus</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>
<p><em>John 16:14</em>: “He will glorify me.”</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Yet, in Islam, Jesus is considered a prophet subordinate to Muhammad, not glorified above him.</p>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Muhammad was sent by God in Jesus’ name</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>
<p><em>John 14:26</em>: “...whom the Father will send in my name.”</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>No Islamic teaching affirms that Muhammad was sent in Jesus' name.</p>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Muhammad was sent by Jesus</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>
<p><em>John 16:7</em>: “...for if I do not go away, the Helper will not come to you. But if I go, I will send him to you.”</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Islam does not teach that Muhammad was sent by Jesus, which would imply Jesus’ divine authority over Muhammad.</p>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Muhammad took from Jesus’ knowledge and made it known</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>
<p><em>John 16:15</em>: “All that the Father has is mine; therefore I said that He will take what is mine and declare it to you.”</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Islam does not claim that Muhammad relied on the teachings or revelation of Jesus for his message.</p>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Muhammad dwelt within the disciples</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>
<p><em>John 14:17</em>: “...He dwells with you and will be in you.”</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Muhammad, a human prophet, did not spiritually indwell anyone, whereas the Holy Spirit is described as indwelling believers.</p>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ol><p>Thus, for the Islamic interpretation to be valid, Muslims would have to accept a Christology and pneumatology inconsistent with Islamic doctrine.</p><hr/><h3>Historical Testimony</h3><p>Before the Council of Nicaea (325 A.D.), early Church Fathers had already identified the <em>Paraklētos</em> as the Holy Spirit. For instance, <strong>Archelaus of Mesopotamia</strong> (c. 262–278 A.D.) in his <strong>Disputation with Manes</strong> (ch. 34–35, pp. 208–209) clearly refers to the Helper as the Holy Spirit.</p><p>This interpretation predates Islam by centuries and shows no evidence that Christians understood this prophecy to refer to a future human prophet.</p><hr/><h3>Scholarly References</h3><ul>

</li>
<li>
<p>Geisler, Norman L., and Thomas Howe. <em>When Critics Ask: A Popular Handbook on Bible Difficulties.</em> Wheaton, IL: Victor Books, 1992, pp. 419–420.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Archelaus. <em>The Acts of the Disputation with the Heresiarch Manes</em>, in <em>Ante-Nicene Fathers</em>, vol. 6. Edited by Alexander Roberts and James Donaldson.</p>
</li>
</ul><hr/><h3>Conclusion</h3><p>The claim that Muhammad is the <em>Paraklētos</em> of John 14–16 is theologically untenable and historically unsupported. The internal logic of the Gospel of John, the consistent witness of early Christian writers, and the contradictions with Islamic doctrine all point to the identification of the <em>Helper</em> as the Holy Spirit, not a future prophet. Consequently, any serious theological reflection must reject the Muslim reinterpretation as an anachronistic and doctrinally flawed imposition on the New Testament text.</p><hr/><p><strong>Shalom,</strong><br/>

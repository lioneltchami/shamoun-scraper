</h1><h2><span style="font-size: small; font-weight: normal;">Abstract</span></h2><h1>
<p><span style="font-size: small; font-weight: normal;">This paper examines the hadith narrated by Aisha (reported in Jāmi‘ at-Tirmidhī, no. 3270) in which a camel is said to have prostrated before the Prophet Muḥammad and the Prophet’s reported comment about commanding prostration. The study: (1) presents the textual evidence; (2) surveys classical and contemporary Sunni explanations; (3) analyses the charge that such reports imply animals/trees “committed shirk” and therefore will be condemned (Q 21:98–99); (4) discusses the normative status of the narrators (Aisha and the Companions) and whether accusing them of lying is warranted; and (5) offers concluding remarks and avenues for further research. Key primary and secondary sources are cited.</span></p>
<hr/>
</h1><h2><span style="font-size: small; font-weight: normal;">1. The primary text (presentation)</span></h2><h1>
<p><span style="font-size: small; font-weight: normal;">The narration commonly cited is recorded in Jāmi‘ at-Tirmidhī (no. 3270) and in other collections: the Arabic and translations give the gist that Aisha narrated an occasion when “a camel came and prostrated itself before him [the Prophet];” the Companions exclaimed that beasts and trees prostrate before him, and the Prophet replied: “Worship your Lord and honour your brother. If I were to order anyone to prostrate himself before another, I would have ordered a woman to prostrate before her husband…” (translation summary). The hadith appears in Tirmidhī’s chapter on tafsīr and has parallel reports in other works. (<a href="https://sunnah.com/tirmidhi%3A3270?utm_source=chatgpt.com" title="Jami` at-Tirmidhi 3270 - Chapters on Tafsir - كتاب تفسير ...">Sunnah.com</a>)</span></p>

</h1><h2><span style="font-size: small; font-weight: normal;">2. Immediate theological problem stated by the questioner</span></h2><h1>

<ol>
<li>
<p><span style="font-size: small; font-weight: normal;">If animals and trees prostrate before the Prophet, does that amount to worship of the Prophet (shirk), and are those creatures therefore condemned with their “lord” (the Prophet) to Hell as implied by a literal reading of Q 21:98–99?</span></p>
</li>
<li>
<p><span style="font-size: small; font-weight: normal;">Given that this hadith is narrated by Aisha (and that Companions reportedly affirmed animals/trees prostrating), is it legitimate to suspect falsehood (i.e., were Aisha/the Companions lying against the Prophet)?</span></p>

</h1><h2><span style="font-size: small; font-weight: normal;">3. Scholarly and traditional Muslim responses (summary and analysis)</span></h2><h3><span style="font-size: small; font-weight: normal;">3.1 On the authenticity and transmission of the narration</span></h3><h1>

<li>
<p><span style="font-size: small; font-weight: normal;">The report appears in Tirmidhī and other compendia and is discussed by hadith scholars. Different transmitters and parallel versions exist; hence scholars discuss its status and variant readings. </li>

</h1><h3><span style="font-size: small; font-weight: normal;">3.2 On the meaning of animals/trees “prostrating”</span></h3><h1>
<p><span style="font-size: small; font-weight: normal;">Muslim exegetical and juridical tradition offers several interpretive options:</span></p>
<ol>
<li>
<p><span style="font-size: small; font-weight: normal;">Literal miraculous submission (miracle): Some scholars accept the report as describing a miraculous instance — animals and creation sometimes manifest submission to God through extraordinary acts when God wills it — but they emphasize that such submission is not equivalent to <em>worship of the Prophet</em>. The miracle would demonstrate the Prophet’s special status and God’s control over creation, not that the creatures redirected their worship from God to a created being. (See classical tafsīr and hadith commentaries treating miraculous signs of the prophets.) (<a href="https://sunnah.com/tirmidhi/47?utm_source=chatgpt.com" title="Chapters on Tafsir">Sunnah.com</a>)</span></p>
</li>
<li>
<p><span style="font-size: small; font-weight: normal;">Phenomenological/figurative reading: Other exegetes read such narrations as figurative or hyperbolic — reporting the companions’ impression that “even beasts show submission,” meaning the environment’s calmness, the camel’s unusual behavior, or exaggerated speech of bystanders, rather than a literal ritual prostration implying liturgical worship of the Prophet. This avoids theological conflict with tawḥīd (divine unity). (<a href="https://www.researchgate.net/publication/335986982_Wife_Prostrating_Upon_The_Husband_From_The_Perspective_Of_Fiqh_Al-Hadith?utm_source=chatgpt.com" title="(PDF) Wife Prostrating Upon The Husband From The ...">ResearchGate</a>)</span></p>
</li>
<li>
<p><span style="font-size: small; font-weight: normal;">Narrative variants and context-sensitive reading: Some hadith variants omit explicit wording that would imply worship of the Prophet; scholars caution against drawing universal doctrines from singular, potentially ambiguous reports. The Prophet’s own reply in the narration — “Worship your Lord and honor your brother” and the clear rejection of instructing people to prostrate to him — is often treated as an internal corrective that prevents a reading that the Prophet accepted prostration to himself. This self-correction in the report is decisive for mainstream Sunni theology. (<a href="https://www.islamweb.net/en/fatwa/275320/meaning-of-the-hadeeth-if-i-were-to-command-anyone-to-fall-prostrate-before-anyone%E2%80%A6?utm_source=chatgpt.com" title="Meaning of the Hadeeth If I were to command anyone ...">IslamWeb</a>)</span></p>
</li>
</ol>
</h1><h3><span style="font-size: small; font-weight: normal;">3.3 On the Qur’anic verses (Q 21:98–99) the questioner cites</span></h3><h1>
<p><span style="font-size: small; font-weight: normal;">Quran 21:98–99 reads in standard translations along the lines: “Verily you (disbelievers) and your partners — the things you used to worship besides God — are the fuel of Hell. Surely you will enter it.” Classical tafsīr notes that the verses warn idolaters that both people and the objects of their worship are (figuratively) fuel for Hell. Commentators discuss apparent paradoxes (e.g., what about prophets or angels who may have been venerated) and explain that <em>those who accept and promote being worshipped instead of God</em> are culpable — if anyone (human, angel, or object) were to claim or accept worship in place of God, that one would share in the accountability of those who worshipped them. Tafsīr literature often quotes reports where the Prophet explains that anyone who accepts worship will be liable alongside those who worshipped them. Thus exegetes harmonize the Qur’anic text with reports of admiration or even veneration by clarifying that veneration must not be formal worship or obedience that contradicts tawḥīd. (<a href="https://myislam.org/surah-al-anbiya/ayat-98/?utm_source=chatgpt.com" title="Surah Al-Anbiya Ayat 98 (21:98 Quran) With Tafsir">My Islam</a>)</span></p>

</h1><h2><span style="font-size: small; font-weight: normal;">4. Does the hadith imply shirk by animals/trees or the Prophet? (analysis)</span></h2><h1>
<p><span style="font-size: small; font-weight: normal;">A careful, historically sensitive answer requires separating levels of meaning:</span></p>
<ul>
<li>
<p><span style="font-size: small; font-weight: normal;">Prostration vs. worship: In the Islamic normative system, <em>prostration as ritual worship (sujūd ibādah)</em> is strictly for God. The Prophet himself explicitly forbade prostrating to anyone other than Allah and rebuked any attempt to ascribe divine status to him. The hadith’s internal dynamics (the companions’ comment and the Prophet’s corrective reply) support the interpretation that the event—if literal—was a sign from God and not an invitation to worship the Prophet as deity. Thus the mainstream Sunni response is that the narration does not imply shirk on the Prophet’s part nor legitimate worship of the Prophet by creatures. (<a href="https://www.islamweb.net/en/fatwa/275320/meaning-of-the-hadeeth-if-i-were-to-command-anyone-to-fall-prostrate-before-anyone%E2%80%A6?utm_source=chatgpt.com" title="Meaning of the Hadeeth If I were to command anyone ...">IslamWeb</a>)</span></p>
</li>
<li>
<p><span style="font-size: small; font-weight: normal;">Quranic compatibility: The Qur’an’s warning (21:98–99) that “you and what you worship besides God will be fuel for Hell” targets <em>those who designate or accept rivals to God’s worship</em>. Classical tafsīr reconciles apparent difficulties by reporting prophetic clarifications: if any created being were to accept or command worship, then that being would share in the moral responsibility (not that animals by nature will be judged the same as rational agents). Many exegetes emphasize moral agency as the determinant of culpability — animals, lacking moral agency, are not morally culpable in the same way humans are. Thus equating a miraculous animal action with intentional shirk is not required by the Qur’an and is rejected by most tafsīrists. (<a href="https://myislam.org/surah-al-anbiya/ayat-98/?utm_source=chatgpt.com" title="Surah Al-Anbiya Ayat 98 (21:98 Quran) With Tafsir">My Islam</a>)</span></p>
</li>
</ul>

</h1><h2><span style="font-size: small; font-weight: normal;">5. Were Aisha and the Companions “lying against the Prophet”? — methodological caution</span></h2><h1>
<p><span style="font-size: small; font-weight: normal;">From a scholarly perspective:</span></p>
<ul>
<li>
<p><span style="font-size: small; font-weight: normal;">Traditional Sunni stance: Aisha (radiyallāhu ‘anha) and the Companions are accorded high credibility in Sunni hadith methodology. Alleging deliberate fabrication (kadhb) against such central figures requires extraordinary evidence. Scholarly practice examines chains (isnād) and variants, and when problems appear, scholars discuss weakness (da‘f), mis-transmission, or interpretive scope — not immediate moral accusation. Primary hadith criticism treats reliability and variant forms as the method to resolve tension. (<a href="https://sunnah.com/tirmidhi/47?utm_source=chatgpt.com" title="Chapters on Tafsir">Sunnah.com</a>)</span></p>
</li>
<li>
<p><span style="font-size: small; font-weight: normal;">Alternative critical academic approaches: Modern historical-critical scholarship may treat such narratives as historically situated reports that reflect later community memory, rhetorical devices, or hagiographic motifs. Such scholarship may propose non-literal readings or regard some narrations as later embellishments. These approaches are methodological and do not necessarily accuse narrators of intentional falsehood; rather, they analyze how oral transmission, redaction, and communal memory shaped the corpus. (See research on the formation of hadith literature and thematic motifs.) (<a href="https://www.researchgate.net/publication/335986982_Wife_Prostrating_Upon_The_Husband_From_The_Perspective_Of_Fiqh_Al-Hadith?utm_source=chatgpt.com" title="(PDF) Wife Prostrating Upon The Husband From The ...">ResearchGate</a>)</span></p>
</li>
</ul>
<p><span style="font-size: small; font-weight: normal;">Conclusion on this question: There is no scholarly warrant in mainstream Sunni tradition to allege that Aisha or the Companions were lying about the event. Debates focus on the precise meaning, the chain strength, and theological implications, not on imputing moral corruption to foundational narrators.</span></p>

<h2>📖 <strong>Arabic Commentary from <em>Tuhfat al-Aḥwadhī</em></strong></h2>
<p><em>(Sharḥ of al-Mubārakfūrī on Jāmi‘ at-Tirmidhī 3270)</em></p>
<blockquote>
<p>قَوْلُهُ: <strong>"فَأَقْبَلَ جَمَلٌ فَخَرَّ سَاجِدًا لِرَسُولِ اللَّهِ ﷺ"</strong><br/>
أَيْ خَرَّ عَلَى وَجْهِهِ تَوَاضُعًا وَخُضُوعًا، لَا سُجُودَ عِبَادَةٍ، بَلْ سُجُودَ تَحِيَّةٍ كَمَا كَانَ فِي شَرِيعَةِ مَنْ قَبْلَنَا، وَكَانَ ذَلِكَ آيَةً مِنْ آيَاتِ نُبُوَّتِهِ ﷺ.</p>
<p>وَقَوْلُهُ: <strong>"تَسْجُدُ لَكَ الْبَهَائِمُ وَالشَّجَرُ"</strong><br/>
أَيْ تَتَذَلَّلُ وَتَنْقَادُ لَكَ انْقِيَادًا تَامًّا، وَيَكُونُ ذَلِكَ بِأَمْرِ اللَّهِ، فَلَا يَكُونُ فِيهِ شَيْءٌ مِنَ الشِّرْكِ.</p>
<p>وَقَوْلُهُ ﷺ: <strong>"اعْبُدُوا رَبَّكُمْ وَأَكْرِمُوا أَخَاكُمْ"</strong><br/>
فِيهِ تَنْبِيهٌ عَلَى أَنَّ السُّجُودَ لِلْمَخْلُوقِ مَمْنُوعٌ فِي شَرِيعَتِنَا، وَإِنَّمَا كَانَ مَشْرُوعًا فِي الْأُمَمِ السَّابِقَةِ، فَنُسِخَ ذَلِكَ فِي الْإِسْلَامِ.</p>
<p>وَقَوْلُهُ: <strong>"لَوْ كُنْتُ آمِرًا أَحَدًا أَنْ يَسْجُدَ لِأَحَدٍ..."</strong><br/>
أَيْ لِبَيَانِ شِدَّةِ حَقِّ الزَّوْجِ عَلَى زَوْجَتِهِ، وَلَيْسَ الْمُرَادُ الْإِيجَازُ بِالْأَمْرِ بِالسُّجُودِ، بَلْ التَّغْلِيظُ فِي الْحَقِّ.</p>
</blockquote>
<hr/>
<h2>🧕🏽 <strong>English Scholarly Explanation</strong></h2>
<h3>1. <strong>Nature of the Camel’s Prostration</strong></h3>
<p>According to al-Mubārakfūrī, the camel’s act of “prostration” (<em>sujūd</em>) should <strong>not</strong> be understood as <em>worship</em> (<em>‘ibādah</em>), but rather as a <strong>gesture of humility and submission</strong> created miraculously by God to demonstrate the Prophet’s honor.</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>It is comparable to the <strong>prostration of the angels to Adam</strong> (Qur’an 2:34) — a sign of respect commanded by Allah, not an act of polytheistic worship.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Thus, the act falls under <strong>sujūd at-taḥiyyah</strong> (prostration of greeting or respect), which was <em>permissible</em> in some previous dispensations but <strong>abrogated</strong> in Islam.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>Al-Mubārakfūrī writes:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>“It was a sign (<em>āyah</em>) of his Prophethood, not a declaration of divinity. The creature’s submission was by Allah’s command, so no element of shirk is contained in it.”</p>
</blockquote>
<hr/>
<h3>2. <strong>Prostration Prohibited in Islam</strong></h3>
<p>The Prophet’s response — “<strong>Worship your Lord and honor your brother</strong>” — serves as a <strong>theological correction</strong>:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>The Companions’ enthusiasm could have led to confusion between <em>respect</em> and <em>worship</em>.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The Prophet clarified that <strong>only Allah</strong> is to be worshipped, and that all forms of prostration (<em>sujūd</em>) before creatures are <strong>forbidden</strong> in Islam.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>By saying, <em>“If I were to order anyone to prostrate before anyone else…”</em>, he used a <strong>rhetorical conditional</strong> — not to command such an act, but to <strong>emphasize the great right of a husband over his wife</strong>.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<hr/>
<h3>3. <strong>Historical and Theological Context</strong></h3>
<ul>
<li>
<p><em>Sujūd at-taḥiyyah</em> (prostration of greeting) existed among previous nations — for instance, Joseph’s brothers bowed before him (Qur’an 12:100).</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Islam <strong>abrogated</strong> that custom, replacing physical prostration with verbal salutation (<em>salām</em>).</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Therefore, when an animal or tree “prostrated,” it symbolized <em>recognition of the Prophet’s status</em>, not <em>worship</em> of him.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>As Ibn al-‘Arabī notes in <em>‘Āriḍat al-Aḥwadhī</em>:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>“This act was extraordinary — a miracle (<em>mu‘jizah</em>) — demonstrating creation’s obedience to the Messenger by God’s will, not a sign of the Prophet’s divinity.”</p>
</blockquote>
<hr/>
<h3>4. <strong>Refutation of the Shirk Allegation (Theological Analysis)</strong></h3>
<p>The questioner’s claim — that if beasts and trees prostrate before Muhammad, they commit shirk (polytheism) — fails under Islamic theology because:</p>
<ol>
<li>
<p><strong>Animals and inanimate objects lack moral agency (taklīf)</strong> and cannot “commit” shirk.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The Qur’an states that <em>“to Allah prostrates whatever is in the heavens and the earth”</em> (Qur’an 16:49–50; 22:18).</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Hence, when creation “prostrates,” it signifies <strong>universal submission to Allah</strong>, not worship of intermediaries.</p>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li>
<p>The Prophet explicitly forbade being prostrated to, thus affirming his pure monotheism.</p>
</li>
</ol>

<hr/>
<h3>6. <strong>Conclusion</strong></h3>
<p>The classical scholars unanimously hold that:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>The <strong>camel’s prostration</strong> was a <strong>miracle (mu‘jizah)</strong> granted by Allah to demonstrate the Prophet’s rank.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The <strong>Companions’ remark</strong> (“Beasts and trees prostrate to you”) expressed amazement, not theological assertion.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The <strong>Prophet’s correction</strong> reaffirmed <strong>tawḥīd</strong> — the exclusive worship of Allah.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Therefore, neither Muhammad, nor the animals, nor the trees committed or accepted <em>shirk</em> in this event.</p>
</li>
</ul>

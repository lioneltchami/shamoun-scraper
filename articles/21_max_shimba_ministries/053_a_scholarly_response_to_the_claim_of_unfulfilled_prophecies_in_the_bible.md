<p></p><h2>A Scholarly Response to the Claim of “Unfulfilled Prophecies” in the Bible</h2><p><strong>By Dr. Maxwell Shimba, Shimba Theological Institute</strong></p><p>The claim that the Bible contains “unfulfilled prophecies” is a common polemical argument often made by Muslim apologists who, unfortunately, approach the text without understanding its literary genres, historical contexts, linguistic nuances, and theological frameworks. Below, we address each of the examples cited and demonstrate that they do not constitute “failed prophecies” at all.</p><hr/><h3>1. <strong>Genesis 4:12 vs. Genesis 4:17 – Cain’s “Wandering”</strong></h3><p><strong>The Claim:</strong> God told Cain he would be a wanderer, but later Cain built a city, which the critic says contradicts the prophecy.</p><p><strong>Textual Analysis:</strong></p><ul><li><p><strong>Genesis 4:12 (ESV):</strong> “When you work the ground, it shall no longer yield to you its strength. You shall be a fugitive and a wanderer on the earth.”</p></li><li><p><strong>Genesis 4:17:</strong> “Cain knew his wife, and she conceived and bore Enoch. When he built a city, he called the name of the city after the name of his son, Enoch.”</p></li></ul><p><strong>Response:</strong><br/>This objection stems from a <strong>misunderstanding of Hebrew idiom and prophecy.</strong> God’s statement was not a deterministic decree that Cain <em>could never</em> build a settlement, but a pronouncement of the <strong>curse and condition</strong> of his life as a <em>restless exile</em>. The Hebrew term translated “wanderer” (<em>nʿ</em>) means <em>to live as one estranged or unsettled</em>, not necessarily physically moving constantly.</p><p>Moreover, Cain’s attempt to “build a city” can be seen as an <strong>act of defiance</strong> against God’s judgment—much like humanity’s later attempt to build the Tower of Babel. It does not nullify the divine sentence but rather <em>demonstrates Cain’s continued rebellion</em>. The narrative does not portray Cain’s “city” as a stable, enduring civilization, but as part of the tragic consequences of sin.</p><hr/><h3>2. <strong>Jeremiah 36:30 vs. 2 Kings 24:6 – Jehoiachin on David’s Throne</strong></h3><p><strong>The Claim:</strong> Jeremiah says no descendant of Jehoiakim will sit on David’s throne, yet his son Jehoiachin does.</p><p><strong>Textual Analysis:</strong></p><ul><li><p><strong>Jeremiah 36:30:</strong> “Therefore thus says the LORD concerning Jehoiakim king of Judah: He shall have none to sit on the throne of David…”</p></li><li><p><strong>2 Kings 24:6:</strong> “So Jehoiakim slept with his fathers, and Jehoiachin his son reigned in his place.”</p></li></ul><p><strong>Response:</strong><br/>The critic misunderstands <strong>prophetic language</strong> and <strong>covenantal context.</strong> Jeremiah’s statement refers not to the <em>immediate succession</em> but to the <strong>enduring Davidic kingship</strong> through Jehoiakim’s line. Indeed, Jehoiachin’s reign lasted only <strong>three months</strong> (2 Kings 24:8) before Babylon deposed him—an event that precisely fulfills Jeremiah’s prophecy.</p><p>In biblical terms, “to sit on the throne” (<em>yashav ʿal-kisseʾ Dāwid</em>) implies <strong>established, enduring rule</strong>, not merely ascending the throne for a brief, failed tenure. Thus, Jehoiakim’s line <em>did not continue the Davidic monarchy</em>, exactly as God foretold.</p><hr/><h3>3. <strong>Ezekiel 26 – The Destruction of Tyre</strong></h3><p><strong>The Claim:</strong> Ezekiel said Nebuchadnezzar would destroy Tyre, but Alexander the Great did.</p><p><strong>Textual Analysis:</strong></p><ul><li><p><strong>Ezekiel 26:7–14:</strong> “For thus says the Lord GOD: Behold, I will bring against Tyre from the north Nebuchadnezzar king of Babylon… he shall kill with the sword your daughters on the mainland… They will break down your walls… and I will make you a bare rock.”</p></li></ul><p><strong>Response:</strong><br/>This is a textbook case of selective reading. The prophecy in Ezekiel 26 is not a single-event prediction but a <strong>multi-stage oracle</strong> against Tyre. The prophecy has two layers:</p><ol><li><p><strong>Nebuchadnezzar (vv. 7–11)</strong> would lay siege to Tyre and destroy its mainland settlements (“your daughters”). This was fulfilled historically when Nebuchadnezzar besieged Tyre for <strong>13 years (586–573 BC)</strong>, devastating the mainland and forcing Tyre to pay tribute.</p></li><li><p><strong>“They” (plural, vv. 12–14)</strong>—a shift in pronoun from singular to plural—refers to <strong>subsequent conquerors</strong>, culminating in Alexander the Great’s conquest in <strong>332 BC</strong>, when the island city was finally demolished and scraped “like a bare rock.”</p></li></ol><p>Thus, Ezekiel’s prophecy was fulfilled <strong>in stages over time</strong>, a common pattern in biblical prophetic literature.</p><hr/><h3>4. <strong>Isaiah 7:14 – “Virgin” and “Immanuel”</strong></h3><p><strong>The Claim:</strong> The Hebrew word <em>ʿalmāh</em> means “young woman,” not “virgin,” and Jesus was never called Immanuel.</p><p><strong>Textual and Linguistic Analysis:</strong></p><ul><li><p><strong>Isaiah 7:14:</strong> “Behold, the virgin (<em>ʿalmāh</em>) shall conceive and bear a son, and shall call his name Immanuel.”</p></li></ul><p><strong>Response:</strong></p><ol><li><p><strong>Linguistics of <em>ʿalmāh</em>:</strong><br/>The claim that <em>ʿalmāh</em> cannot mean “virgin” is linguistically inaccurate. While <em>bĕtûlāh</em> often denotes virginity, <em>ʿalmāh</em> is used <strong>only for unmarried young women of marriageable age</strong>, which <em>implies virginity in the cultural context</em>. The Septuagint (LXX), translated by Jewish scholars two centuries before Christ, rendered <em>ʿalmāh</em> as <strong>παρθένος (<em>parthenos</em>)</strong>, which <em>unequivocally means “virgin.”</em></p></li><li><p><strong>“Immanuel” – God With Us:</strong><br/>Ancient Hebrew naming conventions were <strong>theological, not literal</strong>. Names often described a person’s <em>mission or nature</em> rather than being their personal name. For example, Jacob was called “Israel,” and Jesus was called “Wonderful Counselor, Mighty God” (Isaiah 9:6).</p></li></ol><p>Matthew explicitly interprets Isaiah 7:14 as fulfilled in Christ (Matthew 1:22–23), explaining that <em>Immanuel</em> (“God with us”) is descriptive of Jesus’ divine nature and incarnation—not a literal given name. Jesus’ title “Emmanuel” thus expresses the theological reality of the <strong>Incarnation—God dwelling among humanity.</strong></p><hr/><h2>Conclusion: Misunderstandings Do Not Equal Contradictions</h2><p>Each of the examples presented is based on <strong>surface-level reading, linguistic ignorance, or misinterpretation of prophetic genre.</strong> Biblical prophecy often employs:</p><ul><li><p><strong>Conditional and typological language</strong></p></li><li><p><strong>Partial and progressive fulfillment</strong></p></li><li><p><strong>Symbolic naming and metaphors</strong></p></li></ul><p>These features are not errors but hallmarks of <strong>ancient Near Eastern prophetic literature</strong>, widely recognized by scholars of Semitic languages and biblical theology.</p><p>Ironically, the Qur’an itself acknowledges the Torah and Gospel as <strong>divine revelation (Q 5:44–47)</strong>—yet the same critics reject them on the basis of arguments that crumble under scholarly scrutiny. The alleged “unfulfilled prophecies” are not evidence against the Bible but rather testimony to its <strong>depth, complexity, and enduring truth.</strong></p><hr/><p><strong>References:</strong></p><ul><li><p>John Walton, <em>Ancient Near Eastern Thought and the Old Testament</em> (Baker, 2018)</p></li><li><p>Gleason Archer, <em>Encyclopedia of Bible Difficulties</em> (Zondervan, 1982)</p></li><li><p>R.T.

<h1>Isaiah 9:6–7 and the Question of Corruption:</h1>
<h2>A Scholarly Response to Islamic Polemics</h2>
<p><strong>By Dr. Maxwell Shimba</strong><br/>
<em>Shimba Theological Institute</em></p>
<hr/>
<h3>Abstract</h3>
<p>Muslim apologists frequently charge that Christian translators have “corrupted” Isaiah 9:6–7 by interpolating exalted titles such as <em>“Wonderful Counselor, Mighty God, Everlasting Father, Prince of Peace.”</em> They contrast this with the Septuagint (LXX), which reads <em>“Messenger of Great Counsel.”</em> This paper demonstrates that the alleged corruption is a misunderstanding of <strong>textual criticism, translation history, and theology.</strong> By examining the Hebrew Masoretic Text (MT), the Septuagint (LXX), and the Dead Sea Scrolls (DSS), this study affirms the integrity of Isaiah’s prophecy and its fulfillment in Jesus Christ.</p>
<hr/>
<h3>1. Introduction</h3>
<p>Among the most profound messianic prophecies in the Hebrew Bible, Isaiah 9:6–7 stands as a cornerstone for Jewish and Christian theology. The child promised here is described as a Davidic ruler whose reign will never end. Christians have traditionally understood this prophecy as fulfilled in Jesus Christ.</p>
<p>Islamic critics, however, often argue that the Christian rendering of the text is the result of <strong>interpolation</strong>. They suggest that translators, particularly of the Hebrew Masoretic tradition, added divine titles to support Christological doctrines. Instead, they claim, the Septuagint’s reading (<em>“Messenger of Great Counsel”</em>) represents the authentic text.</p>
<p>This paper will demonstrate that such claims are historically, textually, and theologically flawed.</p>
<hr/>
<h3>2. Textual Foundations of Isaiah 9:6–7</h3>
<h4>2.1 The Masoretic Text (MT)</h4>
<p>The MT, preserved by Jewish scribes, clearly reads:</p>
<blockquote>
<p><em>“Wonderful Counselor, Mighty God, Everlasting Father, Prince of Peace”</em> (Isaiah 9:6, MT).</p>
</blockquote>
<p>The Hebrew terms—<em>peleʾ yōʿēṣ, ʾēl gibbōr, ʾaḇî-ʿaḏ, śar šālôm</em>—unambiguously confer divine attributes on the promised child. This reading has been preserved consistently across Jewish tradition, predating Christianity by centuries.</p>
<h4>2.2 The Dead Sea Scrolls (DSS)</h4>
<p>The DSS (150 B.C.–70 A.D.) provide critical pre-Christian evidence. The <strong>Great Isaiah Scroll (1QIsaᵃ)</strong> contains Isaiah 9:6 with the reading <em>El Gibbor</em> (“Mighty God”).<sup><a aria-describedby="footnote-label" data-footnote-ref="" href="https://chatgpt.com/c/68d48f10-bde0-832c-92e8-b08fb0af7d64#user-content-fn-1" id="user-content-fnref-1">1</a></sup> This demonstrates that the exalted titles existed <strong>before Christianity</strong> and thus cannot be later Christian interpolations.</p>
<h4>2.3 The Septuagint (LXX)</h4>
<p>The LXX, translated into Greek ca. 250–150 B.C., renders Isaiah 9:6 more interpretively:</p>
<blockquote>
<p><em>“His name is called Messenger of Great Counsel, for I will bring peace upon the rulers, peace and health to him.”</em></p>
</blockquote>
<p>This translation does not negate the MT’s reading but reflects a <strong>functional rendering</strong> of the child’s role as the bearer of God’s salvific plan.</p>
<hr/>
<h3>3. Textual Variants and the Question of Corruption</h3>
<p>Muslim polemicists equate textual diversity with corruption. However, <strong>textual criticism</strong> shows otherwise:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Variants reflect <strong>translation philosophy</strong> (literal vs. interpretive), not falsification.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The MT and DSS attest the divine titles long before the rise of Christianity or Islam.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The LXX often paraphrases to make Hebrew idioms accessible to Greek audiences.<sup><a aria-describedby="footnote-label" data-footnote-ref="" href="https://chatgpt.com/c/68d48f10-bde0-832c-92e8-b08fb0af7d64#user-content-fn-2" id="user-content-fnref-2">2</a></sup></p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>To claim “corruption” is to impose an anachronistic Islamic lens on Jewish and Christian textual history.</p>
<hr/>
<h3>4. Theological Implications of the Text</h3>
<h4>4.1 The Masoretic Witness</h4>
<p>The MT emphasizes the <strong>divine identity</strong> of the child: He is <em>Mighty God</em> (<em>El Gibbor</em>) and <em>Prince of Peace</em> (<em>Śar Šālôm</em>). These titles exceed what would be expected of any mere Davidic king.</p>
<h4>4.2 The Septuagint Witness</h4>
<p>The LXX’s <em>“Messenger of Great Counsel”</em> is equally messianic. In biblical theology, God’s “counsel” refers to His eternal redemptive plan (cf. Isaiah 46:10; Ephesians 1:11). To be the “Messenger of Great Counsel” is to be the agent of God’s salvific will, fulfilled supremely in Christ as the <em>Logos</em> (John 1:1, 14).</p>
<h4>4.3 Fulfillment in Christ</h4>
<p>The New Testament directly applies Isaiah’s prophecy to Jesus:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p><em>“He will be great and will be called Son of the Most High, and the Lord God will give Him the throne of His father David, and He will reign… forever.”</em> (Luke 1:32–33)</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><em>“For unto you is born this day… a Savior, who is Christ the Lord.”</em> (Luke 2:11)</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>Both textual traditions—MT and LXX—find their climax in Christ, the divine King whose rule brings peace without end.</p>
<hr/>
<h3>5. Critique of the Islamic Narrative</h3>
<p>The Islamic charge of corruption fails on multiple grounds:</p>
<ol>
<li>
<p><strong>Historical</strong> – The MT and DSS predate both Christianity and Islam; divine titles existed in the Jewish text long before Jesus.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Textual</strong> – Variants in LXX and MT reflect translation strategy, not manipulation.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Theological</strong> – Both traditions affirm a messianic figure whose reign is eternal, incompatible with Islamic denials of Jesus’ divine kingship.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Comparative</strong> – Unlike the Bible, the Quran lacks early manuscript diversity, making Islam’s textual history more fragile by comparison.<sup><a aria-describedby="footnote-label" data-footnote-ref="" href="https://chatgpt.com/c/68d48f10-bde0-832c-92e8-b08fb0af7d64#user-content-fn-3" id="user-content-fnref-3">3</a></sup></p>
</li>
</ol>
<hr/>
<h3>6. Conclusion</h3>
<p>Isaiah 9:6–7 provides one of the clearest testimonies to the Messiah’s divine nature and eternal rule. Far from proving corruption, the MT, DSS, and LXX together illustrate the <strong>rich textual witness</strong> of the Hebrew Bible and its messianic fulfillment in Jesus Christ.</p>
<p>The Muslim charge of interpolation collapses when examined against the weight of <strong>historical manuscripts and textual evidence</strong>. Isaiah foresaw the coming of the divine Son who would bring peace, justice, and eternal rule—a prophecy realized in the birth and reign of Jesus Christ, centuries before Muhammad appeared.</p>

# Helel, Lucifer, and Halal: A Philological and Theological Inquiry into Claims of Linguistic Relationship

<p><strong><em>Helel, Lucifer, and Halal: A Philological and Theological Inquiry into Claims of Linguistic Relationship</em></strong></p><p>
<strong>By Dr. Maxwell Shimba, Shimba Theological Institute</strong></p>
<hr/>
<h2><strong>Abstract</strong></h2>
<p>In recent years, some polemical discussions have attempted to draw a connection between the Hebrew term <em>hêlēl</em> (הֵילֵל) in Isaiah 14:12—later rendered as <em>Lucifer</em> in Latin—and the Arabic term <em>halāl</em> (حلال), which in Islam denotes what is lawful, particularly with reference to food and ritual slaughter. This article offers a critical, academic examination of these claims by exploring the linguistic, historical, and theological foundations of each term. Although superficial phonetic similarities may prompt comparison, the evidence demonstrates that these words arise from entirely different linguistic roots, semantic fields, and historical contexts. Nevertheless, this article investigates <strong>why</strong> such comparisons emerge, <strong>what assumptions they rely on</strong>, and <strong>how such claims function within interreligious debates</strong>.</p>
<hr/>
<h2><strong>1. Introduction</strong></h2>
<p>The idea that <em>halal</em>—Islamic ritual slaughter—derives from, or is conceptually related to, the Hebrew <em>hêlēl</em>, the word underlying the Latin <em>Lucifer</em>, appears frequently in polemical religious discourse. Proponents suggest that because the words sound alike, they share a common origin or meaning. Some even argue that halal practices are therefore connected to worship of a figure associated with evil.</p>
<p>From the standpoint of Hebrew, Arabic, philology, and biblical studies, this claim requires dispassionate academic scrutiny. The goal of this article is <strong>not</strong> to defend Islamic practice, nor to promote Christian polemics, but to analyze the linguistic and theological foundations of the claim and clarify what the biblical and Semitic data actually allow us to conclude.</p>
<hr/>
<h2><strong>2. The Hebrew Term <em>Helel</em> (הֵילֵל) in Isaiah 14:12</strong></h2>
<h3><strong>2.1 Meaning and Context</strong></h3>
<p>The word <em>hêlēl</em> appears only once in the Hebrew Bible (Isaiah 14:12). It derives from the root <strong>hll (הלל)</strong>, which carries the meaning <em>to shine</em>. The phrase <em>hêlēl ben shaḥar</em> translates to <strong>“shining one, son of the dawn.”</strong></p>
<h3><strong>2.2 Literary Purpose in Isaiah</strong></h3>
<p>Isaiah 14 is a poetic taunt against the king of Babylon. The prophet mocks the king’s pride by comparing his arrogance to that of the morning star rising in the sky—only to be cast down.</p>
<h3><strong>2.3 Later Christian Interpretation</strong></h3>
<p>Because the Latin Vulgate translated <em>hêlēl</em> as <strong>Lucifer</strong> (“light-bringer”), later Christian tradition associated this fall-from-heaven imagery with Satan. But in its original Hebrew context, the term referred metaphorically to a human king, not a supernatural being.</p>
<hr/>
<h2><strong>3. The Arabic Term <em>Halal</em> (حلال)</strong></h2>
<h3><strong>3.1 Meaning and Usage</strong></h3>
<p><em>Halāl</em> in Arabic means <strong>“lawful, permissible”</strong> according to Islamic law. It derives from the Semitic root <strong>ḥ-l-l (حلل)</strong>, meaning <em>to untie, to make lawful, to permit.</em> In Islamic jurisprudence, it refers to:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>lawful food</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>lawful conduct</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>lawful economic transactions</p>
</li>
</ul>
<h3><strong>3.2 Ritual Slaughter</strong></h3>
<p><em>Dhabīḥah</em>, the Islamic method of slaughter, is commonly described as “halal meat.” The term <em>halal</em> does <strong>not</strong> originally mean sacrifice; it refers to permissibility.</p>
<hr/>
<h2>**4. <strong>Linguistic Comparison: <em>Helel</em> and <em>Halal</em></strong></h2>
<h3><strong>4.1 Different Root Letters</strong></h3>
<p>Despite superficial similarity in English transliteration, the words come from <strong>entirely different roots</strong>:</p>
<table>
<thead>
<tr>
<th>Word</th>
<th>Language</th>
<th>Root</th>

<td><strong>h-l-l (הלל)</strong></td>

<p>Hebrew <strong>h</strong> (ה) is not the Arabic <strong>ḥ</strong> (ح), which is a deep pharyngeal sound. The roots differ in meaning and structure.</p>
<p>These two words are <strong>not</strong> cognates, nor historically related.</p>
<h3><strong>4.2 Semitic Languages and “False Friends”</strong></h3>
<p>Semitic languages often produce words that look similar but are unrelated. These are called <strong>false cognates</strong>. Examples:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Hebrew <em>kadosh</em> (holy) vs. Arabic <em>qadash</em> (to curse)</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Hebrew <em>melek</em> (king) vs. Arabic <em>malak</em> (angel)</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>Thus, similarity in sound does not establish a linguistic relationship.</p>
<hr/>
<h2><strong>5. Why Then Do Some Suggest a Connection?</strong></h2>
<p>Even though the linguistic evidence is clear, the comparison persists. Several factors explain this:</p>
<h3><strong>5.1 Phonetic Similarity in English Translation</strong></h3>
<p>When Hebrew הֵילֵל (<em>hêlēl</em>) is transliterated as “helel” and Arabic حلال (<em>halāl</em>) is transliterated as “halal,” the spellings look similar. This resemblance disappears when the original scripts and phonetics are considered.</p>
<h3><strong>5.2 Confusion Between Hebrew <em>halal</em> (הלל – “to praise”) and Arabic <em>halal</em> (حلال – “lawful”)</strong></h3>
<p>A separate Hebrew root <em>halal</em> (הלל), meaning <strong>“to praise”</strong>, is unrelated to Arabic <em>halāl</em> (“lawful”), but their English spellings create confusion.</p>
<h3><strong>5.3 Theological Interpretation vs. Linguistic Evidence</strong></h3>
<p>Some Christians argue:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>If <em>Lucifer</em> (Helel) is a fallen angel,</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>and if “halal food” is ritual slaughter,</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>then the similarity in sound might suggest spiritual connection.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>Academically, this falls under <strong>the fallacy of etymological association</strong>—the assumption that similar-sounding words imply shared origin or spiritual meaning.</p>
<hr/>
<h2><strong>6. Theological Evaluation of the Claim</strong></h2>
<p>Even if one rejects Islamic theology, no biblical, historical, or linguistic evidence supports the claim that:</p>
<blockquote>
<p><strong>“Halal comes from helel, therefore halal food is linked to Lucifer.”</strong></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><strong>6.1 Scriptural Silence</strong></h3>
<p>Neither the Bible nor Jewish tradition connects <em>helel</em> with food sacrifice.<br/>
Neither the Qur’an nor Islamic tradition connects <em>halal</em> with heavenly beings.</p>
<h3><strong>6.2 Historical Development</strong></h3>
<p>The term <em>halāl</em> predates Islam and is found in early Arabic usage meaning “lawful,” unrelated to celestial terminology.</p>
<h3><strong>6.3 Logical Implication</strong></h3>
<p>If phonetic similarity were enough to establish theological meaning, then:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>English <em>sun</em> and <em>son</em></p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Hebrew <em>’or</em> (light) and Arabic <em>’awr</em> (shame)</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Latin <em>malum</em> (apple) and <em>malum</em> (evil)</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>Would also be considered spiritually connected. They are not.</p>
<hr/>
<h2><strong>7. Academic Conclusion</strong></h2>
<p>This study demonstrates:</p>
<ol>
<li>
<p><strong>Helel</strong> (הֵילֵל) in Hebrew means <em>“shining one”</em>, referring poetically to the king of Babylon.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Lucifer</strong> is a later Latin interpretive rendering, not the original Hebrew meaning.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Halal</strong> (حلال) in Arabic means <em>“lawful/permitted”</em> and has <strong>no etymological connection</strong> to <em>hêlēl</em>.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Any proposed theological relationship is <strong>non-linguistic</strong>, based on modern polemical interpretation rather than historical or philological evidence.</p>
</li>
</ol>
<p>Thus, claims that Islamic <em>halal</em> derives from Hebrew <em>hêlēl</em> (Lucifer) are <strong>linguistically unfounded</strong>, <strong>historically unsustainable</strong>, and <strong>theologically constructed rather than textually supported</strong>.</p>
<p>Nevertheless, such claims <strong>should be studied academically</strong> because they reveal how language, religion, and polemics interact in contemporary debates.</p>

Introduction</h3><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-end="2636" data-start="2091" data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;">The rise of Islam in the 7th century introduced a new prophetic claim: Muhammad as the “seal of the prophets” and bearer of the final revelation. However, the Qur’an and Hadith preserve accounts that Muhammad not only encountered but also converted <em data-end="2347" data-start="2340" style="box-sizing: border-box;">jinns</em> (spiritual beings in Islamic cosmology). This claim presents a theological dilemma when evaluated against the biblical record. In Scripture, God’s prophets never engaged in dialogue with unclean spirits—rather, they consistently cast them out and demonstrated God’s authority over them.</p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-end="2891" data-start="2638" data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;">The Qur’an’s portrayal of Muhammad as a prophet to both humans and jinn, therefore, raises fundamental questions: Is this consistent with the God of the Bible? Or does it reveal that Muhammad’s experiences originated from a different spiritual source?</p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-end="3112" data-start="2893" data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;">This paper will critically examine Muhammad’s interaction with jinns, contrast it with biblical revelation, and argue that such encounters undermine the claim of Muhammad’s legitimacy as a prophet of the one true God.</p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><hr data-end="3117" data-start="3114" style="border-bottom: none; border-image: initial; border-left: none; border-right: none; border-top-color: rgb(221, 226, 236); border-top-style: solid; box-sizing: content-box; clear: both; height: 0px; overflow: visible;"/><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; clear: both; font-size: 18px; margin: 0.5em 0px;">2. Understanding the Concept of Jinn in Islam</h3><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><h4 style="box-sizing: border-box; clear: both; font-size: 16px; margin: 0.5em 0px;">Jinn in Pre-Islamic Arabia</h4><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-end="3457" data-start="3204" data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;">Before Islam, Arabs believed in jinn as invisible beings inhabiting deserts and ruins. They were associated with soothsayers, poets, and occult practices. Muhammad’s contemporaries were familiar with them as spirits capable of possession and mischief.</p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><h4 style="box-sizing: border-box; clear: both; font-size: 16px; margin: 0.5em 0px;">Qur’anic Descriptions of Jinn</h4><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-end="3901" data-start="3495" data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;">The Qur’an repeatedly mentions jinns (over 30 times). They are described as created from smokeless fire (Surah 55:15), having free will, and divided into believers and unbelievers (Surah 72:11–15). This dual capacity aligns them closer to humans than to the biblical concept of demons, yet their functions—deception, haunting, and whispering evil (Surah 114:4–6)—parallel the role of demons in Scripture.</p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><h4 style="box-sizing: border-box; clear: both; font-size: 16px; margin: 0.5em 0px;">Jinn in Hadith and Tradition</h4><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-end="4244" data-start="3938" data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;">Hadith literature affirms Muhammad’s interaction with jinns. Reports narrate how jinns listened to the Qur’an, converted to Islam, and even asked Muhammad for religious instruction (Sahih al-Bukhari 731; Sahih Muslim 450). The famous <em data-end="4187" data-start="4172" style="box-sizing: border-box;">Laylatul Jinn</em> describes Muhammad spending an entire night with them.</p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-end="4433" data-start="4246" data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;">These sources elevate Muhammad not only as prophet of mankind but also of the jinn—earning him the title <em data-end="4377" data-start="4351" style="box-sizing: border-box;">Mab’uth ila al-Thaqalayn</em> (“Sent to the two heavy creatures: humans and jinn”).</p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><hr data-end="4438" data-start="4435" style="border-bottom: none; border-image: initial; border-left: none; border-right: none; border-top-color: rgb(221, 226, 236); border-top-style: solid; box-sizing: content-box; clear: both; height: 0px; overflow: visible;"/><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; clear: both; font-size: 18px; margin: 0.5em 0px;">3. clear: both; font-size: 16px; margin: 0.5em 0px;">Old Testament Condemnation of Spiritism</h4><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-end="4597" data-start="4543" data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; He did not negotiate with them but commanded them to leave (Mark 1:25; Luke 8:29). The demons recognized Him as the Son of God but remained under condemnation.</p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><h4 style="box-sizing: border-box; clear: both; font-size: 16px; margin: 0.5em 0px;">Apostolic Ministry and Deliverance</h4><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-end="5306" data-start="5151" data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;">The apostles followed Christ’s example by casting out spirits in His name (Acts 16:18; Acts 19:11–12). Nowhere do they invite demons into God’s covenant.</p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-end="5465" data-start="5308" data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><span data-end="5322" data-start="5308" style="box-sizing: border-box; font-weight: 700;">Key point:</span> In biblical theology, demons are irredeemable, destined for eternal fire (Matthew 25:41). Salvation is for humanity, not for fallen spirits.</p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><hr data-end="5470" data-start="5467" style="border-bottom: none; border-image: initial; border-left: none; border-right: none; border-top-color: rgb(221, 226, 236); border-top-style: solid; box-sizing: content-box; clear: both; height: 0px; overflow: visible;"/><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; clear: both; font-size: 18px; margin: 0.5em 0px;">4. clear: both; font-size: 22px; margin: 0.5em 0px;">5. Theological Contradictions with the Bible</h2><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-end="372" data-start="260" data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;">Muhammad’s interaction with jinns creates sharp theological dissonance when compared with biblical revelation.</p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; clear: both; font-size: 18px; margin: 0.5em 0px;">Dialogue vs. Exorcism</h3><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-end="877" data-start="402" data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;">The Qur’an describes Muhammad listening to jinns, allowing them to speak, and even affirming their declarations of faith (Surah al-Jinn 72:1–2). Conversely, biblical prophets—especially Jesus Christ—never entertained dialogue with unclean spirits. When demons spoke in the Gospels, Christ silenced them immediately (Mark 1:25; Luke 4:35). The biblical model reveals that allowing spirits to speak is a violation of divine authority. Dialogue is always replaced by exorcism.</p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-end="997" data-start="879" data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><span data-end="892" data-start="879" style="box-sizing: border-box; font-weight: 700;">Question:</span> Why did Muhammad allow spirits to speak freely instead of silencing or casting them out as Christ did?</p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; clear: both; font-size: 18px; margin: 0.5em 0px;">Fellowship vs. Separation from Spirits</h3><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-end="1272" data-start="1044" data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;">The Qur’an presents jinns as capable of entering fellowship with believers, joining the covenant community, and even evangelizing other jinns (Surah al-Ahqaf 46:29–32). This is in stark contradiction with the biblical command:</p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><blockquote data-end="1424" data-start="1273" style="background: rgb(218, 238, 229); border-bottom-left-radius: 10px; border-color: rgb(237, 240, 245) rgb(237, 240, 245) rgb(237, 240, 245) rgb(195, 216, 228); border-image: initial; border-style: solid; border-top-left-radius: 10px; border-width: 1px 1px 1px 6px; box-sizing: border-box; display: flow-root; font-size: 13px; margin: 0.5em 0px; padding: 12px 14px;"><p data-end="1424" data-start="1275" data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;">“Do not be unequally yoked with unbelievers… what fellowship has light with darkness? Yet, the Bible is clear: demons are eternally condemned. Jesus declares that the lake of fire was prepared “for the devil and his angels” (Matthew 25:41). Revelation 20:10 confirms their final destruction.</p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-end="2196" data-start="2019" data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;">If Muhammad preached salvation for spirits that God has eternally condemned, then Islam introduces a theological impossibility: redemption for fallen beings beyond redemption.</p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-end="2290" data-start="2198" data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><span data-end="2211" data-start="2198" style="box-sizing: border-box; font-weight: 700;">Question:</span> Did Muhammad contradict God’s eternal decree by offering salvation to jinns?</p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><hr data-end="2295" data-start="2292" style="border-bottom: none; border-image: initial; border-left: none; border-right: none; border-top-color: rgb(221, 226, 236); border-top-style: solid; box-sizing: content-box; clear: both; height: 0px; overflow: visible;"/><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><h2 style="box-sizing: border-box; clear: both; font-size: 22px; margin: 0.5em 0px;">6. Questioning the Source of Muhammad’s Revelation</h2><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-end="2507" data-start="2354" data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; font-weight: 700;">What was the true source of Muhammad’s revelation?</span></p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; clear: both; font-size: 18px; margin: 0.5em 0px;">Possible Demonic Deception</h3><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-end="2577" data-start="2542" data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;">Paul warns the Corinthian church:</p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><blockquote data-end="2653" data-start="2578" style="background: rgb(218, 238, 229); margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;">If Satan can disguise himself as an angel, how much more could he disguise himself as “jinns” affirming a false message? Rather than evidence of divine truth, Muhammad’s encounters could be viewed as demonic deception designed to establish a false religion.</p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-end="3070" data-start="2916" data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><span data-end="2929" data-start="2916" style="box-sizing: border-box; font-weight: 700;">Question:</span> If Satan deceives through appearances of light, could the so-called “jinns” Muhammad converted have been demons masquerading as believers?</p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; clear: both; font-size: 18px; margin: 0.5em 0px;">The Problem of Contradictory Revelation</h3><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-end="3437" data-start="3118" data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;">The Qur’an claims continuity with the Torah and Gospel (Surah 5:46; Surah 3:3). Yet, it denies the crucifixion (Surah 4:157), rejects the Sonship of Christ (Surah 19:35), and allows fellowship with spirits. These contradictions reveal that the Qur’an cannot come from the same God who authored the earlier Scriptures.</p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-end="3566" data-start="3439" data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><span data-end="3452" data-start="3439" style="box-sizing: border-box; font-weight: 700;">Question:</span> How can Allah claim to confirm the Torah and Gospel while simultaneously contradicting their central doctrines?</p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; clear: both; font-size: 18px; margin: 0.5em 0px;">Muhammad’s Early Experiences of “Possession”</h3><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-end="4017" data-start="3619" data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;">Historical sources—including Islamic traditions—acknowledge that Muhammad initially feared he was demon-possessed. Sahih al-Bukhari records that he contemplated suicide after his first revelations because he thought he had been afflicted by a <em data-end="3868" data-start="3862" style="box-sizing: border-box;">jinn</em> or demon (Bukhari 6982). His experiences included convulsions, trembling, and auditory hallucinations—phenomena consistent with spirit possession.</p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-end="4199" data-start="4019" data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><span data-end="4032" data-start="4019" style="box-sizing: border-box; font-weight: 700;">Question:</span> If Muhammad himself feared demonic possession at the beginning, should later encounters with jinn be interpreted as divine or as confirmation of his earliest fears?</p><h2 id="isPasted" style="box-sizing: border-box; clear: both; font-size: 22px; margin: 0.5em 0px;">7. Does Allah Equal the God of the Bible?</h2><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-end="616" data-start="267" data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;">One of the most pressing theological questions raised by Muhammad’s interaction with jinns is the identity of <em data-end="384" data-start="377" style="box-sizing: border-box;">Allah</em>. Muslims insist that Allah is the same God worshiped by Abraham, Moses, and Jesus. However, the Qur’an’s teachings about Allah diverge so radically from the Bible’s testimony of Yahweh that such a claim cannot withstand scrutiny.</p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; clear: both; font-size: 18px; margin: 0.5em 0px;">7.1 The Incompatibility of Allah’s Nature with Yahweh</h3><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-end="1015" data-start="678" data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;">The God of the Bible reveals Himself as holy, transcendent, and utterly separate from all forms of darkness. He repeatedly warns His people to avoid mediums, necromancers, and interaction with spirits (Deuteronomy 18:10–12; Isaiah 8:19–20). Yahweh cannot contradict His own holiness by later permitting fellowship with spirits (jinns).</p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-end="1287" data-start="1017" data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;">Allah, however, not only permits such fellowship but also affirms that some jinns can be Muslims and part of his covenant community (Surah al-Jinn 72:11–14). This accommodation of spirits marks a fundamental difference in divine nature: Yahweh rejects, Allah embraces.</p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-end="1416" data-start="1289" data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><span data-end="1311" data-start="1289" style="box-sizing: border-box; font-weight: 700;">Critical Question:</span> If Allah embraces jinns while Yahweh condemns all dealings with spirits, how can they be the same God?</p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; clear: both; font-size: 18px; margin: 0.5em 0px;">7.2 Qur’an’s Rejection of Crucifixion and Sonship</h3><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-end="1779" data-start="1474" data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;">The greatest divergence between Allah and Yahweh is found in Christology. The Bible testifies that Jesus is the eternal Son of God, crucified for the sins of the world and raised to life (John 3:16; Romans 10:9; 1 Corinthians 15:3–4). The crucifixion and resurrection are the core of the gospel message.</p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-end="1821" data-start="1781" data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;">Yet the Qur’an explicitly denies both:</p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><ul data-end="2034" data-start="1822" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><span data-end="1850" data-start="1824" style="box-sizing: border-box; font-weight: 700;">Denial of Crucifixion:</span> “They did not kill him, nor crucify him, but it was made to appear so to them” (Surah 4:157).</p></li><li data-end="2034" data-start="1947" style="box-sizing: border-box;"><p data-end="2034" data-start="1949" data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-end="2143" data-start="2036" data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;">A god who denies the central act of salvation cannot be equated with the God who provided that salvation.</p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-end="2283" data-start="2145" data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><span data-end="2167" data-start="2145" style="box-sizing: border-box; font-weight: 700;">Critical Question:</span> If Allah denies the crucifixion and resurrection, is he not rejecting the very foundation of biblical redemption?</p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><h3 style="box-sizing: border-box; The Torah condemns communication with spirits. The Gospels declare Jesus as the Son of God and affirm His death and resurrection. The Qur’an both permits spirit fellowship and denies the gospel.</p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-end="3088" data-start="2975" data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;">This inconsistency reveals that the Qur’an’s claim to continuity is false. Rather than confirming, it corrupts.</p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><p data-end="3233" data-start="3090" data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><span data-end="3112" data-start="3090" style="box-sizing: border-box; font-weight: 700;">Critical Question:</span> If the Qur’an contradicts the Torah and Gospel on their most essential truths, how can Allah claim to be their author?</p><p data-xf-p="1" style="box-sizing: border-box; margin-bottom: 0px; margin-top: 0px;"><br style="box-sizing: border-box;"/></p><h2 id="isPasted" style="box-sizing: border-box; clear: both; font-size: 22px; margin: 0.5em 0px;">8.

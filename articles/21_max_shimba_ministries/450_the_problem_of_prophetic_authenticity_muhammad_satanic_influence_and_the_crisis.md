# The Problem of Prophetic Authenticity: Muhammad, Satanic Influence, and the Crisis of Revelation

<h2>By Dr. Maxwell Shimba, Shimba Theological Institute</h2>
<hr/>
<h4><strong>Introduction</strong></h4>
<p>The question of prophetic authenticity is central to the legitimacy of any religious movement. In the Islamic tradition, Muhammad is upheld as the "Seal of the Prophets" (Qur’an 33:40), and his revelation is considered final and infallible by the majority of Muslims. Yet, critical scholarship and Islamic historical sources themselves reveal troubling episodes that cast doubt upon this claim—most notably, the so-called "Satanic Verses" incident. This article examines the theological, historical, and logical implications of Muhammad’s confessed encounters with Satanic influence, using Islamic sources and academic analysis to challenge the foundation of his prophethood.</p>
<hr/>
<h4><strong>1. The "Satanic Verses" and Muhammad’s Confession</strong></h4>
<p>The "Satanic Verses" episode is attested in multiple early Islamic sources, including <strong>al-Tabari’s <em>Tarikh</em> (History), Volume 6:111</strong>, as referenced in the image above. According to these accounts, Muhammad allegedly recited verses acknowledging pagan deities (al-Lat, al-Uzza, and Manat) as "exalted cranes whose intercession is to be hoped for" (see al-Tabari 6:107-111; Ibn Ishaq, <em>Sirat Rasul Allah</em>, p. 165). Later, Muhammad claimed that these verses were not from God but were "whispered" to him by Satan.</p>
<blockquote>
<p>“I have fabricated things against God and have imputed to Him words which He has not spoken.”<br/>
— Muhammad, <em>al-Tabari 6:111</em></p>
</blockquote>
<p>This confession raises immediate concerns: <strong>If Muhammad, the supposed messenger of Allah, could be deceived by Satan, how many other revelations may have been similarly compromised?</strong> Furthermore, how can followers be confident in the authenticity of any revelation Muhammad delivered?</p>
<hr/>
<h4><strong>2. Theological Problems: Can God’s Prophet Be Deceived?</strong></h4>
<p>The Qur’an itself asserts that God's messengers are protected from Satanic influence:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p><em>“And We did not send before you any messenger or prophet except that when he spoke [or recited], Satan threw into it [some misunderstanding]. But Allah abolishes that which Satan throws in; then Allah makes precise His verses. And Allah is Knowing and Wise.”</em> (Qur’an 22:52)</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>Ironically, this verse admits the possibility that Satan can, at least temporarily, influence even God's chosen messengers. This stands in stark contrast to the Biblical standard for prophets, where <strong>false prophecy is a sign of disqualification</strong> (Deuteronomy 18:20-22).</p>
<h4><strong>a. Did Muhammad Receive Other Verses from Satan?</strong></h4>
<p>If the most significant Qur’anic surahs were vulnerable to corruption by Satanic suggestion, how can the Islamic community trust that other verses have not been similarly compromised? The lack of a mechanism for verifying the divine origin of each revelation, beyond Muhammad's own assertion, makes the entire corpus of the Qur’an open to suspicion.</p>
<h4><strong>b. Why Did Allah Not Prevent This?</strong></h4>
<p>If Allah is truly all-powerful and all-knowing, why did he allow his final prophet to be so easily deceived by Satan? Should divine omnipotence not guarantee the protection of revelation? The fact that Allah only "abolishes" the satanic verses <strong>after</strong> their recitation does not inspire confidence in the infallibility of the process.</p>
<hr/>
<h4><strong>3. The Confusion Between Gabriel and Satan: A Tragic Dilemma</strong></h4>
<p>One of the greatest ironies is Muhammad’s failure to distinguish between Jibreel (Gabriel), the messenger of God, and Satan, the enemy of God. If both can appear and communicate in similar ways, how can the prophet—and by extension, his followers—have any assurance of the source of revelation? This confusion undermines the credibility of all subsequent Islamic teachings.</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p><strong>Did Jibreel and Satan look alike to Muhammad?</strong></p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Why did Muhammad not test the spirits as instructed in Biblical tradition?</strong> (1 John 4:1)</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>The episode reveals a <strong>theological crisis</strong>: If even the prophet cannot reliably distinguish between divine and demonic communication, no Muslim can be certain of the Qur’an’s authenticity.</p>

<hr/>
<h4><strong>5. Conclusion: A Tragedy for Islamic Theology</strong></h4>
<p>The admission—found within Islam’s own canonical sources—that Muhammad could (and did) speak under the influence of Satan, fabricating words in the name of God, is a <strong>fatal blow to the credibility of his prophethood</strong>. If Satan could infiltrate divine revelation once, how can the entire Qur’an or hadith corpus be trusted? Why did Allah, purportedly all-powerful, not intervene until after the event?</p>
<p>This crisis remains unresolved. The integrity of Islamic revelation is thus <strong>irreparably undermined</strong> by its own historical tradition—a great tragedy for those seeking certainty in the words attributed to God.</p>

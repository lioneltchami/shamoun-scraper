<h1><strong>THE KISSING OF THE BLACK STONE IN ISLAM: A THEOLOGICAL AND HISTORICAL EXPOSITION OF PAGAN CONTINUITY</strong></h1>
<h3><em>By Dr. Maxwell Shimba</em></h3>
<p><em>Shimba Theological Institute</em></p>
<hr/>
<h2><strong>Abstract</strong></h2>
<p>This article evaluates the Islamic practice of <strong>kissing, touching, or venerating the Black Stone (Hajar al-Aswad)</strong> located in the Kaaba. Through historical analysis, hadith literature, Qur’anic silence, and comparative studies of Near Eastern paganism, this study demonstrates that the Black Stone ritual is <strong>not monotheistic</strong>, lacks divine sanction, and is an uninterrupted survival of <em>pre-Islamic Arabian paganism</em>. By examining the prophetic statements attributed to Muhammad—particularly the hadith describing the Black Stone as a creature that will have <strong>eyes, a tongue, and speak on the Day of Judgment</strong>—this article raises essential theological questions about the nature of worship, idolatry, and divine consistency.</p>
<hr/>
<h1><strong>1. Introduction</strong></h1>
<p>Among all Islamic rituals, none is more strikingly contradictory to monotheistic claims than the <strong>kissing of the Black Stone</strong> during the Hajj. Millions of Muslims annually struggle—sometimes violently—to touch, stroke, or kiss a <strong>physical object</strong> believed to possess spiritual power.</p>
<p>Yet, neither the <strong>Torah</strong>, nor the <strong>Psalms</strong>, nor the <strong>Gospel</strong>, nor any previous prophetic Scriptures ever command the veneration of stones. Rather, the Bible explicitly condemns such acts as <strong>pagan idolatry</strong> (Deut. 16:21; Lev. 26:1; 2 Kings 23:10).</p>
<p>Islamic tradition, however, preserves rituals inherited directly from <strong>Arabian pagan worship</strong> of sacred stones, trees, and idols. Even the Qur’an acknowledges that the Kaaba and its rites existed <strong>before Islam</strong> (Qur’an 8:35; 106:1–4), when pagans circled the Kaaba naked, blowing whistles and clapping as religious rites.</p>
<p>Thus, the question arises: <em>Why does Islam retain a central ritual rooted in the very pagan practices it claims to abolish?</em></p>
<hr/>
<h1><strong>2. The Hadith Evidence: The Black Stone Will Have Eyes and a Tongue</strong></h1>
<p>The following hadith—graded <em>hasan</em> (good) by Imam al-Tirmidhi—states:</p>
<blockquote>
<p><strong>Ibn Abbas narrated:</strong><br/>
“The Messenger of Allah said about the Black Stone:<br/>
<em>‘By Allah! Allah will raise it on the Day of Resurrection with two eyes by which it sees and a tongue that it speaks with, testifying to whoever touched it in truth.’</em>”<br/>
— <em>Jami‘ at-Tirmidhi 961</em></p>
</blockquote>
<p>This narration raises profound theological contradictions:</p>
<ol>
<li>
<p><strong>A stone will have eyes and a tongue.</strong><br/>
This contradicts Islamic claims that objects are not divine, conscious, or living.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>The stone will testify for or against worshippers.</strong><br/>
This attribute resembles <strong>pagan divine intermediaries</strong>, not monotheistic purity.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Touching a physical object earns spiritual merit.</strong><br/>
This concept is fundamentally foreign to the monotheism claimed in Islam.</p>
</li>
</ol>
<p>The hadith therefore makes the Stone a <strong>supernatural creature</strong>, endowed with consciousness and eschatological authority—functionally similar to idols in other pagan religions.</p>
<hr/>
<h1><strong>3. Historical Origins: The Pagan Roots of Stone Worship in Arabia</strong></h1>
<p>Before Islam, the Arabs widely practiced <strong>stone-veneration</strong>:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Tribes kept <strong>stone idols</strong> (e.g., al-Lat, al-Uzza, Manat).</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Many tribes carried portable stones from the Kaaba to serve as household gods.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Pilgrims venerated stones, circled shrines, and performed kissing rituals.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>According to early Islamic historians (Ibn Ishaq, al-Tabari):</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p><strong>The Black Stone was part of pre-Islamic idol rituals.</strong></p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The Quraysh performed <strong>tawaf</strong> long before Islam.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Muhammad himself participated in pagan rituals before declaring prophethood.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>Thus, the Black Stone ritual predates Islam by centuries.</p>

<p>Critically:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p><strong>The Qur’an never commands Muslims to kiss the Black Stone.</strong></p>
</li>
<li>
<p>It never states that the stone has spiritual power.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>It never says the stone is from God.</p>
</li>
<li>

</li>
</ul>
<p>This exposes a deep inconsistency:<br/>
<strong>If the Black Stone were essential to Islamic faith, why is it absent from the Qur’an?</strong></p>
<p>All authority for the ritual comes from <strong>hadith</strong>, not Scripture.</p>

<h1><strong>5. Biblical and Theological Critique: Stone Veneration as Paganism</strong></h1>
<p>The Bible clearly condemns the veneration of stones:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p><strong>Leviticus 26:1</strong> – “Do not set up an image or a <em>sacred stone</em>.”</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Deuteronomy 16:22</strong> – “Do not erect a sacred stone, for the Lord your God hates them.”</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Isaiah 44:9–20</strong> mocks those who worship objects made of created matter.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>True monotheism forbids:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>attributing supernatural powers to objects,</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>believing a stone can intercede,</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>performing rituals to earn divine favor through a physical object.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>Therefore, kissing a stone is not merely unnecessary—it is <strong>theologically incompatible</strong> with the monotheism of previous prophets.</p>

<h1>The Anthropomorphic Dilemma: Allah’s “Shin” and the Question of Divine Uniqueness</h1>
<p><em>By Dr. Maxwell Shimba, Shimba Theological Institute</em></p>
<h2>Introduction</h2>
<p>One of the most complex theological tensions in Islamic theology lies in the doctrine of <em>tanzīh</em>—the absolute transcendence of Allah—contrasted with anthropomorphic descriptions of Him found in the Qur’an and Hadith literature. Muslims are taught that Allah is utterly unlike His creation: <em>“There is nothing like unto Him”</em> (Qur’an 42:11). Yet, paradoxically, several Qur’anic verses and Hadith traditions attribute to Allah physical attributes and organs, such as hands (Qur’an 38:75), a face (Qur’an 55:27), eyes (Qur’an 11:37), and even a shin (Qur’an 68:42). This raises a profound theological question: if Allah possesses what is described as a “shin,” how can He simultaneously claim to be wholly unlike human beings, who also possess shins?</p>
<p>This article critically examines this contradiction, placing the Qur’anic notion of Allah’s “shin” under scrutiny while contrasting it with classical Islamic theology, Jewish and Christian theological anthropology, and logical-philosophical inquiry.</p>
<hr/>
<h2>The Qur’anic Description of the “Shin”</h2>
<p>The critical verse is found in <em>Surah al-Qalam</em> (68:42):</p>
<blockquote>
<p><em>“[Remember] the Day when the shin will be uncovered, and they will be invited to prostration but they will not be able.”</em></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Classical commentators (e.g., al-Tabari, Ibn Kathir, al-Qurtubi) debated whether the “shin” (<em>sāq</em>) in this passage refers literally to an organ of Allah or metaphorically to a sign of severe distress. Some schools of thought, particularly the Athari and early Hanbali traditions, insisted on affirming the attribute <em>bi-lā kayf</em> (“without asking how”), meaning that Allah does indeed have a shin but it is unlike the human shin. Ashʿarī and Māturīdī theologians, however, leaned toward metaphorical interpretations, claiming it was an Arabic idiom for hardship.</p>
<p>Yet, regardless of interpretation, the text introduces anthropomorphic imagery, which cannot easily be reconciled with the absolute claim of divine incomparability.</p>
<hr/>
<h2>The Problem of Anthropomorphism</h2>
<p>The central contradiction lies in the following tension:</p>
<ol>
<li>
<p><strong>Allah declares absolute dissimilarity from creation</strong> (Qur’an 42:11).</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Allah is described with human organs</strong> (hands, eyes, face, shin).</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Human beings also possess these very organs.</strong></p>
</li>
</ol>
<p>If Allah’s shin is literal, then He shares a common attribute with humanity, thereby undermining His transcendence. If it is metaphorical, then why employ anthropomorphic language at all, when such language inevitably leads to confusion, anthropomorphism, and internal contradiction?</p>
<p>Christian theology faced similar tensions in Old Testament anthropomorphisms (e.g., “the hand of God”), but these were ultimately resolved in the doctrine of the Incarnation: God took on human form in Jesus Christ while remaining fully divine. Islam, however, denies incarnation while still attributing physical features to Allah. This results in a theological impasse: an anthropomorphic deity who is simultaneously claimed to be beyond all human likeness.</p>
<hr/>
<h2>Comparative Theological Reflection</h2>
<ul>
<li>
<p><strong>Jewish Tradition:</strong> Rabbinic interpretation often treated anthropomorphic references in the Torah as metaphorical or symbolic, pointing to God’s actions rather than His physical form.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Christian Tradition:</strong> Anthropomorphisms find their fulfillment in Christ, who is the “image of the invisible God” (Colossians 1:15). Here, divine embodiment is not a contradiction but the core of the Incarnation.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Islamic Tradition:</strong> Islamic theology vacillates between literalism (<em>bi-lā kayf</em>) and metaphorical reinterpretation (<em>ta’wīl</em>), leaving unresolved the inherent tension between transcendence and anthropomorphism.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<hr/>
<h2>Philosophical Analysis</h2>
<p>From a philosophical standpoint, the problem can be framed as follows:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p><strong>Premise 1:</strong> Allah claims absolute transcendence and dissimilarity.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Premise 2:</strong> Allah is described with human-like organs such as a shin.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Premise 3:</strong> Humans possess shins, hands, and faces.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Conclusion:</strong> Either Allah is not utterly unlike humans, or the Qur’an introduces contradictory statements.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>The principle of non-contradiction (Aristotle, <em>Metaphysics</em>, IV.3) demands consistency. If Allah’s shin is literal, He shares features with creation. If figurative, then the Qur’an’s anthropomorphic language appears misleading or incoherent, especially when taken by literalist traditions.</p>
<hr/>
<h2>Conclusion</h2>
<p>The description of Allah as possessing a “shin” epitomizes the anthropomorphic dilemma within Islamic theology. While theologians have attempted to resolve the issue either through metaphorical interpretation or the doctrine of <em>bi-lā kayf</em>, the contradiction remains: if Allah is unlike humans, why attribute to Him organs that are distinctly human? Christianity resolves this tension through the Incarnation of Jesus Christ, while Islam remains trapped in a paradox between transcendence and anthropomorphism. This inconsistency undermines the Islamic claim of a perfectly coherent doctrine of God.</p>

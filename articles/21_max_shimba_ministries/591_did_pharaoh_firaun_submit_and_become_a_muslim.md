<h3><strong>Introduction</strong></h3>
<p>Dear reader,</p>
<p>Surah Yunus (10:90–92) of the Qur'an, revealed in Mecca and translated into Swahili by Sheikh Ali Muhsin Al-Barwani, appears to suggest that Pharaoh (known in Arabic as Firaun) submitted to God and became a Muslim at the time of his death. However, upon further reading of the Qur'an, a significant theological contradiction arises—challenging the consistency of this claim.</p>
<hr/>
<h3><strong>Surah Yunus 10:90–92: Pharaoh’s Supposed Conversion</strong></h3>
<p><strong>Surah Yunus 10:90–92</strong> states:</p>
<blockquote>
<p><strong>90</strong>: “We took the Children of Israel across the sea. Pharaoh and his soldiers pursued them out of transgression and enmity. When he was about to drown, he said, <em>‘I believe that there is no deity except the One in whom the Children of Israel believe, and I am among the Muslims!’</em></p>
<p><strong>91</strong>: <em>‘Now? But you disobeyed before and were among the corrupters.’</em></p>
<p><strong>92</strong>: <em>‘So today We will preserve your body so that you may be a sign for those after you. And indeed, many among mankind are heedless of Our signs.’</em></p>
</blockquote>
<p>This passage clearly portrays Pharaoh as declaring his faith in the God of the Children of Israel and submitting at the moment of death. Many Islamic scholars, including <strong>Muhammad Asad</strong> in <em>The Message of the Qur'an</em> (Dar al-Andalus Limited, Gibraltar, 1993, p. 306, fn. 112), interpret this as Pharaoh becoming a Muslim before his death and being forgiven.</p>
<hr/>
<h3><strong>The Theological Dilemma: Contradictions in the Qur'an</strong></h3>
<p>However, this interpretation raises profound theological concerns when we compare it with other Qur'anic verses that <strong>seemingly contradict</strong> this account.</p>
<h4><strong>1. Surah An-Nisa’ 4:17–18: Toba (Repentance) Must Be Timely</strong></h4>
<blockquote>
<p><strong>17</strong>: “Verily, repentance with Allah is only for those who do evil in ignorance and then repent soon after. It is they to whom Allah will turn in forgiveness.”</p>
<p><strong>18</strong>: “But repentance is not accepted from those who continue to do evil deeds until death faces one of them and he says: <em>‘Now I repent.’</em> Nor of those who die as disbelievers; for them We have prepared a painful punishment.”</p>
</blockquote>
<p><strong>Observation</strong>:<br/>
According to these verses, repentance at the point of death—after a life of knowing rebellion—is not accepted. Pharaoh, who willfully opposed Moses and committed evil knowingly, clearly does not fit the Qur'anic criteria of <em>“those who sin in ignorance”</em>. Hence, the Qur'an itself appears to <strong>invalidate Pharaoh’s deathbed repentance</strong>.</p>
<hr/>
<h4><strong>2. Surah Ghafir 40:83–85: Repentance After Punishment Is Not Accepted</strong></h4>
<blockquote>
<p><strong>83</strong>: “When their messengers came to them with clear signs, they arrogantly disbelieved in what they had been given of knowledge...”</p>
<p><strong>84</strong>: “Then when they saw Our punishment, they said, <em>‘We believe in Allah alone and disbelieve in what we used to associate with Him.’</em>”</p>
<p><strong>85</strong>: “But their faith was of no benefit to them once they saw Our punishment. Such is Allah’s established way with His servants. And the disbelievers then were utterly lost.”</p>
</blockquote>
<p><strong>Observation</strong>:<br/>
This passage underscores that faith or repentance after witnessing divine punishment is <strong>ineffective and unacceptable</strong>. Again, this disqualifies Pharaoh’s last-minute submission, as it occurred precisely <strong>after</strong> he had seen the punishment—the drowning in the sea.</p>
<hr/>
<h3><strong>Contradiction Within the Qur'an?</strong></h3>
<p>If, according to Surah Yunus 10:90–92, Pharaoh’s repentance is preserved as a sign, but other verses such as Surah An-Nisa 4:17–18 and Surah Ghafir 40:83–85 state that repentance at the point of death or during punishment is rejected, then the Qur’an seems to be in <strong>internal contradiction</strong>. Does Allah forgive those who repent at the brink of death—or not?</p>
<p>Moreover, a serious theological paradox arises concerning Muslim funerary practices. Muslims pray for forgiveness for the dead—even though, by Qur’anic logic, the dead have already tasted death and thus, are beyond repentance (as per 4:18). How, then, is forgiveness expected?</p>
<hr/>
<h3><strong>The Call to Certainty in Jesus Christ</strong></h3>
<p>As I continued to study the Qur’an, I encountered numerous such contradictions—raising fundamental doubts about its consistency and divine authorship. Can a God who claims to be all-knowing and wise contradict Himself within His own revelation?</p>
<p>In stark contrast, the <strong>Holy Bible is clear and consistent</strong> in presenting salvation through Jesus Christ. Jesus Himself declared:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>“I am the way, the truth, and the life. No one comes to the Father except through me.”<br/>
<em>(John 14:6)</em></p>
</blockquote>
<p>And the apostles affirmed:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>“Salvation is found in no one else, for there is no other name under heaven given to mankind by which we must be saved.”<br/>
<em>(Acts 4:12)</em></p>
</blockquote>

<h2>Christian Theological Reflection: Prayer Fulfilled in Christ</h2>
<p>The contrast between the Qur’an, Hadith, and the Christian faith reveals more than a historical or textual contradiction—it demonstrates the unique sufficiency of Jesus Christ. In Islam, prayer is a ritual burden, shaped by human tradition and inconsistent revelations. In Christianity, prayer is the fruit of a reconciled relationship with God through Christ.</p>
<p>Jesus Himself modeled prayer, not as a mechanical ritual, but as intimate communion with the Father. He withdrew to pray (Luke 5:16), taught His disciples to pray simply and directly (Matthew 6:9–13), and interceded for His followers (John 17). Prayer, in the Christian vision, is not about rigid times or quantities, but about living continually in God’s presence (1 Thessalonians 5:17).</p>
<p>Moreover, through His death and resurrection, Christ opened direct access to God:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>“For there is one God and one mediator between God and men, the man Christ Jesus.”<br/>
(1 Timothy 2:5)</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Unlike Muhammad’s story—where Allah’s command had to be negotiated down through Moses—Christ is Himself the final Mediator. He fulfilled the law, including all ritual obligations, and granted believers bold access to God’s throne of grace (Hebrews 4:16).</p>
<p>Thus, prayer in Christianity is not a ritualistic duty to earn divine favor, but a privilege of children approaching their Father (Romans 8:15). The believer prays in freedom, empowered by the Holy Spirit, not compelled by burdensome regulations.</p>
<hr/>
<h2>Final Conclusion</h2>
<p>The Qur’an prescribes three prayers, while Hadith imposes five, revealing internal contradiction and the elevation of Muhammad’s authority above Allah’s. This duality undermines Islam’s claim of pure monotheism and exposes its dependence on human tradition rather than divine revelation.</p>
<p>By contrast, Christianity offers a coherent and liberating vision of prayer. In Christ, believers are freed from ritual legalism and invited into unbroken fellowship with God. Prayer becomes not a burdensome obligation negotiated between prophets and God, but a joyful response to divine love made possible by the cross.</p>
<p>Therefore, where Islam binds its followers to the conflicting commands of Qur’an and Hadith, Christ calls His people to the freedom of direct communion with God—“in spirit and in truth” (John 4:24).</p>

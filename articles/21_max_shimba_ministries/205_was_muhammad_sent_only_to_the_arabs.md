<h1><strong>Was Muhammad Sent Only to the Arabs?</strong></h1>
<p><strong>By Dr. Maxwell Shimba<br/>
Shimba Theological Institute</strong></p>
<hr/>
<h2><strong>Moderator’s Introduction</strong></h2>
<p>Today’s debate addresses a highly consequential question in Islamic theology:<br/>
<strong>Was Muhammad a universal messenger for all mankind, or was he sent exclusively to the Arabs?</strong></p>
<p>Representing <strong>the Argument for Exclusivity</strong> is <em>Scholar A</em>.<br/>
Representing <strong>the Argument for Universality</strong> is <em>Scholar B</em>.</p>
<p>The debate follows an academic format: opening statements, rebuttals, and closing arguments.</p>
<hr/>
<h1><strong>OPENING STATEMENTS</strong></h1>
<hr/>
<h2>**Scholar A (Exclusivity):</h2>
<p>"Muhammad was sent only to the Arabs."**</p>
<p>I will demonstrate—using the Qur’an alone—that Muhammad’s mission was limited to the <strong>Arab peoples</strong>, specifically the <strong>Quraysh and neighboring Arabian tribes</strong>.</p>
<h3><strong>1. The Qur'an Teaches That Every Nation Has Its Own Messenger</strong></h3>
<p><strong>Surah Yunus 10:47</strong> states:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>“Every nation has its messenger.”</p>
</blockquote>
<p>If every nation was <em>individually</em> assigned a prophet, then Muhammad cannot logically be the prophet of <em>all</em> nations.</p>
<h3><strong>2. Prophets Are Sent in Their Own Language to Their Own People</strong></h3>
<p><strong>Surah Ibrahim 14:4</strong>:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>“We have not sent a messenger except in the language of his people.”</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Muhammad spoke <strong>Arabic</strong> and belonged to <strong>an Arab tribe</strong>.<br/>
Thus his "people" are Arabs — not Africans, not Indians, not Europeans.</p>
<h3><strong>3. Earlier Nations Already Received Their Messengers</strong></h3>
<p><strong>Surah An-Nahl 16:36</strong>:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>“We certainly sent a messenger to every nation…”</p>
</blockquote>
<p>The Qur'an claims Africans, Indians, and Persians had prophets <em>before</em>.<br/>
Thus Muhammad cannot be their prophet.</p>
<h3><strong>4. The Qur'an Explicitly Declares Different Shari’as for Different Peoples</strong></h3>
<p><strong>Surah Al-Mā’idah 5:48</strong>:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>“To each of you We prescribed a law and a method.”</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Different peoples = different laws = different messengers.</p>
<h3><strong>5. Muhammad Sent to a People Who Had Never Received a Messenger Before</strong></h3>
<p><strong>Surah As-Sajdah 32:3</strong>:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>“So that you may warn a people to whom no warner has come before you.”</p>
</blockquote>
<p>This describes <strong>Arab pagans</strong>, not the whole world.</p>
<h3><strong>6. Muhammad Was One Among Many Regional Messengers</strong></h3>
<p><strong>Surah Ya-Sin 36:6</strong>:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>“To warn a people whose forefathers were not warned.”</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Again—Arabs only.</p>
<h3><strong>7. The Qur'an Distinguishes Between “Two Former Nations” and Muhammad’s Own Nation</strong></h3>
<p><strong>Surah Al-An’ām 6:156–157</strong>:<br/>
Here Allah speaks to <em>a new nation</em> different from the “two nations” (Jews and Christians) who already had scriptures.</p>
<p>Who is this new nation?<br/>
<strong>The Arabs.</strong></p>
<h3><strong>8. The Qur’an Is a Book in “Clear Arabic” Sent to an Arab Prophet</strong></h3>
<p><strong>Surah Ash-Shu’ara 26:195</strong>:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>“In a clear Arabic language.”</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Then Allah adds:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>“If We had revealed it to a non-Arab… they would not have believed.”</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Thus the Qur'an is <em>not universal</em>.</p>
<h3><strong>9. Muhammad Sent to “The Mother of Cities (Mecca) and its Surrounding Regions”</strong></h3>
<p><strong>Surah Al-An’ām 6:92</strong>; <strong>Surah Ash-Shūrā 42:7</strong>:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>“To warn the Mother of Cities and those around it.”</p>
</blockquote>
<p>“Those around it” cannot mean <em>the whole world</em>; it refers to nearby Arab regions.</p>
<h3><strong>10. Qur'an Revealed in Arabic <em>for Arabs</em></strong></h3>
<p>If the Qur'an were for <strong>all humanity</strong>, why restrict it to <strong>Arabic only</strong>?</p>
<p>No universal scripture demands one ethnic language.</p>
<hr/>
<h2><strong>Conclusion of Scholar A</strong></h2>
<p>Based solely on Qur’anic evidence, Muhammad is <strong>not</strong> a prophet for all humanity.<br/>
He is the prophet <strong>for the Arabs only</strong>, and the Qur'an is <strong>their regional scripture</strong>.</p>
<hr/>
<h1>**Scholar B (Universality):</h1>
<p>"Muhammad was sent to all mankind."**</p>
<p>Scholar B acknowledges the verses cited but provides interpretive arguments common in Islamic theology.</p>
<h3><strong>1. The Qur'an Uses Gradual Revelation</strong></h3>
<p>Muslims argue that early verses emphasize Arabs because Muhammad was <em>starting</em> with his own people.</p>
<h3><strong>2. Universality Claimed Explicitly</strong></h3>
<p>Two verses are commonly cited:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p><strong>Surah Al-Anbiya 21:107</strong>:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>“We have sent you as a mercy to the worlds.”</p>
</blockquote>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Surah Saba 34:28</strong>:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>“We have sent you to all mankind.”</p>
</blockquote>
</li>
</ul>
<p>These verses form the basis for universality.</p>
<h3><strong>3. “Language” Does Not Limit Audience</strong></h3>
<p>They argue: Moses spoke Hebrew but Jews still believe his message is universal in moral terms.</p>
<h3><strong>4. Arabs Were the First Audience, Not the Only Audience</strong></h3>
<p>Muslims say the Qur’an began with Arabs but extended globally through dawah.</p>
<h3><strong>5. “Every Nation Has a Messenger” Indicates Continuity, Not Exclusivity</strong></h3>
<p>Thus Muhammad fulfills earlier prophets and unifies humanity.</p>
<hr/>
<h1><strong>REBUTTALS</strong></h1>
<hr/>
<h2><strong>Scholar A Responds</strong></h2>
<h3>✔ Verse 34:28 Does Not Override 10:47, 14:4, 16:36, 42:7</h3>
<p>The universal claim contradicts earlier foundational principles:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p><strong>Every nation has its prophet.</strong></p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Prophets are sent to their own people.</strong></p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Each nation has its own law.</strong></p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>These cannot harmonize with a universal prophet.</p>
<h3>✔ “Mercy to the Worlds” Does Not Mean “Messenger to the Worlds”</h3>
<p>It is a <strong>poetic honorific</strong>, not a doctrinal definition.</p>
<h3>✔ A truly universal message cannot be locked in one ethnic language</h3>
<p>Why is the Qur'an:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>eternally Arabic</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>untranslatable (translations are not Qur’ans)</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>limited to Arab culture, law, and context?</p>
</li>
</ul>
<h3>✔ The Qur'an repeatedly points only to Arabs</h3>
<p>Not once does it name Africans, Persians, Chinese, or Europeans as part of Muhammad’s mandate.</p>
<hr/>
<h2><strong>Scholar B Responds</strong></h2>
<p>Scholar B appeals to Islamic consensus:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p>Muslim scholars historically agree that Muhammad is universal.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The Qur'an was revealed in Arabic simply because Muhammad was Arab.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>But Scholar B cannot negate the explicit limiting verses.</p>
<hr/>
<h1><strong>CLOSING STATEMENTS</strong></h1>
<hr/>
<h2><strong>Scholar A (Exclusivity)</strong></h2>
<p>The Qur’an itself—consistently, repeatedly, and explicitly—defines:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p><strong>Muhammad = Messenger to Arabs.</strong></p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Each nation = its own messenger.</strong></p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Qur'an = Arabic message for Arabic speakers.</strong></p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Mission field = Mecca and its regional tribes.</strong></p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>No verse can overturn this structural doctrine.</p>
<p>Therefore:<br/>
<strong>Muhammad is not the prophet of the world.<br/>
He is the prophet of the Arabs.</strong></p>
<hr/>
<h2><strong>Scholar B (Universality)</strong></h2>
<p>Muslim theology relies heavily on later interpretations and hadith, not the explicit Qur’anic text.<br/>
But the Islamic consensus still insists on Muhammad’s universal mission.</p>
<hr/>

<p>A textual analysis of the Qur'an <em>as it reads literally</em> strongly supports Scholar A’s position:</p>
<h3><strong>The Qur’an repeatedly limits Muhammad’s mission to:</strong></h3>
<ul>
<li>
<p><strong>His people</strong></p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>His language</strong></p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>His region</strong></p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>His tribe</strong></p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>His culture</strong></p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>His historical context</strong></p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>Universal claims appear <strong>later</strong> and are <strong>contradicted</strong> by foundational principles.</p>

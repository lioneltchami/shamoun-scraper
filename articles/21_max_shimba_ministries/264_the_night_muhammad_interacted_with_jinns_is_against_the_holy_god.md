<h2 data-end="2089" data-start="2069">1. Introduction</h2>
<p data-end="2636" data-start="2091">The rise of Islam in the 7th century introduced a new prophetic claim: Muhammad as the “seal of the prophets” and bearer of the final revelation. However, the Qur’an and Hadith preserve accounts that Muhammad not only encountered but also converted <em data-end="2347" data-start="2340">jinns</em> (spiritual beings in Islamic cosmology). This claim presents a theological dilemma when evaluated against the biblical record. In Scripture, God’s prophets never engaged in dialogue with unclean spirits—rather, they consistently cast them out and demonstrated God’s authority over them.</p>
<p data-end="2891" data-start="2638">The Qur’an’s portrayal of Muhammad as a prophet to both humans and jinn, therefore, raises fundamental questions: Is this consistent with the God of the Bible? Or does it reveal that Muhammad’s experiences originated from a different spiritual source?</p>
<p data-end="3112" data-start="2893">This paper will critically examine Muhammad’s interaction with jinns, contrast it with biblical revelation, and argue that such encounters undermine the claim of Muhammad’s legitimacy as a prophet of the one true God.</p>

<h2 data-end="3169" data-start="3119">2. Understanding the Concept of Jinn in Islam</h2>
<h3 data-end="3203" data-start="3171">Jinn in Pre-Islamic Arabia</h3>
<p data-end="3457" data-start="3204">Before Islam, Arabs believed in jinn as invisible beings inhabiting deserts and ruins. They were associated with soothsayers, poets, and occult practices. Muhammad’s contemporaries were familiar with them as spirits capable of possession and mischief.</p>
<h3 data-end="3494" data-start="3459">Qur’anic Descriptions of Jinn</h3>
<p data-end="3901" data-start="3495">The Qur’an repeatedly mentions jinns (over 30 times). They are described as created from smokeless fire (Surah 55:15), having free will, and divided into believers and unbelievers (Surah 72:11–15). This dual capacity aligns them closer to humans than to the biblical concept of demons, yet their functions—deception, haunting, and whispering evil (Surah 114:4–6)—parallel the role of demons in Scripture.</p>
<h3 data-end="3937" data-start="3903">Jinn in Hadith and Tradition</h3>
<p data-end="4244" data-start="3938">Hadith literature affirms Muhammad’s interaction with jinns. Reports narrate how jinns listened to the Qur’an, converted to Islam, and even asked Muhammad for religious instruction (Sahih al-Bukhari 731; Sahih Muslim 450). The famous <em data-end="4187" data-start="4172">Laylatul Jinn</em> describes Muhammad spending an entire night with them.</p>
<p data-end="4433" data-start="4246">These sources elevate Muhammad not only as prophet of mankind but also of the jinn—earning him the title <em data-end="4377" data-start="4351">Mab’uth ila al-Thaqalayn</em> (“Sent to the two heavy creatures: humans and jinn”).</p>
<hr data-end="4438" data-start="4435"/>
<h2 data-end="4495" data-start="4440">3. Biblical Theology of Demons and Unclean Spirits</h2>
<h3 data-end="4542" data-start="4497">Old Testament Condemnation of Spiritism</h3>
<p data-end="4597" data-start="4543">The Torah strictly forbids interaction with spirits:</p>
<ul data-end="4839" data-start="4598">
<li data-end="4683" data-start="4598">
<p data-end="4683" data-start="4600">“Do not turn to mediums or necromancers; do not seek them out” (Leviticus 19:31).</p>
</li>
<li data-end="4839" data-start="4684">
<p data-end="4839" data-start="4686">“There shall not be found among you… one who inquires of the dead, for whoever does these things is an abomination to the Lord” (Deuteronomy 18:10–12).</p>
</li>
</ul>
<h3 data-end="4885" data-start="4841">Jesus Christ and Authority Over Demons</h3>
<p data-end="5108" data-start="4886">Jesus’ ministry demonstrated complete authority over demons. He did not negotiate with them but commanded them to leave (Mark 1:25; Luke 8:29). The demons recognized Him as the Son of God but remained under condemnation.</p>
<h3 data-end="5150" data-start="5110">Apostolic Ministry and Deliverance</h3>
<p data-end="5306" data-start="5151">The apostles followed Christ’s example by casting out spirits in His name (Acts 16:18; Acts 19:11–12). Nowhere do they invite demons into God’s covenant.</p>
<p data-end="5465" data-start="5308"><strong data-end="5322" data-start="5308">Key point:</strong> In biblical theology, demons are irredeemable, destined for eternal fire (Matthew 25:41). Salvation is for humanity, not for fallen spirits.</p>

This is in stark contradiction with the biblical command:</p><blockquote data-end="1424" data-start="1273">
<p data-end="1424" data-start="1275">“Do not be unequally yoked with unbelievers… what fellowship has light with darkness? What accord has Christ with Belial?” (2 Corinthians 6:14–15).</p>
</blockquote><p data-end="1557" data-start="1426">If Muhammad integrated jinns into his community, then the God behind his message is permitting what the God of the Bible forbids.</p><p data-end="1656" data-start="1559"><strong data-end="1572" data-start="1559">Question:</strong> Why does Islam embrace fellowship with spirits, while Yahweh commands separation?</p><h3 data-end="1713" data-start="1658">Salvation for Jinn vs. Eternal Judgment of Demons</h3><p data-end="2017" data-start="1714">The Qur’an depicts some jinns as “Muslims” and recipients of guidance (Surah al-Jinn 72:13–14). Revelation 20:10 confirms their final destruction.</p><p data-end="2196" data-start="2019">If Muhammad preached salvation for spirits that God has eternally condemned, then Islam introduces a theological impossibility: redemption for fallen beings beyond redemption.</p><p data-end="2290" data-start="2198"><strong data-end="2211" data-start="2198">Question:</strong> Did Muhammad contradict God’s eternal decree by offering salvation to jinns?</p><hr data-end="2295" data-start="2292"/><h2 data-end="2352" data-start="2297">6. </blockquote><p data-end="2914" data-start="2655">If Satan can disguise himself as an angel, how much more could he disguise himself as “jinns” affirming a false message? Rather than evidence of divine truth, Muhammad’s encounters could be viewed as demonic deception designed to establish a false religion.</p><p data-end="3070" data-start="2916"><strong data-end="2929" data-start="2916">Question:</strong> If Satan deceives through appearances of light, could the so-called “jinns” Muhammad converted have been demons masquerading as believers?</p><h3 data-end="3117" data-start="3072">The Problem of Contradictory Revelation</h3><p data-end="3437" data-start="3118">The Qur’an claims continuity with the Torah and Gospel (Surah 5:46; Surah 3:3). These contradictions reveal that the Qur’an cannot come from the same God who authored the earlier Scriptures.</p><p data-end="3566" data-start="3439"><strong data-end="3452" data-start="3439">Question:</strong> How can Allah claim to confirm the Torah and Gospel while simultaneously contradicting their central doctrines?</p><h3 data-end="3618" data-start="3568">Muhammad’s Early Experiences of “Possession”</h3><p data-end="4017" data-start="3619">Historical sources—including Islamic traditions—acknowledge that Muhammad initially feared he was demon-possessed. Sahih al-Bukhari records that he contemplated suicide after his first revelations because he thought he had been afflicted by a <em data-end="3868" data-start="3862">jinn</em> or demon (Bukhari 6982). </p><p data-end="4199" data-start="4019"><strong data-end="4032" data-start="4019">Question:</strong> If Muhammad himself feared demonic possession at the beginning, should later encounters with jinn be interpreted as divine or as confirmation of his earliest fears?</p><h2 data-end="265" data-start="219">7. Does Allah Equal the God of the Bible?</h2><p data-end="616" data-start="267">One of the most pressing theological questions raised by Muhammad’s interaction with jinns is the identity of <em data-end="384" data-start="377">Allah</em>. Yahweh cannot contradict His own holiness by later permitting fellowship with spirits (jinns).</p><p data-end="1287" data-start="1017">Allah, however, not only permits such fellowship but also affirms that some jinns can be Muslims and part of his covenant community (Surah al-Jinn 72:11–14). The crucifixion and resurrection are the core of the gospel message.</p><p data-end="1821" data-start="1781">Yet the Qur’an explicitly denies both:</p><ul data-end="2034" data-start="1822">

<li data-end="2034" data-start="1947">
<p data-end="2034" data-start="1949"><strong data-end="1971" data-start="1949">Denial of Sonship:</strong> “It is not befitting for Allah to take a son” (Surah 19:35).</p>
</li>
</ul><p data-end="2143" data-start="2036">A god who denies the central act of salvation cannot be equated with the God who provided that salvation.</p><p data-end="2283" data-start="2145"><strong data-end="2167" data-start="2145">Critical Question:</strong> If Allah denies the crucifixion and resurrection, is he not rejecting the very foundation of biblical redemption?</p><h3 data-end="2353" data-start="2285">7.3 The Qur’an’s Claim to Confirm Previous Scriptures Examined</h3><p data-end="2420" data-start="2354">The Qur’an repeatedly claims to affirm the Torah and the Gospel:</p><ul data-end="2689" data-start="2421">

</li>
</ul><p data-end="2973" data-start="2691">Yet, when measured against these very Scriptures, the Qur’an stands in contradiction. The Torah condemns communication with spirits. The Gospels declare Jesus as the Son of God and affirm His death and resurrection. The Qur’an both permits spirit fellowship and denies the gospel.</p><p data-end="3088" data-start="2975">This inconsistency reveals that the Qur’an’s claim to continuity is false. Rather than confirming, it corrupts.</p><p data-end="4199" data-start="4019">
</p><p data-end="3233" data-start="3090"><strong data-end="3112" data-start="3090">Critical Question:</strong> If the Qur’an contradicts the Torah and Gospel on their most essential truths, how can Allah claim to be their author?</p><h2 data-end="287" data-start="234">8. </li>
</ul><p data-end="1168" data-start="1002"><strong data-end="1015" data-start="1002">Question:</strong> If Muhammad were a prophet of the holy God, why did he normalize communion with spirits instead of demonstrating divine authority by casting them out?</p><hr data-end="1173" data-start="1170"/><h3 data-end="1260" data-start="1175">8.2 Why Does Islam Offer Salvation to Jinns When the Bible Declares Their Doom?</h3><ul data-end="1555" data-start="1261">

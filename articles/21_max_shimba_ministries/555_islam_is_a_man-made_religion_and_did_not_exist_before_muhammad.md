# ISLAM IS A MAN-MADE RELIGION AND DID NOT EXIST BEFORE MUHAMMAD

<h1><strong>A Critical Theological and Historical Analysis</strong></h1><p>
<em>By Dr. Maxwell Shimba, Shimba Theological Institute</em></p>
<hr/>
<h2>Introduction</h2>
<p>The claim that Islam predates Muhammad and has been the original faith since Adam is central to Islamic theology. Islamic orthodoxy posits a universalist view: all prophets were Muslims, all past revelations are part of the continuum of Islam, and thus, Islam is as old as humanity itself.<sup><a aria-describedby="footnote-label" data-footnote-ref="" href="https://chatgpt.com/c/684a0974-88e0-8006-b57d-4433a7b82986#user-content-fn-1" id="user-content-fnref-1">1</a></sup> Yet, this claim merits rigorous examination against the backdrop of ancient textual evidence, religious self-identification, and historical consciousness.</p>
<p>This paper critically examines whether Islam, as a religious identity and doctrinal system, existed before Muhammad. Special emphasis is placed on primary sources, historical method, and direct engagement with major Islamic apologetic arguments.</p>
<hr/>
<h2>The Islamic Universalist Claim</h2>
<h3>The Core Assertion</h3>
<p>Muslim theologians assert that "Islam" is not limited to the system established by Muhammad, but is instead the primordial faith of all genuine prophets and their followers.<sup><a aria-describedby="footnote-label" data-footnote-ref="" href="https://chatgpt.com/c/684a0974-88e0-8006-b57d-4433a7b82986#user-content-fn-2" id="user-content-fnref-2">2</a></sup> The Quran itself declares:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"Indeed, the religion in the sight of Allah is Islam."<br/>
<em>(Quran 3:19)</em></p>
</blockquote>
<p>And regarding Abraham:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"He was neither a Jew nor a Christian, but a true Muslim (hanifan musliman), and was not of the polytheists."<br/>
<em>(Quran 3:67)</em></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Based on such verses, Muslims believe that Adam, Noah, Abraham, Moses, David, and Jesus all preached "Islam" and were "Muslims."<sup><a aria-describedby="footnote-label" data-footnote-ref="" href="https://chatgpt.com/c/684a0974-88e0-8006-b57d-4433a7b82986#user-content-fn-3" id="user-content-fnref-3">3</a></sup></p>
<h4>Apologetic Argument</h4>
<p><strong>Islamic apologetics</strong> often claims:</p>
<ol>
<li>
<p>The message of submission (<em>Islam</em>) is universal and found in earlier revelations.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>Practices such as ritual purity, prostration, and fasting are evidence of continuity between Islam and previous faiths.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p>The Torah and Gospel were originally Islamic, though later corrupted.</p>
</li>
</ol>
<hr/>
<h2>Methodological Framework</h2>
<h3>Criteria for Analysis</h3>
<ul>
<li>
<p><strong>Primary Sources</strong>: Only documents demonstrably written before Muhammad (i.e., before the 7th century CE) will be considered.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Terminological Evidence</strong>: Explicit use of "Islam" (الإسلام) or "Muslim" (مسلم) as religious identity.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Doctrinal Substance</strong>: Beyond practices, doctrinal and theological content must reflect Islam as later defined.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<hr/>
<h2>1. Textual Silence on "Islam" and "Muslim" Prior to Muhammad</h2>
<h3>A. Hebrew Bible / Old Testament</h3>
<p>Nowhere in the Hebrew Bible do we find the term "Islam" or "Muslim." Abraham is called the "friend of God" (Isaiah 41:8), a recipient of the covenant (Genesis 17), and the progenitor of Israel—not a "Muslim." The central theme is covenant, not submission.<sup><a aria-describedby="footnote-label" data-footnote-ref="" href="https://chatgpt.com/c/684a0974-88e0-8006-b57d-4433a7b82986#user-content-fn-4" id="user-content-fnref-4">4</a></sup> Even in post-exilic literature (e.g., Daniel, Ezra), the concept of "Islam" as later articulated is absent.</p>
<h3>B. New Testament</h3>
<p>Jesus and his followers never identify as "Muslims" or claim to follow a religion called "Islam." Early Christians referred to themselves as members of "the Way" (Acts 9:2; 19:9, 23), and later as "Christians" (Acts 11:26). Paul explicitly refers to himself as a "servant of Christ Jesus" (Romans 1:1), not as a "Muslim."<sup><a aria-describedby="footnote-label" data-footnote-ref="" href="https://chatgpt.com/c/684a0974-88e0-8006-b57d-4433a7b82986#user-content-fn-5" id="user-content-fnref-5">5</a></sup></p>
<h3>C. Second Temple and Greco-Roman Literature</h3>
<p>No usage of "Islam" or "Muslim" is found in the Dead Sea Scrolls, Philo of Alexandria, Josephus, or any pre-Islamic Arabian inscriptions.<sup><a aria-describedby="footnote-label" data-footnote-ref="" href="https://chatgpt.com/c/684a0974-88e0-8006-b57d-4433a7b82986#user-content-fn-6" id="user-content-fnref-6">6</a></sup> Jewish and Christian sects of the period—Essenes, Pharisees, Sadducees, Nazarenes, Ebionites—had well-defined self-identities, none of which included the terms or concepts later associated with Islam.</p>
<hr/>
<h2>2. The Argument from Shared Practices</h2>
<p>Islamic apologetics often points to shared ritual practices—ablution, prostration, fasting—as evidence of pre-Islamic Islam. However, this argument is fundamentally flawed:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p><strong>Ablution</strong>: Ritual washing is central to Levitical law (see Leviticus 15), as well as to ancient Egyptian and Zoroastrian practice.<sup><a aria-describedby="footnote-label" data-footnote-ref="" href="https://chatgpt.com/c/684a0974-88e0-8006-b57d-4433a7b82986#user-content-fn-7" id="user-content-fnref-7">7</a></sup> It does not define a religious identity.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Prostration</strong>: Prostration is an ancient gesture of worship seen in Daniel 6:10 (Jewish), Matthew 26:39 (Christian), and in Hindu and Buddhist traditions.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Fasting</strong>: Fasting predates Islam by centuries and is prominent in Judaism (Leviticus 16:29-31; Esther 4:16) and Christianity (Matthew 6:16-18).</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>These practices are not unique to Islam, nor do they imply Islamic self-identity. Religious universals cannot serve as evidence for a specific faith.</p>
<hr/>
<h2>3. Doctrinal and Theological Distinctions</h2>
<h3>A. Judaism</h3>
<p>The center of Jewish theology is <strong>covenant</strong> (ברית, <em>berit</em>) and Torah obedience, not universal submission.<sup><a aria-describedby="footnote-label" data-footnote-ref="" href="https://chatgpt.com/c/684a0974-88e0-8006-b57d-4433a7b82986#user-content-fn-8" id="user-content-fnref-8">8</a></sup> God is known as Yahweh, not Allah; the Shema (Deuteronomy 6:4) is the core confession, not the Shahada.</p>
<h3>B. Christianity</h3>
<p>Christian faith revolves around the <strong>incarnation, death, and resurrection of Jesus Christ</strong> and the <strong>new covenant</strong> (Luke 22:20). Early Christians confessed "Jesus is Lord" (Romans 10:9), not "there is no god but Allah, Muhammad is the Messenger of Allah."</p>
<h3>C. Islam</h3>
<p>Islam, as established by Muhammad, is defined by the Five Pillars, the Quran, and the Sunnah. The doctrine of <em>tawhid</em> (absolute monotheism) and the prophethood of Muhammad are non-negotiable tenets unknown to previous revelations.<sup><a aria-describedby="footnote-label" data-footnote-ref="" href="https://chatgpt.com/c/684a0974-88e0-8006-b57d-4433a7b82986#user-content-fn-9" id="user-content-fnref-9">9</a></sup></p>
<hr/>
<h2>4. Historical and Archaeological Evidence</h2>
<p>No archaeological inscription, manuscript, or papyrus prior to the 7th century contains reference to "Islam" as a religious system or to its followers as "Muslims."<sup><a aria-describedby="footnote-label" data-footnote-ref="" href="https://chatgpt.com/c/684a0974-88e0-8006-b57d-4433a7b82986#user-content-fn-10" id="user-content-fnref-10">10</a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>
<p><strong>Pre-Islamic Arabia</strong>: Inscriptions reference gods like Hubal, Al-Lat, Al-Uzza, and Manat—not Allah in a monotheistic Islamic sense.<sup><a aria-describedby="footnote-label" data-footnote-ref="" href="https://chatgpt.com/c/684a0974-88e0-8006-b57d-4433a7b82986#user-content-fn-11" id="user-content-fnref-11">11</a></sup></p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Christian and Jewish Writings</strong>: Non-Islamic sources do not recognize Islam or Muslims prior to Muhammad.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<hr/>
<h2>5. Islamic Apologetics: Critical Engagement</h2>
<h3>A. Retrojection and Reinterpretation</h3>
<p>Islamic polemics often retroject later concepts into ancient texts. For instance, calling Abraham a "Muslim" or claiming "Islam" means generic submission. However, historical-critical scholarship rejects such anachronisms, as self-identity in ancient texts is contextually bound.<sup><a aria-describedby="footnote-label" data-footnote-ref="" href="https://chatgpt.com/c/684a0974-88e0-8006-b57d-4433a7b82986#user-content-fn-12" id="user-content-fnref-12">12</a></sup></p>
<h3>B. Alleged Corruption of Previous Scriptures</h3>
<p>Muslim apologists assert that Jews and Christians corrupted their scriptures, hence the absence of explicit references to Islam.<sup><a aria-describedby="footnote-label" data-footnote-ref="" href="https://chatgpt.com/c/684a0974-88e0-8006-b57d-4433a7b82986#user-content-fn-13" id="user-content-fnref-13">13</a></sup> However, textual criticism and manuscript evidence (Dead Sea Scrolls, Codex Sinaiticus, etc.) demonstrate remarkable fidelity in the transmission of biblical texts long before the advent of Islam.<sup><a aria-describedby="footnote-label" data-footnote-ref="" href="https://chatgpt.com/c/684a0974-88e0-8006-b57d-4433a7b82986#user-content-fn-14" id="user-content-fnref-14">14</a></sup> There is no textual gap where "Islam" was erased or altered.</p>
<h3>C. "Hanif" and Primordial Monotheism</h3>
<p>The term <em>hanif</em> in the Quran refers to pre-Islamic monotheists, often linked to Abraham. Yet, outside the Quran, there is no evidence that such a community called itself "Muslim" or that their faith matched Islam doctrinally or ritually.<sup><a aria-describedby="footnote-label" data-footnote-ref="" href="https://chatgpt.com/c/684a0974-88e0-8006-b57d-4433a7b82986#user-content-fn-15" id="user-content-fnref-15">15</a></sup></p>

<hr/>
<h2>Conclusion</h2>
<p>The proposition that Islam, as a religious identity, existed prior to Muhammad is not supported by historical, textual, or archaeological evidence. All available sources point to Islam as a <strong>distinct, post-7th-century religious phenomenon</strong>.</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p><strong>No use of the term "Islam" or "Muslim"</strong> in any pre-Islamic scripture.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Shared rituals</strong> are universal, not uniquely Islamic.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Doctrinal substance</strong> of pre-Muhammad faiths is distinct from later Islamic teaching.</p>
</li>
</ul>
<p>It is, therefore, both academically and theologically sound to affirm that <strong>Islam is a man-made religion that began with Muhammad</strong>, not a timeless faith existing since Adam.<br/>
To argue otherwise is to engage in retrospective reinterpretation, unsupported by the historical record.</p>

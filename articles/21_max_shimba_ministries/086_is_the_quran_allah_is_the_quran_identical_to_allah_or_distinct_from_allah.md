# Is the Qur’an Allah? Is the Qur’an identical to Allah, or distinct from Allah?

<div class="post-body entry-content" id="post-body-4598932727864746478" itemprop="description articleBody">
<p> <strong>Is the Qur’an Allah?</strong></p><p><strong>Eternity, Createdness, and the Problem of Monotheism in Islamic Theology</strong><br/><strong>By Dr. Maxwell Shimba</strong><br/><strong>Shimba Theological Institute</strong></p><hr/><h3>Abstract</h3><p>One of the most persistent and unresolved theological tensions within Islam concerns the nature of the Qur'an. Classical Islamic theology is divided over whether the Qur’an is <strong>created</strong> or <strong>uncreated</strong>. This article argues that both positions generate serious philosophical and theological problems for Islamic monotheism (<em>tawḥīd</em>). If the Qur’an is created, it cannot be the eternal speech of Allah. If it is uncreated and eternal, then Islam appears to affirm <strong>two eternal realities</strong>, raising fundamental questions about whether Islam consistently maintains monotheism while simultaneously rejecting the Christian doctrine of the Trinity.</p><hr/><h3>1. The Central Question: What Is the Qur’an?</h3><p>Islam universally affirms that the Qur’an is the <em>kalām Allāh</em>—the speech of Allah. However, Islamic theology has never reached consensus on <strong>how</strong> this speech exists in relation to Allah Himself. The dilemma can be framed simply:</p><ul><li><p>Is the Qur’an <strong>created in time</strong>, or</p></li><li><p>Is the Qur’an <strong>uncreated and eternal</strong>?</p></li></ul><p>This question is not peripheral. It strikes at the heart of divine ontology, revelation, and the coherence of Islamic monotheism.</p><hr/><h3>2. The Created Qur’an: A Finite Revelation?</h3><p>The <strong>Muʿtazilite</strong> school famously argued that the Qur’an is <strong>created</strong>. Their motivation was to preserve absolute divine unity and avoid positing anything eternal alongside Allah.</p><p>However, this position generates severe consequences:</p><ol><li><p><strong>Denial of Eternal Speech</strong><br/>If the Qur’an is created, then Allah’s speech is not eternal. This implies that Allah was once <em>without</em> speech, contradicting the Islamic claim that divine attributes are eternal.</p></li><li><p><strong>Undermining Divine Perfection</strong><br/>Speech is not an accidental attribute but an essential expression of intellect and will. A God who acquires speech in time is, by definition, mutable—an attribute incompatible with classical theism.</p></li><li><p><strong>Theological Instability</strong><br/>If the Qur’an is created, then it becomes a contingent object, subject to historical conditions. This weakens claims of its absolute, timeless authority.</p></li></ol><p>Thus, in attempting to protect monotheism, the “created Qur’an” position undermines divine immutability and perfection.</p><hr/><h3>3. The Uncreated Qur’an: Two Eternals?</h3><p>The dominant <strong>Sunni</strong> position holds that the Qur’an is <strong>uncreated, eternal, and subsisting with Allah</strong>. This view avoids the problems of mutability but introduces a far deeper contradiction.</p><p>If the Qur’an is:</p><ul><li><p>Eternal</p></li><li><p>Uncreated</p></li><li><p>Distinct from creation</p></li></ul><p>then the following dilemma arises:</p><blockquote><p><strong>Is the Qur’an identical to Allah, or distinct from Allah?</strong></p></blockquote><h4>3.1 If Identical to Allah</h4><p>If the Qur’an <em>is</em> Allah, then Muslims effectively identify a <strong>book, words, sounds, and letters</strong> with God Himself. This is theologically incoherent and borders on bibliolatry.</p><h4>3.2 If Distinct from Allah</h4><p>If the Qur’an is <strong>not Allah</strong>, yet eternal and uncreated, then Islam affirms <strong>two eternal realities</strong>:</p><ol><li><p>Allah</p></li><li><p>The Qur’an</p></li></ol><p>This violates the very principle of <em>tawḥīd</em> Islam claims to defend. Monotheism, by definition, does not permit multiple uncreated eternals.</p><hr/><h3>4. The Trinity Objection Reconsidered</h3><p>Islam frequently rejects the Christian Trinity on the grounds that it compromises divine unity. Yet Christianity does <strong>not</strong> teach three gods, but one God with eternally existing personal distinctions.</p><p>Ironically, classical Sunni Islam affirms:</p><ul><li><p>One God (Allah)</p></li><li><p>An uncreated, eternal speech (the Qur’an)</p></li><li><p>Multiple eternal divine attributes without personal distinction</p></li></ul><p>The question then becomes unavoidable:</p><blockquote><p><strong>How can Islam accuse Christianity of polytheism while affirming an eternal reality alongside Allah that is not Allah?</strong></p></blockquote><p>At minimum, Islam faces the same metaphysical complexity it condemns—without the philosophical clarity Christianity provides through Trinitarian ontology.</p><hr/><h3>5. Philosophical Implications</h3><p>From a philosophical standpoint, Islam is caught in a false dilemma:</p><ul><li><p><strong>Created Qur’an</strong> → God is mutable and not eternally speaking</p></li><li><p><strong>Uncreated Qur’an</strong> → Two eternals, undermining monotheism</p></li></ul><p>Both options compromise either:</p><ul><li><p>Divine perfection, or</p></li><li><p>Divine unity</p></li></ul><p>There is no third coherent alternative within Islamic theology.</p><hr/><h3>6. Conclusion</h3><p>The question <strong>“Is the Qur’an Allah?”</strong> is not rhetorical—it is unavoidable. Islamic theology cannot coherently maintain that the Qur’an is eternal, uncreated, and yet not divine without collapsing into contradiction.</p><p>Thus, Islam faces a fundamental challenge:</p><blockquote><p><strong>How can Islam consistently claim monotheism if both Allah and the Qur’an are eternal and uncreated?</strong></p></blockquote><p>Until this dilemma is resolved, Islamic claims of theological simplicity and superior monotheism remain philosophically and logically unsubstantiated.</p><hr/><p><strong>Dr. Maxwell Shimba</strong><br/><em>Shimba Theological Institute</em></p>

# The Term “God” in Arabic is Ilāh

<p></p><h2>The Term “God” in Arabic is <em>Ilāh</em>: A Scholarly Examination of the Shahada, Qur’anic Text, and Theological Implications</h2><p><strong>By Max Shimba</strong></p><hr/><h3>Abstract</h3><p>This article investigates the linguistic and theological distinctions between the Arabic terms <em>ilāh</em> (god) and <em>Allāh</em> (Allah) within the context of Islamic doctrine, particularly the Shahada (Islamic declaration of faith). Drawing from the Qur’an, authoritative hadith, and academic sources, it examines how these terms are used and their implications for interfaith discourse.</p><hr/><h3>1. Introduction</h3><p>In Islamic theology, the <em>Shahada</em>—“Lā ilāha illā Allāh Muhammadur Rasūl Allāh”—is central, representing the cornerstone of Muslim belief. It can be found explicitly in the Qur’an (e.g., 3:18), as well as in canonical hadith collections such as <em>Sahih al-Bukhari</em> (Vol. 4, Book 56, No. 725). The meaning and translation of key terms within the Shahada are crucial to understanding Islamic monotheism and its distinction from other faiths.</p><hr/><h3>2. Linguistic Analysis of the Shahada</h3><p>The Arabic phrase <em>lā ilāha illā Allāh</em> can be broken down as follows:</p><ul>
<li>
<p><strong>lā</strong> (لا): “There is no,” “not,” “none” (Wehr, 1976).</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>ilāha</strong> (إِلَـٰهَ): “god,” “deity,” “object of worship.” This is a generic noun (Lane, 1863).</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>illā</strong> (إِلَّا): “except,” “but.” A contraction of <em>in-lā</em> (Wehr, 1976).</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Allāh</strong> (اللّٰه): Proper noun; the unique name for the one God in Islam. Not translated; considered linguistically and theologically unique (Peters, 2003).</p>
</li>
</ul><p>Thus, the phrase translates as:<br/>
<strong>“There is no god except Allah.”</strong></p><hr/><h3>3. Academic and Lexical Sources</h3><h4>3.1. Lexicographical Evidence</h4><ul>
<li>
<p><strong>Hans Wehr, “A Dictionary of Modern Written Arabic” (1976):</strong> Defines <em>ilāh</em> as “a god, deity, object of worship.”</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Edward William Lane, “An Arabic-English Lexicon” (1863):</strong> Notes <em>ilāh</em> as a generic term, while <em>Allāh</em> is used exclusively for the God of Islam.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Al-Tafsir.com</strong> (Saudi Government’s Official Portal): Confirms that <em>ilāh</em> is generic, while <em>Allāh</em> is not translated and has no equivalent in other languages.</p>
</li>
</ul><h4>3.2. Theological and Qur’anic Context</h4><ul>
<li>
<p><strong>The Qur’an 3:18:</strong> “Allah bears witness that there is no deity except Him…” (Sahih International translation).</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Sahih al-Bukhari, Vol. 4, Book 56, Hadith 725:</strong> Records the Shahada and its significance in Islamic faith.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Tafsir al-Jalalayn:</strong> Explains that <em>ilāh</em> denotes any object of worship, but <em>Allāh</em> is unique and incomparable.</p>
</li>
</ul><hr/><h3>4. Theological Implications</h3><h4>4.1. The Uniqueness of Allah</h4><p>The Qur’an states in Surah al-Ikhlas (112:4): “And there is none comparable to Him.” Islamic theology maintains that <em>Allāh</em> is sui generis, without partner, equal, or likeness (Nasr, 2003). To equate Allah with deities of other religions—such as the Christian concept of God, Buddha, or traditional African gods—contradicts Islamic monotheism (<em>tawhid</em>).</p><h4>4.2. Polemics and Interfaith Discourse</h4><p>The issue of translating <em>Allāh</em> as “God” in other languages is a subject of ongoing theological debate (see Reynolds, 2010; Peters, 2003). While Arabic-speaking Christians may use “Allah” for God, Islamic doctrine maintains a theological distinction: <em>ilāh</em> can refer to any deity, while <em>Allāh</em> refers uniquely to the one God of Islam.</p><hr/><h3>5. Conclusion</h3><p>A careful analysis of the Shahada and relevant Arabic terms demonstrates a clear distinction:</p><ul>
<li>
<p><strong>ilāh</strong> = a generic term for “god” or “deity”</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Allāh</strong> = the unique, untranslatable proper name for the God of Islam</p>
</li>
</ul><p>Therefore, the assertion that “Allah is not simply ‘God’” but is uniquely identified in Islamic theology is well-supported by both textual and academic evidence.</p><hr/><h3>6. References</h3><ul>

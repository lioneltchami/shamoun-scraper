(Haddad; bold and capital emphasis mine)</p><p style="box-sizing: inherit; margin: 1em 0px 1.1em;">Specific Muslim scholars even went as far as to condemn those that would deny Muhammad’s enthronement as heretics who must be avoided, exposed and warned against:</p><p style="box-sizing: inherit; margin: 1em 0px 1.1em;">15. Ibn Batta’s Doctrine</p><p style="box-sizing: inherit; margin: 1em 0px 1.1em;">Ibn Batta stated in his book <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">al-Sharh wa al-Ibana ‘ala Usul al-Sunna wa al-Diyana</em> (“Elaboration of the Principles of Sunni Doctrine”):</p><p style="box-sizing: inherit; margin: 1em 0px 1.1em;"><strong style="box-sizing: inherit; font-weight: bold;">The Prophet shall be seated on the Throne with his Lord <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">(yujlas ma‘a rabbihi ‘alâ al-‘arsh)</em>, and this privilege belongs to no-one else</strong>. Thus did Nafi‘ narrate it from Ibn ‘Umar<a style="background-color: transparent; border-bottom: 1px dotted rgb(170, 170, 170); box-sizing: inherit; color: #444444;"></a> from the Prophet concerning the verse: “It may be that thy Lord will raise you to an Exalted Station” – he said that He shall seat him with Him on the Throne. Thus also did Mujahid explain it, as narrated by Muhammad ibn Fudayl, from al-Layth, from Mujahid. (Ibid.; bold emphasis mine)</p><p style="box-sizing: inherit; margin: 1em 0px 1.1em;">Even the renowned scholar Ibn Taymiyyah and his star pupil Ibn Qayyim believed and embraced the veracity of this tradition!</p><p style="box-sizing: inherit; margin: 1em 0px 1.1em;">12. Ibn al-Qayyim’s List of Supporters</p><p style="box-sizing: inherit; margin: 1em 0px 1.1em;">Ibn Qayyim al-Jawziyya<span style="box-sizing: inherit; font-size: 13.5px; line-height: 0; position: relative; top: -0.5em; vertical-align: baseline;">[78]</span> said in his <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">Bada’i‘ al-Fawa’id</em>:</p><p style="box-sizing: inherit; margin: 1em 0px 1.1em;">Al-Qadi [Ibn Abi Ya‘la]<span style="box-sizing: inherit; font-size: 13.5px; line-height: 0; position: relative; top: -0.5em; vertical-align: baseline;">[79]</span> said: “Al-Marwazi<span style="box-sizing: inherit; font-size: 13.5px; line-height: 0; position: relative; top: -0.5em; vertical-align: baseline;">[80]</span> compiled a book on the superlative merits of the Prophet in which he mentioned his seating <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">(iq‘âduhu)</em> on the Throne <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">(al-‘arsh)</em>.”</p><p style="box-sizing: inherit; margin: 1em 0px 1.1em;">Al-Qadi further said: “This is the position of Abu Dawud, Ahmad ibn Asram,<span style="box-sizing: inherit; font-size: 13.5px; line-height: 0; position: relative; top: -0.5em; top: -0.5em; vertical-align: baseline;">[93]</span> Muhammad ibn Bishr ibn Sharik,<span style="box-sizing: inherit; font-size: 13.5px; line-height: 0; position: relative; top: -0.5em; vertical-align: baseline;">[94]</span> Abu Qilaba,<span style="box-sizing: inherit; font-size: 13.5px; line-height: 0; position: relative; top: -0.5em; vertical-align: baseline;">[95]</span> ‘Ali ibn Sahl,<span style="box-sizing: inherit; font-size: 13.5px; line-height: 0; position: relative; font-size: 13.5px; line-height: 0; position: relative; top: -0.5em; vertical-align: baseline;">[98]</span> al-Husayn ibn Fadl,<span style="box-sizing: inherit; font-size: 13.5px; line-height: 0; position: relative; font-size: 13.5px; line-height: 0; position: relative; top: -0.5em; vertical-align: baseline;">[101]</span> Muham­mad ibn ‘Imran al-Farisi al-Zahid,<span style="box-sizing: inherit; font-size: 13.5px; line-height: 0; position: relative; top: -0.5em; vertical-align: baseline;">[102]</span> Muhammad ibn Yunus al-Basri,<span style="box-sizing: inherit; font-size: 13.5px; line-height: 0; position: relative; top: -0.5em; vertical-align: baseline;">[103]</span> ‘Abd Allah ibn Ahmad ibn Hanbal,<span style="box-sizing: inherit; font-size: 13.5px; line-height: 0; position: relative; top: -0.5em; vertical-align: baseline;">[104]</span> al-Marwazi, and Bishr al-Hafi.”<span style="box-sizing: inherit; font-size: 13.5px; line-height: 0; position: relative; top: -0.5em; vertical-align: baseline;">[105]</span></p><p style="box-sizing: inherit; margin: 1em 0px 1.1em;">I say: It is also the position of Ibn Jarir al-Tabari,<span style="box-sizing: inherit; font-size: 13.5px; line-height: 0; position: relative; top: -0.5em; vertical-align: baseline;">[106]</span> <strong style="box-sizing: inherit; font-weight: bold;">and the leader of all the above in this is Mujahid, the imam of Qur’anic commentary</strong>. It is also Abu al-Hasan al-Daraqutni’s<span style="box-sizing: inherit; font-size: 13.5px; line-height: 0; position: relative; top: -0.5em; vertical-align: baseline;">[107]</span> who said:</p><p style="box-sizing: inherit; margin: 1em 0px 1.1em;"><em style="box-sizing: inherit;">The hadith of Intercession narrated by Ahmad<br style="box-sizing: inherit;"/>Is traced back to the Elect, Ahmad.<br style="box-sizing: inherit;"/>Also known to us is the hadith of his seating<br style="box-sizing: inherit;"/>On the Throne, <strong style="box-sizing: inherit; font-weight: bold;">THEREFORE DO NOT DENY IT</strong>.<br style="box-sizing: inherit;"/>Let the hadith pass exactly as narrated,<br style="box-sizing: inherit;"/>And do not enter into false notions.<br style="box-sizing: inherit;"/><strong style="box-sizing: inherit; font-weight: bold;">Neither deny that the Prophet sits on the Throne,</strong><br style="box-sizing: inherit;"/><strong style="box-sizing: inherit; font-weight: bold;">Nor deny that Allah makes him sit there!</strong></em><span style="box-sizing: inherit; font-size: 13.5px; line-height: 0; position: relative; top: -0.5em; vertical-align: baseline;">[108]</span></p><p style="box-sizing: inherit; margin: 1em 0px 1.1em;"><span style="box-sizing: inherit; font-size: 13.5px; line-height: 0; position: relative; top: -0.5em; vertical-align: baseline;">[108]</span> Ibn Qayyim al-Jawziyya, <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">Bada’i‘ al-Fawa’id</em> (1900 ed. 4:39-40, 1994 ed. 2:328-329). (Ibid.; bold and capital emphasis mine)</p><p style="box-sizing: inherit; margin: 1em 0px 1.1em;">And:</p><p style="box-sizing: inherit; margin: 1em 0px 1.1em;">16.  Ibn Taymiyya’s Inheritance</p><p style="box-sizing: inherit; margin: 1em 0px 1.1em;">Ibn Taymiyya wrote:</p><p style="box-sizing: inherit; margin: 1em 0px 1.1em;">The scholars recognized by Allah and His accepted Friends have narrated that Muhammad the Messenger of Allah <strong style="box-sizing: inherit; font-weight: bold;">will be seated by His Lord on the Throne next to Him</strong>.</p><p style="box-sizing: inherit; margin: 1em 0px 1.1em;">Muhammad ibn Fudayl narrated this from Layth from Mujahid in the commentary of the verse: “It may be that your Lord will raise you to an Exalted Station.” This was also mentioned through other chains, some traced back to the Prophet and some not.</p><p style="box-sizing: inherit; margin: 1em 0px 1.1em;">Ibn Jarir [al-Tabari] said: “This does not contradict the nearly-mass-narrated narrations <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">(ma istafâdat bihi al-ahâdith)</em> whereby the Exalted Station is the Intercession as agreed upon by the Imams of all Muslims.” <strong style="box-sizing: inherit; font-weight: bold;">He does not say that the Prophet’s seating on the Throne is denounced as false; ONLY SOME JAHMIS HELD IT SO</strong>. Nor is it objectionable to mention it in the context of a commentary on the verse.<span style="box-sizing: inherit; font-size: 13.5px; line-height: 0; position: relative; top: -0.5em; vertical-align: baseline;">[141]</span></p><p style="box-sizing: inherit; margin: 1em 0px 1.1em;"><span style="box-sizing: inherit; font-size: 13.5px; line-height: 0; position: relative; top: -0.5em; vertical-align: baseline;">[141]</span> Ibn Taymiyya, <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">Majmu‘ al-Fatawa</em> (<em style="box-sizing: inherit;">Mufassal al-I‘tiqad</em> – “Specifics of Belief” – 4:374). (Ibid.; bold and capital emphasis mine)</p><p style="box-sizing: inherit; margin: 1em 0px 1.1em;">In case Muhammadans argue that this narration is weak or <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">da’eef</em> here is a report which is deemed to be reliable or sound:</p><p style="box-sizing: inherit; margin: 1em 0px 1.1em;">8. Another Narration From ‘Abd Allah ibn Salam</p><p style="box-sizing: inherit; margin: 1em 0px 1.1em;">From ‘Abd Allah ibn Salam, in a long hadith on the Day of Judgment: “<strong style="box-sizing: inherit; font-weight: bold;">A seat <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">(kursî)</em> will be placed for the Prophet on the right of Allah</strong>.”<span style="box-sizing: inherit; font-size: 13.5px; line-height: 0; position: relative; top: -0.5em; vertical-align: baseline;">[38]</span></p><p style="box-sizing: inherit; margin: 1em 0px 1.1em;"><span style="box-sizing: inherit; font-size: 13.5px; line-height: 0; position: relative; top: -0.5em; vertical-align: baseline;">[38]</span> Al-Hakim narrated it in his <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">Mustadrak</em> (4:568-569) <strong style="box-sizing: inherit; font-weight: bold;">and declared its chain sound <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">(sahîh)</em>, as confirmed by al-Dhahabi</strong>. (Ibid.; bold and capital emphasis mine)</p><p style="box-sizing: inherit; margin: 1em 0px 1.1em;">Islamic scholar Livnat Holtzman mentions the controversy this hadith of Muhajid’s caused for the Muslim community:</p><p style="box-sizing: inherit; margin: 1em 0px 1.1em;">… According to Q. 17:79, God promised Muhammad ‘an honourable station’ (<em style="box-sizing: inherit;">maqāman mahmūdan</em>). Mujahid explained this phrase: ‘[God] will make [Muhammad] sit down with Him on His throne’. <span style="box-sizing: inherit; font-size: 13.5px; line-height: 0; position: relative; top: -0.5em; vertical-align: baseline;">9</span> Previous scholarship that discussed this <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">hadīth</em> focused on its authenticity and its connection to the hardships that the illustrious historian and Quran exegete, Abu Jaʿfar Muhammad ibn Jarir al-Tabari (d. 923), suffered from the Hanbalites of Baghdad.<span style="box-sizing: inherit; font-size: 13.5px; line-height: 0; position: relative; top: -0.5em; vertical-align: baseline;">10</span> …</p><p style="box-sizing: inherit; margin: 1em 0px 1.1em;">When Abu Bakr al-Najjad completed his thorough investigations about this <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">hadīth</em>, he issued a creed (<em style="box-sizing: inherit;">ʿ</em><em style="box-sizing: inherit;">aqīda pl. </em><em style="box-sizing: inherit;">ʿ</em><em style="box-sizing: inherit;">aqā</em><em style="box-sizing: inherit;">ʾ</em><em style="box-sizing: inherit;">id</em>), a profession of faith which reflected the feelings of the Hanbalites on this matter:</p><p style="box-sizing: inherit; margin: 1em 0px 1.1em;">This is our profession of faith before God: We believe in the content of the <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">ahādīth</em> that are attributed to the Messenger of God, but also to ʿAbd Allah ibn ʿAbbas (d. 687–8), and the scholars who succeeded him. These <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">ahādīth</em> were transmitted from one great scholar to another, from one generation to the next generation, until the times of our great teachers. We wrote these <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">ahādīth</em>, and meticulously investigated their content. These <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">ahādīth</em> interpret the meaning of the Quranic verse [Q. 17:79] ‘your Lord may exalt you to an honourable station’. <strong style="box-sizing: inherit; font-weight: bold;">According to these <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">ahādīth</em>, ‘the honourable station’ means that Muhammad will sit with His Lord on His throne. Whoever rejects this interpretation and refutes it, expresses the views of the Jahmiyya (the Muʿtazilites). One must avoid this person, turn away from him, and beware of him</strong>. Abu Bakr al-Khatib (d. 933) informed me that Abu Dawud al-Sijistani told him: ‘Whoever rejects the <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">hadīth</em> attributed to Mujahid is a Jahmite (Muʿtazilite)’.<span style="box-sizing: inherit; font-size: 13.5px; line-height: 0; position: relative; top: -0.5em; vertical-align: baseline;">11</span></p><p style="box-sizing: inherit; margin: 1em 0px 1.1em;"><strong style="box-sizing: inherit; font-weight: bold;">Al-Najjad concluded that the belief in Muhammad’s noble virtue (<em style="box-sizing: inherit;">fadīla</em>) of sitting with God on the throne was one of the cornerstones of the Hanbalite creed</strong>. To illustrate the strength of this belief, al-Najjad explained that hypothetically, a man who declared that God would make Muhammad sit down with Him on the throne and thereafter took the oath on pain of triple divorce<span style="box-sizing: inherit; font-size: 13.5px; line-height: 0; position: relative; top: -0.5em; vertical-align: baseline;">12</span> to strengthen his declaration, should have never feared that he needed to divorce his wife. If this man came to al-Najjad to seek his legal advice whether he should divorce his wife or not, the scholar would tell him:</p><p style="box-sizing: inherit; margin: 1em 0px 1.1em;">You declared the truth, your oath is valid, and your wife should remain safely in her position. Because this is our way, our religion, and our creed. This is the root of our conviction from which we emerged. We will adhere to this conviction until the day we die.<span style="box-sizing: inherit; font-size: 13.5px; line-height: 0; position: relative; top: -0.5em; vertical-align: baseline;">13</span></p><p style="box-sizing: inherit; margin: 1em 0px 1.1em;">Written in the middle of the tenth century, al-Najjad’s creed paraphrased the <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">hadīth </em>on Mujahid’s interpretation of ‘an honourable station’ <strong style="box-sizing: inherit; font-weight: bold;">and elevated it to the degree of an article of faith for the Hanbalites</strong>. By doing so, al-Najjad sealed the turbulent history of this <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">hadīth</em> <strong style="box-sizing: inherit; font-weight: bold;">and silenced all the voices that spoke against this <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">hadīth</em> or merely doubted its veracity</strong>. Al-Najjad’s creed concluded the continuous controversy about this <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">hadīth </em>with a smashing victory over the middle-of-the-road traditionalists.<span style="box-sizing: inherit; font-size: 13.5px; line-height: 0; position: relative; top: -0.5em; vertical-align: baseline;">14 </span>To correctly evaluate al-Najjad’s creed, we need to examine the meaning of this <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">hadīth </em>and its history.</p><p style="box-sizing: inherit; margin: 1em 0px 1.1em;">The <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">hadīth</em> on Mujahid’s interpretation of ‘an honourable station’ was one of the conspicuous <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">ahādīth al-sifāt</em>, the traditions on the divine attributes that included anthropomorphic descriptions of God. This <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">hadīth</em> was controversial in spite of the fact that it did not describe God in any physical way or form. <strong style="box-sizing: inherit; font-weight: bold;">When read literally, this <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">hadīth</em> implies that ‘an honourable station’ is an actual, physical place, and that God’s sitting on the throne (with Muhammad next to Him) is accordingly an actual sitting of a physical body</strong>. This reading obviously led to <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">tashbīh</em>, namely comparing God to His creation and anthropomorphising Him. Laymen who heard this <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">hadīth </em><strong style="box-sizing: inherit; font-weight: bold;">might have been led to visualise a majestic human figure on a throne, sitting next to a much smaller human figure</strong>. The implied description of God in this <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">hadīth</em> <strong style="box-sizing: inherit; font-weight: bold;">was unmistakably an anthropomorphic description</strong>… (Holtzman, <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">Anthropomorphism in Islam: The Challenge of Traditionalism (700-1350) (Edinburgh Studies in Classical Islamic History and Culture) </em>[Edinburg University Press, Ltd. 2019], Introduction, 1. The Narrator and Narrative: A Literary Analysis of <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">Ahadith al-Sifat</em>, pp. 4-5; bold emphasis mine)</p><p style="box-sizing: inherit; margin: 1em 0px 1.1em;"><strong style="box-sizing: inherit; font-weight: bold;">The ninth-century ultra-traditionalists who defended this <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">hadīth</em> and studied it,</strong> <strong style="box-sizing: inherit; font-weight: bold;">toiled a great deal to prove the antiquity of the text</strong>. Thus, a marginal <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">muhaddith</em> by the name of Muhammad ibn ʿAbd al-Malik al-Daqiqi (d. 876) declared: ‘<strong style="box-sizing: inherit; font-weight: bold;">I heard this <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">hadīth</em> for the last fifty years, and I never heard anyone rejecting it. Only the heretics and the Jahmites deny its veracity</strong>.’<span style="box-sizing: inherit; font-size: 13.5px; line-height: 0; position: relative; top: -0.5em; vertical-align: baseline;">23</span> The traditionalists also decorated the <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">hadīth</em> with further details during their study sessions. The Hanbalite Abu ’l-Hasan ibn al-ʿAttar (d. 881) reminisced about such a study session, conducted by the <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">muhaddith</em> Muhammad ibn Musʿab al-ʿAbid al-Daʿʿaʾ (d. 843) from Baghdad:</p><p style="box-sizing: inherit; margin: 1em 0px 1.1em;">Once I heard Muhammad ibn Musʿab al-ʿAbid recount this <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">hadīth</em> which is attributed to Mujahid, <strong style="box-sizing: inherit; font-weight: bold;">namely ‘[God] will make [Muhammad] sit down with Him on His throne’</strong>. After transmitting this <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">hadīth</em>, Muhammad ibn Musʿab al-ʿAbid added: ‘<strong style="box-sizing: inherit; font-weight: bold;">So all the creatures will see Muhammad’s position at His Lord</strong>, and the respect that His Lord has for him. Thereafter, Muhammad will retire to his chambers, gardens and wives in Paradise, so God will remain alone in His ruling of the world.’<span style="box-sizing: inherit; font-size: 13.5px; line-height: 0; position: relative; top: -0.5em; vertical-align: baseline;">24</span> (Ibid., pp. 7-8; bold emphasis mine)</p><p style="box-sizing: inherit; margin: 1em 0px 1.1em;">Holtzman further notes how Muslims resorted to violence over this hadith, with some Hanbalites physically attacking the great Muslim exegete and historian al-Tabari for daring to question its veracity:</p><p style="box-sizing: inherit; margin: 1em 0px 1.1em;">The Hanbalite scholarship in defence of the anthropomorphic version included enthusiastic declarations supporting this <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">hadīth</em> <strong style="box-sizing: inherit; font-weight: bold;">and crowning it as authentic and valuable. Some Hanbalites went further and declared that this <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">hadīth</em> reflected the absolute truth, and should it be refuted, they would immediately divorce their wives</strong>.<span style="box-sizing: inherit; font-size: 13.5px; line-height: 0; position: relative; top: -0.5em; vertical-align: baseline;">40</span> The more reserved Hanbalites searched for Ahmad ibn Hanbal’s opinion on this <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">hadīth</em>. We found evidence of his supposed opinion in <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">Ibtāl al-Ta</em><em style="box-sizing: inherit;">ʾ</em><em style="box-sizing: inherit;">wīlāt</em>, a thematic Hadith compilation authored by the Hanbalite theologian and qadi Abu Yaʿla ibn al-Farraʾ (d. 1066; he was also the father of the biographer Ibn Abi Yaʿla). Abu Yaʿla dedicated a lengthy chapter in his book to the efforts of the Hanbalite <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">muhaddithūn</em> to validate the <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">hadīth</em> attributed to Mujahid. <strong style="box-sizing: inherit; font-weight: bold;">Thus, the Hanbalites claimed that Ahmad ibn Hanbal himself ordered his disciples to transmit the anthropomorphic version attributed to Mujahid in the exact wording as it was received</strong>. The Hanbalites further claimed that Ahmad ibn Hanbal believed that the anthropomorphic <strong style="box-sizing: inherit; font-weight: bold;">version should have been attributed to Mujahid’s teacher, the <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">sahābī </em>Ibn </strong><strong style="box-sizing: inherit; font-weight: bold;">ʿ</strong><strong style="box-sizing: inherit; font-weight: bold;">Abbas</strong>.<span style="box-sizing: inherit; font-size: 13.5px; line-height: 0; position: relative; top: -0.5em; vertical-align: baseline;">41</span> These two claims do not correspond with the fact that the <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">hadīth</em> attributed to Mujahid was not included in the <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">Musnad </em>of Ahmad ibn Hanbal, the canonical Hadith compilation of the Hanbalites. Furthermore, Ahmad ibn Hanbal is not mentioned in the list of some thirty early Hanbalite scholars who professed their support for the anthropomorphic version attributed to Mujahid. These names were assembled by Abu Bakr al-Marwazi himself.<span style="box-sizing: inherit; font-size: 13.5px; line-height: 0; position: relative; top: -0.5em; vertical-align: baseline;">42 </span>It is reasonable to assume that if al-Marawzi thought that Ahmad ibn Hanbal had supported this <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">hadīth</em>, or merely acknowledged its existence, he would have mentioned Ahmad ibn Hanbal at the top of the list.<span style="box-sizing: inherit; font-size: 13.5px; line-height: 0; position: relative; top: -0.5em; vertical-align: baseline;">43</span></p><p style="box-sizing: inherit; margin: 1em 0px 1.1em;">The debate about the anthropomorphic version did not remain a theoretical issue. The <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">hadīth</em> became a major component in the political agenda of the Hanbalites. In the year 922 (this is an approximate chronology, as the historical sources do not provide specific details about the following occurrence), <strong style="box-sizing: inherit; font-weight: bold;">a group of Hanbalites attacked the illustrious historian and Quran exegete Abu Ja</strong><strong style="box-sizing: inherit; font-weight: bold;">ʿ</strong><strong style="box-sizing: inherit; font-weight: bold;">far Muhammad ibn Jarir al-Tabari (d. 923) in Baghdad</strong>. Al-Tabari, as one of the sources claimed, <strong style="box-sizing: inherit; font-weight: bold;">refused to accept the anthropomorphic version attributed to Mujahid and had the audacity to reject this <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">hadīth</em> in public</strong>.<span style="box-sizing: inherit; font-size: 13.5px; line-height: 0; position: relative; top: -0.5em; vertical-align: baseline;">44</span></p><p style="box-sizing: inherit; margin: 1em 0px 1.1em;">In his great exegesis of the Quran, al-Tabari implanted subtle references to his rejection of the <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">hadīth</em> attributed to Mujahid: he first established that ‘the majority of the scholars’ (<em style="box-sizing: inherit;">akthar ahl al-</em><em style="box-sizing: inherit;">ʿ</em><em style="box-sizing: inherit;">ilm</em>) believed that the ‘honourable station’ was Muhammad’s intercession for the people on the Day of Resurrection,<span style="box-sizing: inherit; font-size: 13.5px; line-height: 0; position: relative; top: -0.5em; vertical-align: baseline;">45 </span>by quoting a dozen <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">ahādīth</em> to prove his point.<span style="box-sizing: inherit; font-size: 13.5px; line-height: 0; position: relative; top: -0.5em; vertical-align: baseline;">46</span> In the next phase, al-Tabari remarked that ‘others’ (<em style="box-sizing: inherit;">ākharūn</em>) claimed that the ‘honourable station’ meant that God promised the Prophet that He would make the Prophet sit on the throne, next to God.<span style="box-sizing: inherit; font-size: 13.5px; line-height: 0; position: relative; top: -0.5em; vertical-align: baseline;">47</span> Thereafter, al-Tabari quoted ten anthropomorphic variants of the <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">hadīth </em>attributed to Mujahid.<span style="box-sizing: inherit; font-size: 13.5px; line-height: 0; position: relative; top: -0.5em; vertical-align: baseline;">48</span> As a rule, al-Tabari did not hesitate throughout his work of exegesis to accept certain <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">ahādīth</em> as authentic and reject others. Nonetheless, he was extremely cautious in the case of the <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">hadīth</em> attributed to Mujahid: Al-Tabari declared that there is no way to refute the authenticity of the <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">hadīth</em> attributed to Mujahid, neither by locating some textual evidence (khabar) nor by applying rational reasoning (<em style="box-sizing: inherit;">nazar</em>).<span style="box-sizing: inherit; font-size: 13.5px; line-height: 0; position: relative; top: -0.5em; vertical-align: baseline;">49 </span>This saying is far from the enthusiastic declarations of the Hanbalites support for this <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">hadīth</em>’s veracity. We note that the Hanbalites declared that the sceptics who did not accept this <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">hadīth</em> were heretics. However, in the severe circumstances in which al-Tabari lived in Baghdad (he was forced to stay at home, while visitors were prevented from visiting him),<span style="box-sizing: inherit; font-size: 13.5px; line-height: 0; position: relative; top: -0.5em; vertical-align: baseline;">50</span> al-Tabari seemed to have no other choice but to issue his lukewarm support of this <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">hadīth</em>’s authenticity.</p><p style="box-sizing: inherit; margin: 1em 0px 1.1em;">In 929, strife arose between the Hanbalite supporters of Abu Bakr al-Marwazi and ‘a group of commoners’ (<em style="box-sizing: inherit;">tā</em><em style="box-sizing: inherit;">ʾ</em><em style="box-sizing: inherit;">ifa min ’l-</em><em style="box-sizing: inherit;">ʿ</em><em style="box-sizing: inherit;">āmma</em>) in Baghdad. This strife, <strong style="box-sizing: inherit; font-weight: bold;">which soon escalated into riots (<em style="box-sizing: inherit;">fitna</em>)</strong> was ignited because of an argument about Q. 17:79, and ‘an honourable station’. Relying on earlier sources, <strong style="box-sizing: inherit; font-weight: bold;">the Damascene historian Ibn Kathir (d. 1373) reported on these riots</strong> in his monumental chronicle <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">al-Bidāya wa’l-Nihāya</em>. Ibn Kathir’s report is quite odd, because in it the ‘commoners’ claimed that ‘an honourable station’ was ‘the great intercession’, <strong style="box-sizing: inherit; font-weight: bold;">while the Hanbalites held their traditional position about Muhammad’s sitting on the throne</strong>. According to Ibn Kathir, <strong style="box-sizing: inherit; font-weight: bold;">the riots resulted in the deaths of an unspecified number of rioters</strong>. Ibn Kathir adds his opinion that according to <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">Sahīh al-Bukhāri</em>, the canonical Hadith compilation, ‘an honourable station’ was indeed the great intercession.<span style="box-sizing: inherit; font-size: 13.5px; line-height: 0; position: relative; top: -0.5em; vertical-align: baseline;">51</span> It seems that by determining that there was only one possible interpretation of ‘an honourable station’, and that this interpretation was not the one favoured by the Hanbalites, Ibn Kathir (who was considered an indirect disciple of the Hanbalite Ibn Qayyim al-Jawziyya) expressed his reservation about the behaviour of the tenth-century Hanbalites and their choice of texts to venerate. Other authors of the Mamluk period, <strong style="box-sizing: inherit; font-weight: bold;">like the Shafi</strong><strong style="box-sizing: inherit; font-weight: bold;">ʿ</strong><strong style="box-sizing: inherit; font-weight: bold;">ite Ibn Hajar al-</strong><strong style="box-sizing: inherit; font-weight: bold;">ʿ</strong><strong style="box-sizing: inherit; font-weight: bold;">Asqalani (d. 1449)</strong>, who in fact was inclined towards Ashʿarite theology, <strong style="box-sizing: inherit; font-weight: bold;">accepted the <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">hadīth</em> attributed to Mujahid</strong>.<span style="box-sizing: inherit; font-size: 13.5px; line-height: 0; position: relative; top: -0.5em; vertical-align: baseline;">52</span> <strong style="box-sizing: inherit; font-weight: bold;">Ibn Hajar even harshly condemned a later rationalistic scholar who refuted this hadīth</strong>.<span style="box-sizing: inherit; font-size: 13.5px; line-height: 0; position: relative; top: -0.5em; vertical-align: baseline;">53</span> The admission of the anthropomorphic version to the traditionalistic canon was therefore fully accomplished in the fifteenth century. Whatever it was, the view expressed in <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">Tafsir</em> did nothing to assuage Hanbalite opposition to him which appears to have had deeper roots than merely disagreement about a catchy slogan.</p><p style="box-sizing: inherit; margin: 1em 0px 1.1em;">The arguments marshalled by Tabari <strong style="box-sizing: inherit; font-weight: bold;">for the purpose of making Mujahid’s tradition admissible</strong> were derived from speculative theology and show him adept in its ways of thinking and debating. The basic issue, as he sees it, is the problem of contiguity (<em style="box-sizing: inherit;">mumassah</em>). It had its proper place in physics but was transferred to theology by religious thinkers<span style="box-sizing: inherit; font-size: 13.5px; line-height: 0; position: relative; top: -0.5em; vertical-align: baseline;">289</span> Al-Ash’ari (ca 260324/873(41-935(6)), who lived most of his life in al-Basrah and was but a generation removed from Tabari, considered the matter important enough to refer to it in his discussion of anthropomorphism (<em style="box-sizing: inherit;">tajsim</em>). <strong style="box-sizing: inherit; font-weight: bold;">God is not upon the Throne, except in the sense that He is above it but does not touch it</strong>. According to Hisham b. al-Hakam, <strong style="box-sizing: inherit; font-weight: bold;">God’s location is in one specific place (<em style="box-sizing: inherit;">fi makan dun makan</em>). His place is the Throne, and He is in touch with it. The Throne encompasses and delimits Him. Another view holds that the Creator fills the Throne and is in touch with it</strong>. At this point, <strong style="box-sizing: inherit; font-weight: bold;">al-Ash’ari adds that some hadith scholars hold that the Throne is not filled by Him and that He (is thus able to) SEAT HIS PROPHET WITH HIMSELF ON THE THRONE</strong>.<span style="box-sizing: inherit; font-size: 13.5px; line-height: 0; position: relative; top: -0.5em; vertical-align: baseline;">290</span> Tabari considers the problem of God completely filling the Throne. He remarks on His contiguity and finds that only three possibilities apply to it. For him, however, <strong style="box-sizing: inherit; font-weight: bold;">the crucial point that must be made is that God’s seating of Muhammad on the Throne, with or without Himself, does not imply divinity (“lordship” <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">rububiyyah</em>) for the Prophet or deny his status as a human being (“servantship” <em style="box-sizing: inherit;">‘ubudiyyah</em>)</strong>. In fact, <strong style="box-sizing: inherit; font-weight: bold;">the implied hint at Muhammadan divinity</strong> would appear to be the most objectionable feature of Mujahid’s tradition. In touching upon this aspect, <strong style="box-sizing: inherit; font-weight: bold;">Tabari comes close to the possible reason why Mujahid might have made this seemingly un-Islamic statement. Christianity speaks of the Son not only as sitting on a throne but also of some mysterious being as sitting together with the Father in His Throne (Rev. 3:21)</strong>. Even in remote Mecca, Mujahid could have heard about these views <strong style="box-sizing: inherit; font-weight: bold;">or seen one of the many representations of the Trinity or the enthroned Christ</strong>.<span style="box-sizing: inherit; font-size: 13.5px; line-height: 0; position: relative; top: -0.5em; vertical-align: baseline;">291</span> He may very well have felt that Muhammad should be similarly distinguished as was the prophet of Christianity.

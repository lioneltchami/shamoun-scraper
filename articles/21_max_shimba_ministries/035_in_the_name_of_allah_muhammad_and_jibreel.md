# In the name of Allah, Muhammad, and Jibreel

<p></p><h2>WHY THE QURAN FORBIDS</h2><p><strong>“In the name of Allah, Muhammad, and Jibreel”</strong><br/>— and WHY JESUS COMMANDED<br/><strong>“In the name of the Father, the Son, and the Holy Spirit”</strong></p><p>This contrast exposes a <strong>decisive theological divide</strong> between Islam and Christianity.</p><hr/><h2>1. Why the Quran PROHIBITS saying</h2><p><strong>“In the name of Allah, Muhammad, and Jibreel”</strong></p><p>The Quran absolutely forbids invoking anyone alongside Allah.</p><blockquote><p><strong>“Do not call upon anyone with Allah.”</strong><br/>(Quran 72:18)</p></blockquote><p>Why?</p><p>Because <strong>to invoke someone “in the name of” is to attribute divine authority</strong>.<br/>In Islamic theology:</p><ul><li><p>Allah alone is God</p></li><li><p>Muhammad is only a messenger</p></li><li><p>Jibreel is only an angel</p></li></ul><p>Therefore, to say <em>“In the name of Allah, Muhammad, and Jibreel”</em> would be <strong>shirk</strong>—making Muhammad and Jibreel <strong>co-equal with God</strong>.</p><p>Islam understands something crucial here:</p><blockquote><p><strong>Sharing the divine “name” = sharing divine identity</strong></p></blockquote><p>That is precisely why the Quran forbids it.</p><hr/><h2>2. Now the decisive question:</h2><h3>Why did Jesus command THIS?</h3><blockquote><p><strong>“Go therefore and make disciples of all nations,<br/>baptizing them in the name of the Father<br/>and of the Son<br/>and of the Holy Spirit.”</strong><br/>(Matthew 28:19)</p></blockquote><p>Not <strong>names</strong> (plural).<br/>But <strong>NAME</strong> (singular).</p><p>This is not accidental.<br/>This is not poetic.<br/>This is not symbolic.</p><p>It is <strong>ontological</strong>.</p><hr/><h2>3. Jesus did what the Quran forbids — and did it deliberately</h2><p>If Jesus were merely a prophet like Muhammad, this command would be <strong>blasphemy</strong> by Islamic standards.</p><p>Because Jesus:</p><ul><li><p>Places <strong>Himself (the Son)</strong> inside the divine Name</p></li><li><p>Places the <strong>Holy Spirit</strong> inside the same divine Name</p></li><li><p>Shares that Name with the <strong>Father</strong></p></li></ul><p>Exactly what Islam says <strong>no creature may ever do</strong>.</p><p>Yet Jesus does it without apology, explanation, or fear.</p><p>Why?</p><p>Because <strong>He is not a creature</strong>.</p><hr/><h2>4. The unavoidable conclusion</h2><p>Islam says:</p><blockquote><p>“You cannot say ‘in the name of Allah and anyone else’ because that would make them God.”</p></blockquote><p>Christianity says:</p><blockquote><p>“Baptize in the name of the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit.”</p></blockquote><p>Therefore, one of two things must be true:</p><h3>Either:</h3><p>Jesus committed the greatest possible act of shirk…</p><h3>Or:</h3><p><strong>The Father, the Son, and the Holy Spirit share the one divine essence.</strong></p><p>There is no middle ground.</p><hr/><h2>5. The Trinity is not a later invention</h2><p>It is <strong>declared by Jesus Himself</strong></p><p>Jesus did not say:</p><ul><li><p>“In the name of God and His prophet”</p></li><li><p>“In the name of God and His angel”</p></li><li><p>“In the name of God alone”</p></li></ul><p>He said:</p><blockquote><p><strong>ONE NAME</strong><br/><strong>THREE PERSONS</strong></p></blockquote><p>This is <strong>Trinity</strong>:</p><ul><li><p>Not three gods</p></li><li><p>Not one person playing three roles</p></li><li><p>But <strong>one God, eternally revealed as Father, Son, and Holy Spirit</strong></p></li></ul><hr/><h2>6. Final challenge</h2><p>Islam unintentionally proves the Trinity by its own logic.</p><p>Because Islam is correct about one thing:</p><blockquote><p><strong>To share the divine Name is to share divinity.</strong></p></blockquote><p>And Jesus shared it.</p><p>Boldly. Publicly. Authoritatively.</p><p><strong>Not as a prophet — but as God.</strong></p><hr/><p><br/></p>

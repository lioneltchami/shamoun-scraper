Islamic jurisprudence does not recognize “extremism” as deviation when actions are rooted in Qur’an, Hadith, and the example of Muhammad. Condemning Violence While Defending the Texts That Command It</strong></h3><p>The so-called moderate Muslim occupies a peculiar position: condemning violent outcomes while refusing to confront violent sources. Instead, critics are accused of “misunderstanding context,” a context that somehow never seems to invalidate the text.</p><p>This creates a moral paradox: <strong>violence is rejected in practice but preserved in principle</strong>.</p><p>History teaches us that ideas preserved in principle eventually return in practice.</p><hr/><h3><strong>3. The Strategic Silence of Moderation</strong></h3><p>Moderate Islam is loud in condemnation and silent in reform. It speaks fluently to Western media but hesitates before its own mosques, scholars, and jurists. Public outrage is carefully calibrated; doctrinal clarity is endlessly postponed.</p><p>Why? Because confronting the foundations risks unraveling the entire structure.</p><p>Christianity survived reform because its center is Christ, not conquest. Institutional Infiltration Without Theological Transparency</strong></h3><p>Across Western democracies, Muslims identified as “moderate” occupy positions of influence—politics, military, academia, media, and human rights organizations. The concern arises when <strong>loyalty is demanded outwardly but ambiguity is maintained inwardly</strong>.</p><p>When questioned about Sharia, apostasy laws, blasphemy, or Islamic governance, answers become evasive, conditional, or deferred. The problem is not participation—it is <strong>duplicity</strong>.</p><p>A worldview that cannot speak plainly about its end goals is not misunderstood; it is <strong>strategic</strong>.</p><hr/><h3><strong>5. This is precisely why it terrifies—and why it exposes the fiction of moderation.</p><p>Radicals are not theological rebels; they are <strong>literalists</strong>.</p><p>Moderates, by contrast, benefit from the radicals’ actions while disowning them rhetorically, allowing the system to advance without accountability.</p><hr/><h3><strong>6. Christianity’s Uncomfortable Advantage: Truthfulness</strong></h3><p>Christian theology places God’s self-revelation in Jesus Christ, whose kingdom is not advanced by the sword, whose disciples are forbidden from coercion, and whose truth survives scrutiny. Christianity does not fear examination because its center is moral consistency.</p><p>Islam fears scrutiny because its foundations are inseparable from power.</p><hr/><h3><strong>Conclusion: Enough Theater</strong></h3><p>The radical–moderate narrative is no longer convincing to serious historians, theologians, or informed observers.

One commonly cited passage is Isaiah 29:12:</p><blockquote><p><em>"Then the book was given to a man who had no understanding, and he said, ‘Please read it.’ And he said, ‘I have no understanding.’"</em></p></blockquote><p>Muslims interpret this verse as evidence that Muhammad, who was reportedly illiterate, was prophesied in the Bible. However, a careful examination of Isaiah 29 in its entirety reveals a broader context that challenges this interpretation.</p><hr/><h2>1. The Textual Context of Isaiah 29</h2><p>Chapter 29 addresses <strong>Ariel (Jerusalem) and the people of Judah</strong>, pronouncing judgment due to their rebellion against God’s laws (Isaiah 29:1–10). The chapter describes a people spiritually blind and deaf to God’s word, including even prophets and seers. Verses 11–12 emphasize that both the learned and the unlearned cannot comprehend God’s message:</p><blockquote><p><em>“All the visions have become to you like the words of a sealed book… Then the book was given to someone who has no understanding, and he said, ‘Please read this’; and he said, ‘I have no understanding.’”</em></p></blockquote><p>This context clearly situates the passage as <strong>judgment on Judah</strong>, not as a prophecy of a future prophet.</p><hr/><h2>2. Islamic Interpretation</h2><p>Islamic scholars often argue:</p><ol><li><p>The “book” in Isaiah represents the Qur’an.</p></li><li><p>The “unlearned person” is Muhammad, who could not read prior to receiving revelation.</p></li></ol><p>This interpretation is theological, not based on historical or literary analysis of the biblical text. It draws parallels between the <em>illiteracy of Muhammad</em> and the figure described in Isaiah 29:12.</p><hr/><h2>3. Classical Biblical Scholarship Perspective</h2><ol><li><p><strong>Historical context:</strong> Isaiah lived in the 8th century BCE, centuries before Muhammad.</p></li><li><p><strong>Literary context:</strong> The passage describes Jerusalem’s rebellion and spiritual blindness, using metaphorical language.</p></li><li><p><strong>No explicit reference:</strong> There is no mention of Arabia, Muhammad, or a prophet outside Israel.</p></li></ol><p><strong>Conclusion:</strong> The text does not support the claim that Muhammad is prophesied in Isaiah 29.</p><hr/><h2>4. Challenging Questions for Critical Thinking</h2><p>To deepen understanding and test the validity of interpretations, consider these questions:</p><ol><li><p><strong>Historical Challenge:</strong> How can a passage written in the 8th century BCE, concerning Jerusalem and Judah, be verified as a prophecy about someone living in 7th century CE Arabia?</p></li><li><p><strong>Textual Challenge:</strong> Does Isaiah 29:12 explicitly mention Arabia, Muhammad, or the Qur’an? If not, how valid is the claim of prophecy?</p></li><li><p><strong>Contextual Challenge:</strong> Considering the broader context of Isaiah 29 (judgment on Jerusalem), how does the Islamic interpretation account for verses 1–11 and 13–24?</p></li><li><p><strong>Logical Challenge:</strong> If the “book” is the Qur’an, and the unlearned man is Muhammad, why does the text describe judgment and spiritual blindness rather than the reception of divine truth?</p></li><li><p><strong>Comparative Challenge:</strong> Are there other biblical passages where prophecy is clearly fulfilled in someone outside Israel? How does Isaiah 29 compare to these examples?</p></li><li><p><strong>Epistemological Challenge:</strong> Can theological interpretation alone (without historical or textual support) be considered sufficient evidence for prophecy?</p></li><li><p><strong>Verification Challenge:</strong> What sources outside the Islamic tradition (archaeological, historical, linguistic) could confirm or contradict the claim?</p></li></ol><hr/><h2>6. Conclusion</h2><p>Isaiah 29:12, when read in context, does not support the Islamic claim that Muhammad was prophesied in the Bible. The passage is part of a broader declaration of judgment on Judah, emphasizing spiritual blindness and the inability to comprehend God’s word.</p><p>Muslims’ interpretation relies on theological symbolism, which lacks historical or literary verification. Believers are encouraged to carefully examine scriptural context and engage critically with such claims.</p><hr/><p><strong>Shalom,</strong><br/>Dr. Maxwell Shimba<br/>Servant of Jesus Christ, the Most High God (Titus 2:13)</p><hr/><p><br/></p>

# A Critical Examination of the Moon-God Theory, Pre-Islamic Arabia, and Qur’anic Theology

<p> </p><h2>A Critical Examination of the Moon-God Theory, Pre-Islamic Arabia, and Qur’anic Theology</h2><p><strong>By Shimba Theological Institute</strong></p><hr/><h2>Introduction</h2><p>Among critics of Islam, a recurring claim asserts that <em>Allah was originally a female deity</em>, a moon goddess who later underwent a theological “gender reassignment” into a masculine monotheistic god. This claim is often linked to pre-Islamic Arabian religion, the so-called “moon-god theory,” and the Qur’anic mention of three female figures—<strong>al-Lāt, al-ʿUzzā, and Manāt</strong>.</p><p>This article does not merely repeat popular polemics. Instead, it <strong>critically examines</strong> the historical, linguistic, archaeological, and theological claims behind the assertion and evaluates whether they withstand academic scrutiny.</p><hr/><h2>1. Pre-Islamic Arabia: Polytheism and the Kaaba</h2><p>Pre-Islamic Arabia was unquestionably <strong>polytheistic</strong>. According to Islamic and non-Islamic sources alike, the Kaaba housed numerous idols representing tribal deities. Among them were:</p><ul><li><p><strong>al-Lāt</strong></p></li><li><p><strong>al-ʿUzzā</strong></p></li><li><p><strong>Manāt</strong></p></li></ul><p>The Qur’an itself acknowledges their worship:</p><blockquote><p><em>“Have you considered al-Lāt and al-ʿUzzā? And Manāt, the third, the other?”</em><br/>— Qur’an 53:19–20</p></blockquote><p>Importantly, the Qur’an explicitly <strong>rejects</strong> the belief that these goddesses were daughters of Allah:</p><blockquote><p><em>“Is the male for you and for Him the female?”</em><br/>— Qur’an 53:21</p></blockquote><p>This polemic indicates that Muhammad confronted <strong>existing pagan theology</strong>, not that Allah emerged from it.</p><hr/><h2>2. The Moon-God Theory: Origins and Problems</h2><p>The “Allah as a moon god” theory gained popularity through 19th–20th century Orientalist speculation and later Christian apologetics.</p><h3>Key Problems with the Theory:</h3><ol><li><p><strong>No inscriptional evidence</strong> proves that Allah was ever a moon deity.</p></li><li><p>Arabian moon gods (e.g., <em>Sin</em>, <em>Wadd</em>) are <strong>named and distinct</strong> from Allah.</p></li><li><p>The Qur’an <strong>condemns celestial worship</strong>, including the moon:</p></li></ol><blockquote><p><em>“Do not prostrate to the sun or to the moon…”</em><br/>— Qur’an 41:37</p></blockquote><p>If Allah were originally a moon god, this condemnation would be theologically incoherent.</p><hr/><h2>3. Linguistic Claim: Is “Allah” Feminine?</h2><p>A common argument claims that <em>Allah is linguistically feminine</em> because the name ends with <strong>ه (hāʾ)</strong>. This argument is <strong>linguistically incorrect</strong>.</p><h3>Arabic Grammar Facts:</h3><ul><li><p>Feminine nouns in Arabic are typically marked by <strong>tāʾ marbūṭa (ة)</strong>, <em>not</em> hāʾ (ه).</p></li><li><p>The word <strong>Allah (الله)</strong> does <strong>not</strong> contain a feminine ending.</p></li><li><p>Classical Arabic grammar treats <em>Allah</em> as <strong>grammatically masculine</strong>, though semantically <strong>transcendent of gender</strong>.</p></li></ul><p>The Qur’an itself denies biological gender to God:</p><blockquote><p><em>“He neither begets nor is born.”</em><br/>— Qur’an 112:3</p></blockquote><p>Thus, the linguistic argument for Allah’s femininity <strong>fails</strong>.</p><hr/><h2>4. Crescent Moon Symbol: Theology or Politics?</h2><p>The crescent moon commonly seen on flags of Muslim-majority nations is often cited as evidence of moon worship.</p><h3>Historical Reality:</h3><ul><li><p>The crescent became associated with Islam <strong>centuries after Muhammad</strong>, particularly under the <strong>Ottoman Empire</strong>.</p></li><li><p>The Qur’an never endorses the crescent as a religious symbol.</p></li><li><p>Early Islam used <strong>no universal religious iconography</strong>.</p></li></ul><p>Therefore, the crescent moon is <strong>political and cultural</strong>, not theological.</p><hr/><h2>5. Were Allah’s “Daughters” Evidence of a Goddess Origin?</h2><p>The Qur’an records pagan Arabs calling angels or deities “daughters of Allah,” but this is portrayed as <strong>blasphemy</strong>, not doctrine.</p><blockquote><p><em>“They have attributed to Him daughters—exalted is He.”</em><br/>— Qur’an 16:57</p></blockquote><p>Rather than proving Allah was once female, the text demonstrates Islam’s <strong>rejection of goddess theology</strong>.</p><hr/><h2>6. Biblical and Theological Contrast</h2><p>From a Judeo-Christian perspective, God is:</p><ul><li><p><strong>Personal</strong></p></li><li><p><strong>Relational</strong></p></li><li><p><strong>Consistently revealed across history</strong></p></li></ul><p>The Bible never presents God as evolving from a goddess, nor as absorbing pagan deities.</p><blockquote><p><em>“Before Me no god was formed, nor shall there be any after Me.”</em><br/>— Isaiah 43:10</p></blockquote><p>This sharply contrasts with Islam’s historical emergence within a polytheistic environment, a tension the Qur’an itself reflects.</p><hr/><h2>Conclusion</h2><p>The claim that <em>“Allah was originally a woman”</em> collapses under <strong>historical, linguistic, and theological analysis</strong>.</p><p>What the evidence actually shows is:</p><ul><li><p>Pre-Islamic Arabia was polytheistic</p></li><li><p>Islam emerged by <strong>rejecting</strong> goddess worship</p></li><li><p>Allah was <strong>not</strong> a moon deity</p></li><li><p>The linguistic argument for femininity is incorrect</p></li><li><p>Crescent symbolism is post-Qur’anic</p></li></ul><p>For serious theological critique, <strong>accuracy is more powerful than sensationalism</strong>. Effective apologetics must confront Islam at its strongest points, not its weakest caricatures.</p><hr/><h2>References</h2><ul><li><p>القرآن الكريم (The Qur’an)</p></li><li><p>Ibn Hisham, <em>Sirat Rasul Allah</em></p></li><li><p>W.

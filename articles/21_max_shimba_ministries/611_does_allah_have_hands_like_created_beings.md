# Does Allah Have Hands Like Created Beings?

<p> Title: Does Allah Have Hands Like Created Beings? An Analytical Examination of Islamic Texts</p>
<p>Author: Dr. Maxwell Shimba</p>
<p>Published by: Max Shimba Ministries Org.</p>
<p>Date: August 3, 2015</p>
<p><strong>Introduction</strong><br/>
This paper explores a provocative theological inquiry: Does Allah, as depicted in Islamic scripture and tradition, possess hands and eyes similar to those of created beings? By examining select Qur'anic verses and Hadith narrations, and juxtaposing them with Islamic theological positions, this study seeks to critically analyze whether the anthropomorphic descriptions in Islamic texts imply a corporeal deity.</p>
<p><strong>Qur'anic Passages Referencing the Hands of Allah</strong><br/>
One of the primary passages frequently cited is:</p>
<p><em>Az-Zumar (39:67):</em><br/>
"They did not appraise Allah with true appraisal, while the earth entirely will be within His grip on the Day of Resurrection, and the heavens will be folded in His right hand. Exalted is He and high above what they associate with Him."</p>
<p>Additionally, <em>Al-Ma'idah (5:64)</em> states:<br/>
"The Jews say, 'Allah's hand is chained.' Chained are their hands, and cursed are they for what they say. Rather, both His hands are extended; He spends however He wills."</p>
<p>Other relevant verses include:</p>
<p><em>Yasin (36:83):</em> "So exalted is He in whose hand is the realm of all things, and to Him you will be returned."</p>
<p><em>Ali 'Imran (3:26):</em> "Say, 'O Allah, Owner of Sovereignty, You give sovereignty to whom You will and You take sovereignty away from whom You will. You honor whom You will and You humble whom You will. In Your hand is [all] good. Indeed, You are over all things competent.'"</p>
<p><em>Sad (38:75):</em> "[Allah] said, 'O Iblis, what prevented you from prostrating to that which I created with My hands? Are you arrogant, or are you among the exalted?'"</p>
<p><strong>Hadith Evidence</strong><br/>
According to narrations in Sunni compilations, Abu Huraira reported that the Prophet Muhammad said:</p>
<p>"Allah created Adam with His own hands and wrote the Torah for Moses with His own hands."</p>
<p>Another hadith states:<br/>
"Allah descends to the nearest heaven and stretches out His two hands, saying…"</p>
<p><strong>Theological Commentary</strong><br/>
Islamic theologians from various schools—Ash'ari, Maturidi, Hanbali, and others—have engaged extensively with these anthropomorphic expressions. The traditional Sunni creed acknowledges Allah’s 'hands' and 'face' as mentioned in the Qur’an and Sunnah but asserts that these attributes are unique to Allah and bear no resemblance to human characteristics.</p>
<p>A popular theological analogy explains: A table and a person both have 'legs,' but the nature of these legs differs. Similarly, Allah's 'hands' are befitting His majesty and beyond human comprehension.</p>
<p><strong>Additional Qur'anic Descriptions: Eyes of Allah</strong><br/>
The Qur'an also references Allah's 'eyes':</p>
<p><em>Hud (11:37):</em> "And construct the ship under Our eyes and Our revelation…"</p>
<p>Another hadith states:<br/>
"The Dajjal is one-eyed, and your Lord is not one-eyed."</p>
<p>These descriptions, like those of Allah's 'hands,' are interpreted by theologians within the framework of <em>bilā kayf</em> (without asking how) and <em>tanzīh</em> (declaring Allah's incomparability).</p>
<p><strong>Do These Attributes Imply Corporeality?</strong><br/>
Max Shimba Ministries contends that anthropomorphic language, if taken literally, challenges Islamic claims of Allah's absolute transcendence. If Allah possesses 'hands' and 'eyes' in any form comparable to creation, this could imply corporeality.</p>
<p><strong>Conclusion</strong><br/>
This analysis reveals significant anthropomorphic language within the Qur’an and Hadith. Traditional Islamic theology resolves this tension through principles of <em>tanzīh</em> and <em>bilā kayf</em>, affirming the attributes without likening them to human attributes. However, literalist interpretations risk portraying Allah as a corporeal being, conflicting with Islamic assertions of divine incomparability.</p>

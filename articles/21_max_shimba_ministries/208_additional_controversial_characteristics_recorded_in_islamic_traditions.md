<p><strong>Additional Controversial Characteristics Recorded in Islamic Traditions</strong></p><h3><strong>1. He claimed revelations only when convenient</strong></h3><p>In several hadith, revelations appeared exactly when they solved his personal or political problems (e.g., Qur’an 33:37 regarding Zaynab).</p><h3><strong>2. He married his adopted son’s wife</strong></h3><p>The marriage to <strong>Zaynab bint Jahsh</strong>, formerly the wife of his adopted son Zayd, caused scandal even among his companions (Qur’an 33:37–40).</p><h3><strong>3. He allowed temporary marriages (Mut’ah)</strong></h3><p>Mut’ah (temporary sexual contracts) were practiced during Muhammad’s time, later banned by Umar, showing internal contradictions in Islamic law.</p><h3><strong>4. He approved the killing of critics</strong></h3><p>Biographical sources record the assassinations of poets and critics such as <strong>Ka’b bin al-Ashraf</strong> and <strong>Asma bint Marwan</strong>.</p><h3><strong>5. He ordered violent raids on caravans</strong></h3><p>Early Islamic expansion involved economic raids (ghazawat), including the raid on the Quraysh caravan that triggered the Battle of Badr.</p><h3><strong>6. He married multiple women for political reasons</strong></h3><p>His marriages often strengthened tribal alliances; this is acknowledged in classical Islamic scholarship.</p><h3><strong>7. He cursed entire groups of people</strong></h3><p>Hadith collections record him cursing groups such as women, Jews, poets, and tribes that opposed him.</p><h3><strong>8. He claimed special privileges to break moral laws</strong></h3><p>Islamic law gives Muhammad exceptions: more wives, exemption from certain restrictions, special allowances on marriage, etc.</p><h3><strong>9. He believed magic was cast on him</strong></h3><p>Sahih al‑Bukhari states that Muhammad believed he was magically bewitched and unable to distinguish reality.</p><h3><strong>10. He engaged in harsh punishments</strong></h3><p>Reports describe beheading, amputation, and severe retribution against opponents (e.g., the Banu Qurayza incident).</p><h3><strong>11. He spoke words not found in the Qur’an</strong></h3><p>Hadith record that verses were eaten by a sheep, others forgotten, others abrogated — raising questions of textual stability.</p><h3><strong>12. He allowed beating of women</strong></h3><p>Sahih Hadith record permission to strike a disobedient wife, though not severely.</p><h3><strong>13. He claimed the Qur’an was protected but also missing verses</strong></h3><p>Islamic tradition acknowledges missing or forgotten verses after his death, such as the verse of stoning.</p><h3><strong>14. He declared himself the final prophet based on personal testimony</strong></h3><p>No external witness or prophecy (from Torah or Gospel) confirms this claim; the testimony comes from Muhammad alone.</p><h3><strong>15. He allowed child concubinage with slave girls</strong></h3><p>Concubines like <strong>Maria the Copt</strong> were taken as part of war spoils, including sexual relations without formal marriage.</p><hr/><h2><br/></h2>

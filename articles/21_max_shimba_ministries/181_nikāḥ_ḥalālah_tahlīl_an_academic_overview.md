<p> </p><h1>Nikāḥ ḥalālah (tahlīl) — an academic overview</h1>
<p><em>By Dr. Maxwell Shimba, Shimba Theological Institute</em></p>
<h2>Abstract</h2>
<p>This short paper explains the Islamic legal rule commonly called <strong>nikāḥ ḥalālah</strong> (also <em>tahlīl</em>): the situation in which a woman who has become permanently divorced from her first husband (after a third, irrevocable <em>talaq</em>) may only remarry him if, after that final divorce, she lawfully marries another man and that marriage is consummated and then ends. The note summarizes the scriptural bases (Qurʾān and ḥadīth), the classical juristic rationale, the prohibition of <em>pre-arranged</em> or <em>manufactured</em> halālah, and ethical implications. Key primary sources from the hadith corpus are cited.</p>
<hr/>
<h2>1. Scriptural and textual basis</h2>
<p><strong>Qurʾānic text.</strong> The principal Qurʾānic verse is explicit: “And if he has divorced her (the third time), then she is not lawful unto him thereafter until she has married another husband.” (Qurʾān 2:230). This verse is the foundational legal text that creates the condition requiring an intervening marriage after an irrevocable third divorce. (<a href="https://previous.quran.com/2%3A230/tafsirs/en-tafsir-maarif-ul-quran?locale=en&amp;utm_source=chatgpt.com" title="Maarif-ul-Quran - 2:230 - english">Quran</a>)</p>
<p><strong>Prophetic practice and explanatory ḥadīth.</strong> The Prophet’s practice and sayings further explain what “another husband” and the required condition mean. Sahih reports narrated in collections such as Ṣaḥīḥ al-Bukhārī describe instances and rulings where the Prophet explained that the subsequent husband must consummate the marriage for the woman to become lawful again for her first husband. For example, Bukhārī records the Prophet’s reply that a wife who was divorced and then married another man can only return to her first husband if the second marriage was consummated in the normal way. (<a href="https://sunnah.com/bukhari%3A5261?utm_source=chatgpt.com" title="Sahih al-Bukhari 5261 - Divorce - كتاب الطلاق">Sunnah.com</a>)</p>
<hr/>
<h2>2. The classical juristic rationale</h2>
<p>Classical jurists (Hanafi, Shāfiʿī, Mālikī, and Ḥanbalī schools) read Qurʾān 2:230 together with the Prophet’s explanations to achieve two objectives:</p>
<ol>
<li>
<p><strong>Protection against abuse.</strong> The rule prevents husbands from treating pronouncements of divorce as a reversible threat (pronouncing and then rescinding talaq capriciously) to coerce wives. The third, irrevocable divorce with the intervening-marriage condition is a deterrent against such abuse.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Affirmation of marital sanctity.</strong> The requirement of a bona fide intervening marriage and consummation underscores that the woman cannot be made “lawful” again to her first husband by arrangement or artifice; rather a genuine change of marital status must occur. Classical manuals thus emphasize that the intervening marriage must be genuine and not a sham. (See classical commentaries on Kitāb al-Talaq and the works of jurists explaining Qurʾān 2:230.) (<a href="https://previous.quran.com/2%3A230/tafsirs/en-tafsir-maarif-ul-quran?locale=en&amp;utm_source=chatgpt.com" title="Maarif-ul-Quran - 2:230 - english">Quran</a>)</p>
</li>
</ol>
<hr/>
<h2>3. Prohibition of <em>pre-arranged</em> halālah (manufactured tahlīl)</h2>
<p>While the Qurʾān and Sunnah set the <em>condition</em> for returning after an irrevocable divorce, the Prophetic literature also contains clear condemnations of <strong>manufactured</strong> halālah — that is, when a man marries a divorced woman with the explicit intention of divorcing her solely so that she becomes lawful for her former husband. A hadith reported in Sunan Abī Dāwūd (and reported elsewhere) records the Prophet as saying, in reference to such conduct, “May Allah curse the <em>muhallil</em> and the <em>muhallal lahu</em>” — that is, the man who performs the intervening marriage as a trick and the man for whom this is being done. This hadith has been authenticated by many scholars and is widely used by jurists to declare pre-arranged tahlīl unlawful and morally condemned. (<a href="https://sunnah.com/abudawud%3A2076?utm_source=chatgpt.com" title="Sunan Abi Dawud 2076 - Marriage (Kitab Al-Nikah)">Sunnah.com</a>)</p>
<p>Scholarly fatwās and juristic texts therefore distinguish sharply between:</p>
<ul>
<li>
<p><strong>A lawful intervening marriage</strong> (entered sincerely, consummated, and not undertaken as a ruse), which makes remarriage to the first husband legally possible; and</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>A sham/contracted halālah</strong> (a marriage contracted with the explicit, pre-agreed purpose of making the woman lawful for her ex-husband), which is condemned and invalid as a means to circumvent the law. (<a href="https://islamqa.info/en/answers/109245?utm_source=chatgpt.com" title="Tahleel marriage is haram and invalid - Islam Question &amp; ...">Islam-QA</a>)</p>
</li>
</ul>

<h2>5. Practical and ethical considerations for contemporary application</h2>
<ol>
<li>
<p><strong>Do not arrange or instrumentalize another person.</strong> Islamic legal ethics and the explicit prophetic condemnation caution strongly against arranging an intervening marriage as a device to reverse an irrevocable divorce. Such behavior is described as sinful in the Prophetic traditions and undermines the moral aims of the law. (<a href="https://sunnah.com/abudawud%3A2076?utm_source=chatgpt.com" title="Sunan Abi Dawud 2076 - Marriage (Kitab Al-Nikah)">Sunnah.com</a>)</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Protect the vulnerable.</strong> In many contemporary contexts, women are at risk when husbands use talaq irresponsibly or when economic and social pressures push women into exploitative arrangements. Scholars emphasize protective measures: family counseling, legal reform where necessary, and community education about the consequences of rash divorces.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Authenticity of the second marriage matters.</strong> If, after a legitimate and consummated second marriage, that marriage ends (by divorce or death), reunification with the first husband is legally possible — provided the intervening marriage was entered into sincerely and not merely as a pretext. The law thus preserves an objective procedural reality (a genuine change of marital status) while discouraging circumvention. (<a href="https://www.pfandercenter.org/blog/halala-marriage?utm_source=chatgpt.com" title="'Halala' marriage">Pfander Center</a>)</p>
</li>
</ol>
<hr/>
<h2>6. Conclusion</h2>
<p>Nikāḥ ḥalālah as a legal institution is rooted in Qurʾān 2:230 and expounded in the Sunnah and classical jurisprudence: it permits a woman, after an irrevocable triple divorce, to be lawful again for her first husband only after a genuine interposing marriage that was consummated. However, the prophetic condemnations recorded in the ḥadīth literature make clear that <strong>pre-arranged</strong> or <strong>instrumental</strong> uses of such intervening marriages to circumvent the law are forbidden, morally condemned, and—according to the ḥadīth—cursed. Ethical application therefore requires both fidelity to the text and protection of human dignity.</p>

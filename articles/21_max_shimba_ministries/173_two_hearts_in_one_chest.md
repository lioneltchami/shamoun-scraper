<h1>“Two Hearts in One Chest?”</h1><h2>A Theological and Scientific Appraisal of Qur’an 33:4</h2><p><strong>By Dr. Maxwell Shimba — Shimba Theological Institute, New York, NY</strong></p><hr/><h3>Abstract</h3><p>Qur’an 33:4 states that God “has not placed two hearts in any person’s chest.” Read straightforwardly, the verse appears to make an empirical claim about human anatomy, while the classical exegetical tradition typically treats it as a rhetorical denial aimed at Arab idioms and certain pre-Islamic practices. This article evaluates the verse on two fronts: (1) its literary-historical intent and the range of traditional interpretations; and (2) the biological record on “two hearts,” from human anomalies and surgical cases to animals with multiple cardiac pumps. I then pose a series of theological and scientific questions intended to sharpen debate across confessional lines and to clarify what, exactly, the verse is—and is not—asserting.</p><hr/><h2>1) Text and Immediate Context</h2><p><strong>Qur’an 33:4 (Al-Aḥzāb)</strong> in a widely used modern English rendering reads:</p><blockquote>
<p>“Allah does not place two hearts in any person’s chest; nor does He regard your wives as your mothers [by your saying ‘you are as my mother’s back’], nor your adopted sons as your [biological] sons…” (<a href="https://quran.com/en/al-ahzab/4?utm_source=chatgpt.com" title="Surah Al-Ahzab - 4">Quran.com</a>)</p>
</blockquote><p>Classical and modern tafsīr link 33:4–5 to two reforms: (a) invalidating <strong>ẓihār</strong> (a pre-Islamic formula likening a wife to one’s mother to dissolve marital obligations) and (b) correcting <strong>adoption/naming</strong> customs associated with Zayd ibn Ḥāritha, whose case is further discussed in 33:37. (<a href="https://quranx.com/tafsirs/33.4?utm_source=chatgpt.com" title="Commentaries for 33.4 - Quran">QuranX</a>, <a href="https://islam.stackexchange.com/questions/34997/confusion-about-quran-qur-an-3337-and-334-5-and-adoption-ruling?utm_source=chatgpt.com" title="Confusion about Qur'an Qur'an 33:37 and 33:4-5 and ...">Islam Stack Exchange</a>, <a href="https://yaqeeninstitute.org/read/paper/the-prophets-marriage-to-zaynab-bint-jahsh?utm_source=chatgpt.com" title="The Prophet's ﷺ Marriage to Zaynab bint Jaḥsh">Yaqeen Institute for Islamic Research</a>)</p><p>Notably, <strong>al-Jalālayn</strong> says the opening clause refutes those who boasted they had “two hearts” (i.e., superior intellect/resolve), situating the line as a <strong>rhetorical denial</strong> rather than a literal foray into anatomy. Maudūdī’s <em>Tafhīm</em> likewise reads it as an emphatic negation placed alongside the reforms of ẓihār and adoption. (<a href="https://quranx.com/tafsirs/33.4?utm_source=chatgpt.com" title="Commentaries for 33.4 - Quran">QuranX</a>, <a href="https://surahquran.com/tafsir-english-aya-4-sora-33.html?utm_source=chatgpt.com" title="Surah Ahzab ayat 4 Tafsir Quran 33:4 - Ibn Kathir">Surah Quran</a>)</p><hr/><h2>2) Linguistic Considerations</h2><p>The verse says <strong>“God has not placed two hearts in any man/person (min rajulin) within his chest.”</strong> On its face, it universalizes to <em>any human</em>. Yet in idiomatic Arabic, “two hearts” functioned as a <strong>figure of speech</strong> for divided allegiance or an alleged mental superiority. Thus, the line plausibly means: <em>a person cannot be of two minds in ultimate loyalty,</em> dovetailing with the adjoining legal-ethical reforms. This reading is consistent with major tafsīr. (<a href="https://quranx.com/tafsirs/33.4?utm_source=chatgpt.com" title="Commentaries for 33.4 - Quran">QuranX</a>, <a href="https://quran.com/en/33%3A4/tafsirs/en-tafsir-maarif-ul-quran?utm_source=chatgpt.com" title="Tafsir Surah Al-Ahzab - 4">Quran.com</a>)</p><p>Still, because the verse uses <strong>concrete anatomical wording</strong>, questions arise about <strong>scope</strong>: Is the statement (a) purely idiomatic; (b) a generalization about natural human anatomy; (c) a theological axiom about undivided devotion; or (d) more than one of these at once?</p><hr/><h2>3) Biology: What Do We Know about “Two Hearts”?</h2><h3>3.1 Humans</h3><ul>
<li>
<p><strong>Naturally occurring two complete hearts</strong> in a single human being (from conception) is not a recognized viable phenotype in medical literature. Congenital anomalies can produce complex hearts (incorrect chambering, duplication of parts, situs disorders), but not a fully duplicated, independent second heart. (<a href="https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIR.0000000000000606?utm_source=chatgpt.com" title="Genetic Basis for Congenital Heart Disease: Revisited">Circulation Journals</a>, <a href="https://tp.amegroups.org/article/view/77549/html?utm_source=chatgpt.com" title="Genetics of congenital heart disease: a narrative review of ...">Translational Pediatrics</a>)</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Conjoined twins</strong> can present with two hearts within a shared thoracic complex, but that is <strong>two individuals</strong> biologically. (This article focuses on single individuals.)</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Heterotopic heart transplantation</strong> (a “piggy-back” procedure) can result in <strong>one patient living with two hearts</strong> (the native and the donor heart) functioning simultaneously for years or decades—a medical reality since the late 20th century. Clinicians and case reports explicitly describe “living with two hearts.” (<a href="https://news.temple.edu/in-the-media/case-man-two-hearts?utm_source=chatgpt.com" title="The case of the man with two hearts | Temple Now">Temple Now</a>, <a href="https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9871692/?utm_source=chatgpt.com" title="To hit a home run as a heterotopic heart recipient—living ...">PMC</a>, <a href="https://academic.oup.com/ehjcr/article/7/1/ytac473/6918734?utm_source=chatgpt.com" title="Double heart after heart transplantation - Oxford Academic">Oxford Academic</a>)</p>
</li>
</ul><p><strong>Implication:</strong> If 33:4 were read as a strict, timeless anatomical claim, heterotopic transplants form an interesting boundary case: <em>a single person with two functional hearts in one chest by medical intervention.</em></p><h3>3.2 Animals</h3><p>Multiple “hearts” (or heart-like pumps) do occur in other species:</p><ul>
<li>
<p><strong>Octopuses and squids</strong>: typically <strong>three hearts</strong>—one systemic heart and two branchial hearts that pump blood across the gills. (<a href="https://www.nhm.ac.uk/discover/octopuses-keep-surprising-us-here-are-eight-examples-how.html?utm_source=chatgpt.com" title="Octopuses keep surprising us - here are eight examples how">Natural History Museum</a>)</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Hagfish</strong>: one principal heart plus several accessory pumps (some accounts enumerate <strong>up to five “hearts”</strong> when counting auxiliaries). (<a href="https://www.thelancet.com/journals/lancet/article/PIIS0140-6736%2805%2960870-7/fulltext?utm_source=chatgpt.com" title="Multiple hearts in animals other than Barosaurus">The Lancet</a>, <a href="https://www.labroots.com/trending/cell-and-molecular-biology/14540/understanding-zombie-heart-hagfish?srsltid=AfmBOoqXEklZFDEXu7FVMH8bNvN4COTjk4Nmnnrg_uEHpUlG24g_0vCz&amp;utm_source=chatgpt.com" title="Understanding the Zombie Heart of the Hagfish">Labroots</a>)</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Earthworms</strong>: often misdescribed as having “five hearts,” but these are <strong>aortic arches</strong>—contractile vessels, not homologous to a vertebrate heart. (<a href="https://www.ck12.org/flexi/life-science/segmented-worms/are-aortic-arches-the-hearts-of-earthworms/?utm_source=chatgpt.com" title="Flexi answers - Are aortic arches the hearts of earthworms?">CK-12 Foundation</a>, <a href="https://a-z-animals.com/blog/how-many-hearts-does-a-worm-have/?utm_source=chatgpt.com" title="How Many Hearts Does a Worm Have?">A-Z Animals</a>)</p>
</li>
</ul><p>Popular science outlets continue to highlight animals with multiple pumping organs, underscoring that “more than one heart” is a real, well-documented <strong>non-human</strong> adaptation. (<a href="https://www.worldatlas.com/animals/animals-with-more-than-one-heart.html?utm_source=chatgpt.com" title="Animals With More Than One Heart">WorldAtlas</a>, <a href="https://timesofindia.indiatimes.com/etimes/trending/animals-with-multiple-hearts-natures-diverse-answers/articleshow/112481312.cms?utm_source=chatgpt.com" title="Animals with multiple hearts; Nature's diverse answers">The Times of India</a>)</p><hr/><h2>4) Theological Readings and Points of Tension</h2><ol>
<li>
<p><strong>Rhetorical/Idiomatic Reading (dominant in tafsīr):</strong><br/>
The verse negates the <strong>metaphor</strong> of dual hearts to insist upon undivided loyalty and to anchor legal reforms. On this reading, the statement is <strong>not</strong> a propositional claim about anatomy, and neither conjoined twins nor heterotopic transplants are relevant. (<a href="https://quranx.com/tafsirs/33.4?utm_source=chatgpt.com" title="Commentaries for 33.4 - Quran">QuranX</a>, <a href="https://surahquran.com/tafsir-english-aya-4-sora-33.html?utm_source=chatgpt.com" title="Surah Ahzab ayat 4 Tafsir Quran 33:4 - Ibn Kathir">Surah Quran</a>)</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Literal-Anatomical Reading (minor but possible):</strong><br/>
If taken as a universal claim about <strong>creation</strong>, then (a) natural human development indeed does not produce two separate hearts; yet (b) modern surgery can place <strong>two hearts</strong> in one chest with both beating—raising the question whether the verse intended to preclude such a state “for any human” <em>in principle</em>, or speaking of <strong>how God originally fashions humans</strong> (pre-intervention). (<a href="https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1894712/?utm_source=chatgpt.com" title="Two Donor Hearts Beat in One Chest - PMC">PMC</a>)</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Meta-Ethical Reading (allegiance):</strong><br/>
The “one heart” motif functions as a theological axiom: <em>God does not design humans to sustain ultimate dual loyalties.</em> This coheres with the immediate legal corrections (ẓihār, adoption naming) as acts that required <strong>cleaving</strong> false equivalences (wife ≠ mother; adopted son ≠ biological son) to restore moral clarity. (<a href="https://myislam.org/surah-ahzab/ayat-4/?utm_source=chatgpt.com" title="Surah Al-Ahzab Ayat 4 (33:4 Quran) With Tafsir">My Islam</a>)</p>
</li>
</ol><hr/><h2>5) A Structured Challenge: Questions for Debate</h2><h3>A. Theological Questions</h3><ol>

</ol><hr/><h2>6) Synthesis and Position</h2><ul>
<li>
<p><strong>Historically and exegetically</strong>, 33:4 functions as a <strong>rhetorical</strong> and <strong>ethical</strong> pivot: “two hearts” negates divided allegiance and undergirds reforms to marriage and adoption practices. This is well attested in classical tafsīr. (<a href="https://quranx.com/tafsirs/33.4?utm_source=chatgpt.com" title="Commentaries for 33.4 - Quran">QuranX</a>, <a href="https://surahquran.com/tafsir-english-aya-4-sora-33.html?utm_source=chatgpt.com" title="Surah Ahzab ayat 4 Tafsir Quran 33:4 - Ibn Kathir">Surah Quran</a>)</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Empirically</strong>, no naturally developed human is known to possess <strong>two fully independent hearts</strong>; however, <strong>medicine can—and does—produce a two-heart state</strong> in one chest via heterotopic transplantation, sometimes for decades. Thus, a <strong>strictly literal, exceptionless</strong> reading that outlaws the very possibility is difficult to maintain without narrowing the claim to <strong>natural formation</strong> only. (<a href="https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9871692/?utm_source=chatgpt.com" title="To hit a home run as a heterotopic heart recipient—living ...">PMC</a>, <a href="https://news.temple.edu/in-the-media/case-man-two-hearts?utm_source=chatgpt.com" title="The case of the man with two hearts | Temple Now">Temple Now</a>)</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Comparative biology</strong> demonstrates that <strong>multiple hearts are a real feature</strong> of God’s broader creation (octopus, hagfish, etc.), underscoring that the Qur’anic clause, if read as a universal biological principle, cannot extend beyond <strong>humans</strong> without qualification. (<a href="https://www.nhm.ac.uk/discover/octopuses-keep-surprising-us-here-are-eight-examples-how.html?utm_source=chatgpt.com" title="Octopuses keep surprising us - here are eight examples how">Natural History Museum</a>, <a href="https://www.thelancet.com/journals/lancet/article/PIIS0140-6736%2805%2960870-7/fulltext?utm_source=chatgpt.com" title="Multiple hearts in animals other than Barosaurus">The Lancet</a>)</p>
</li>
</ul><hr/><h2>7) Conclusion</h2><p>A careful, scholarly reading suggests Qur’an 33:4 is best understood as <strong>rhetorical theology</strong> in service of moral-legal reform, not as a blanket scientific assertion about anatomy. Nevertheless, because the verse uses <strong>anatomical language</strong>, it invites empirical scrutiny and, with modern cardiothoracic practice, yields <strong>borderline counterexamples</strong> (two hearts in one person post-transplant). Theologically, Muslim scholars can preserve the verse’s integrity by anchoring it in <strong>idiom and intention</strong> (undivided allegiance, rejection of false equivalences), while acknowledging that <strong>medical technology</strong> can generate physiological states that the verse was <strong>not</strong> addressing. From a debate standpoint, the key is <strong>clarifying scope and genre</strong>—and resisting the urge to stretch the text into a universal scientific maxim.</p><hr/><h3>Selected References</h3><ul>

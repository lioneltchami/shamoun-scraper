<h3>What Did the Qur’an Originally Say?</h3>
<p>Surah <em>An-Najm</em> (The Star), verses 19–20, state:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>“Have you considered al-Lat and al-‘Uzza,<br/>
and another, the third, Manat?”</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Before Islam, <em>Allah</em> was already known in Arabia as a god who had three daughters: <em>al-Lat, al-‘Uzza,</em> and <em>Manat.</em> (Note: “al-” in Arabic means “the.”)</p>
<p>Four early Muslim biographers wrote that these verses were originally followed by:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>“These are the exalted maidens (intercessors) whose prayers are to be hoped for.”</p>
</blockquote>
<p><strong>Translation:</strong> The daughters of Allah were believed to be heavenly beings who could intercede for others. “Exalted cranes” or “lofty maidens” was a poetic metaphor describing them. Alternate readings for “whose prayers are to be hoped for” (<em>turtaja</em>) include “whose intercession is accepted with approval” (<em>turtada</em>).<br/>
(Source: Alfred Guillaume’s translation of <em>The Life of Muhammad</em> by Ibn Ishaq, p.166.)</p>
<p>Later, this passage was <strong>removed</strong> and replaced with the current reading:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>“What! For you the male and for Him, the female?<br/>
Behold, such would be an unfair division!” (Qur’an 53:21–22)</p>
</blockquote>
<p><strong>Meaning:</strong> Those who believed that Allah had three daughters treated Him unjustly, since they preferred sons for themselves but assigned daughters to God.</p>
<p>These added lines are what came to be called <em>“The Satanic Verses.”</em> Centuries later, author Salman Rushdie used the phrase as the title of his novel, though his work was fictional and unrelated to these original verses.</p>
<p>The remainder of the text provides both <strong>direct</strong> and <strong>indirect</strong> evidence supporting the historical reality of the Satanic Verses — along with nine Muslim objections to the story and responses to each.</p>
